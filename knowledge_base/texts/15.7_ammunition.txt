CHAPTER 15. FIREARMS: HANDLING BASICS
15.7 Ammunition: cartridge types, compatibility, storage

CARTRIDGE PARTS

1. **Projectile** — bullet, shot payload, or slug.
2. **Case** — brass, steel, aluminum, or bi-metal.
3. **Propellant** — smokeless powder inside the case.
4. **Primer** — in case head; struck by firing pin to ignite powder.

Headstamp often shows **caliber**, factory, year.


CALIBER LABELS (EXAMPLES)

**Pistol**
— **9×18 mm** — common in Eastern Europe; **not** interchangeable with 9×19.
— **9×19 mm Parabellum** — NATO/police standard worldwide.
— **.45 ACP**, **.40 S&W** — common western/defense calibers.
— **7.62×25 mm** — older Tokarev class; still encountered in some regions.

**Critical:** **9×18 vs 9×19** — different case and pressure — **never cross-use.**

**Rifle**
— **7.62×39 mm** — very common intermediate.
— **5.45×39 mm** — AK-74 lineage.
— **7.62×54R** — rimmed; machine guns, DMRs, Mosin-type rifles where found.
— **5.56×45 mm NATO** — AR-15 family.
— **.308 Win / 7.62×51 mm NATO** — battle rifle / hunting.
— **.22 LR** — rimfire training/small game.

**Shotgun**
— **12 ga** most common; lengths **2¾″ (70 mm)** vs **3″ (76 mm) “magnum”** — **chamber length must match or exceed shell length.**
— **16**, **20**, **.410** — verify gun marking.


COMPATIBILITY — NON-NEGOTIABLE

**Wrong caliber can destroy the gun and kill the shooter.**

Rules:
— Match **exactly** what is stamped on **barrel** and/or **receiver.**
— Examples: **5.45×39** vs **7.62×39** — different bullets, **never** mix up.
— If a round **does not chamber easily by hand** with light pressure — **wrong round** or damaged chamber — **stop.**

**Too small** — deep seat, bad ignition, bore obstruction risk.
**Too large / forced** — catastrophic pressure.


BULLET TYPES (RIFLE/PISTOL)

**FMJ** — full metal jacket; penetrates well; common military ball.
**Soft-point** — exposed lead tip; more expansion for hunting (where legal).
**Hollow-point** — expands; common law-enforcement/defense (law varies).
**AP / steel core** — armor penetration; **heavily restricted** in many jurisdictions.
**Tracer** — visible trace; **fire hazard**; range restrictions.

**Shotshells**
— **Shot size** numbered — smaller number = larger shot (regional labeling differs — use local charts).
— **Buckshot** — large pellets for defense/large game where lawful.
— **Slugs** — single projectile for smoothbores (Foster/Brenneke types).


INSPECT AMMO BEFORE USE

**Reject** if:
— Dented / cracked case.
— **Green corrosion** on brass.
— Rust on steel case.
— Bullet **loose** or **seated too deep.**
— **Primer** pierced or badly marred (not from firing pin).
— **Submerged** ammo — moisture risk → **unsafe pressures / squibs.**

**Questionable** rounds: isolate; expect possible misfire.
**Water-damaged:** avoid for serious use.


STORAGE

**Enemies:** moisture, heat cycles (condensation), UV, impact on primers, acids.

**Good practice:**
— **Dry** (**&lt;~60% RH** if you can).
— Stable **coolish** temps; avoid trunk baking in sun.
— Dark container; original boxes or **sealed** ammo cans.
— **Desiccant** (silica) in sealed storage; **regenerate** silica per product instructions (often low oven for food-grade packs — follow label).

**Shelf life:** quality ammo in good conditions often **10–30+ years**; first sign of age is **more misfires**, then erratic velocity.

**Transport:**
— **Rigid** boxes; avoid loose rounds **rattling** in a pack where primers get **struck** by tools or keys.


WORKING WITH AMMO

— Long-term storage: many prefer **empty chamber**, mag separate.
— **Loaded magazines:** springs can take **set** over years — rotate mags, unload occasionally per doctrine.
— **Do not mix** bullet types in one mag (different POI and function).
— **Label** containers by caliber.
— **Rotate stock:** older lots shoot first.
