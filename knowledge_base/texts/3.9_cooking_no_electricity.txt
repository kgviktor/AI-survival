CHAPTER 3. FOOD
3.9 Cooking without electricity

MAIN HEAT SOURCES

Outdoor fire:
Most accessible. Controlled flame, heat from coals. For cooking you want coals, not tall flame — flame smokes and heats unevenly. Let fuel burn down to coals (~30–40 min), then cook.

Gas stove:
Camping stove + canister — ideal in crisis. ~220 g canister ≈ 1–1.5 h burn. Save fuel: cook in batches, use a lid (roughly halves boil time).

Alcohol stove:
Can with cloth wick + alcohol / denatured spirit. ~100 ml alcohol ≈ 30–40 min burn. Weak heat — mainly boiling water and warming.

Hexamine / solid fuel tablets:
1 tablet ≈ 10–12 min high heat. Compact, long shelf life. Smoke can contain formaldehyde — use outdoors or with ventilation.

Wood stove:
Most efficient for long-term indoor use. Retains heat, better efficiency than open fire.


IMPROVISED COOKING DEVICES

Rocket stove:
Simple L-shaped brick or metal channel. Air feeds from below (draft), fuel burns horizontally, heat rises vertically. Often 3–4× more efficient than open campfire. Thin sticks and kindling burn very well.

Brick build:
1. Horizontal channel (~5 bricks): fuel inlet.
2. Vertical stack: 4–6 bricks high.
3. Pot on top of vertical outlet.
Fuel: sticks ~3–5 cm thick, splinters.

Barrel stove:
~200 L metal drum with chimney stub — effective wood stove. Holds heat; cook on top.

Stone fire triangle:
Three large flat stones as triangle — pot on top. Simple universal setup at a fire.


COOKWARE FROM SCRAP

Metal cans:
Tin can 0.5–5 L — good pot. Clean, suspend over fire. Thin aluminum (e.g. drink cans) — boiling water only, thin metal.

Large food cans:
Big can with wire bail handle. Boil, simmer, shallow fry.

Flat stone griddle:
Flat stone heated in fire or on coals. Eggs, thin flatbread, fish — works well. Avoid water-saturated river stones — can explode when heated.

Clay pot (if you have time):
Shape from clay, dry days, fire in coals. Porous — not for water storage, but good for stewing/baking.

Earth oven:
Pit ~30×30×30 cm. Heat stones in fire 30–40 min. Move stones into pit. Wrap food in leaves or cloth, place on stones. Cover with leaves, then soil. Cooks 1–3 h. Whole bird ~2–3 h; roots ~1–1.5 h.


COOKING METHODS

Boiling:
Primary method. Kills parasites, softens food. Pot of water over heat.

Stewing:
Slow simmer with little water. Tough meat softens in ~1.5–2 h.

Baking in coals:
Tubers (potato, sweet potato, burdock root) directly in coals, no foil. Turn. Medium potato ~20–30 min. Done when pierced easily.

Grilling on sticks:
Meat or fish on sharpened stick over coals. Use hardwood (not resinous conifers — pitch). Aromatic wood (apple) adds flavor.

Frying on hot stone:
Heated flat stone = pan. Fat or water as cooking liquid.

Boiling without a pot (stone boil):
Pit lined with stones or clay. Fill with water, add hot stones from fire — water boils. Used traditionally in many regions.

Smoking:
Cooks and preserves — especially fish.


FUEL ECONOMY

Lid:
Covered pot boils ~2× faster. Simple but powerful.

Soaking:
Beans, peas, lentils — soak 8–12 h. Cuts cook time 2–3×. Major fuel savings.

“Haybox” / retained heat:
Bring to boil ~5 min. Wrap pot tightly in blankets, clothing. Leave 1–2 h — finishes without fire. Rice, buckwheat, porridge, soup work well.

Right fire size:
You need concentrated heat under the pot, not a huge bonfire.

Batch cooking:
One large boil saves more fuel than many small ones.


BASIC RECIPES FROM MINIMAL INGREDIENTS

Any grain porridge:
1 part grain + 2–3 parts water. Boil, salt, simmer 10–20 min (buckwheat ~15 min, rice ~20, oats ~5). Retained-heat: boil 3–5 min, wrap 30–40 min.

Unleavened flatbread:
Flour + water + salt + fat (optional). Knead, roll thin. Fry on hot stone or dry pan ~2–3 min per side. Staple bread of many cultures.

Wild greens soup:
Water + what you found (nettle, dandelion, lamb’s quarters, rowan bark in pinch). Salt. Little grain if you have it. Simmer 10–15 min. Soup extracts more from plants than raw salad.

Bone broth:
Bones (game, bird, fish) + water. Simmer 1–2 h. Salt. Fats and minerals in broth. Gelatin from bones thickens. Calories from “scraps.”

Acorn porridge:
Soak acorns 1–3 days (change water). Grind or chop. Cook as porridge. Bitter but filling.


COOKING INDOORS

Ventilation is mandatory:
— Open window or vent
— Smallest fire that works
— Never sleep with smoldering fire indoors without airflow
— Carbon monoxide is odorless and colorless — kills quietly

Safer indoor options:
— Gas stove near open window
— Alcohol stove — less CO than open wood fire in a closed room
— Candle for minimal warming only — fire and CO risk still apply
