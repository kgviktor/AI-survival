CHAPTER 3. FOOD
3.8 Preservation: drying, salting, smoking

WHY PRESERVE

You got a lot of food at once — you cannot eat it all before it spoils. Preservation turns perishables into long-term stores. All methods address the same goal: remove conditions for bacteria and mold (moisture, warmth, oxygen, nutrients).


DRYING (JERKY / DEHYDRATION)

Principle: remove water. Without water, bacteria and mold do not grow. Oldest and simplest method.

Drying meat and fish:
1. Cut thin strips (3–5 mm) along the grain.
2. Salt each layer (salt pulls moisture and inhibits microbes). About 1 tbsp salt per 500 g meat.
3. Hang on cord or rack — pieces must not touch.
4. Sun and wind: 2–3 days at ~+30°C, up to 7 days in cool weather.
5. Done when: bends but does not break, not sticky, no wet spots inside when snapped.

Fly protection:
Flies lay eggs on raw meat — larvae in ~24 h. Cover with gauze or cloth. Smoke nearby repels flies. Dry a surface crust quickly — 2–3 h in strong sun.

Oven or over-fire drying:
— Temperature: 60–70°C, oven door ajar.
— Over fire: ~40–60 cm above flames.
— Time: 6–12 h at low heat.

Storage:
— Dried meat in dry place: 1–2 months; vacuum-packed: up to ~1 year.
— Dried fish: 1–3 months.

Drying plants:
— Berries, mushrooms, herbs — string or thin layer in shade with airflow.
— Direct sun destroys vitamins — prefer partial shade.
— Mushrooms: slices 5–7 mm, dry at 40–60°C several hours.
— Berries: 60–70°C, 6–12 h.
— Herbs: room temp, 1–2 weeks.

Storing dried goods:
Dry sealed containers. Moisture is the enemy. Glass jars ideal. Plastic bags worse (condensation). Add desiccant (dry rice, salt in cloth bag).


SALTING

Principle: salt creates osmotic pressure — pulls water from bacterial cells and from the product.

Dry salt cure (meat/fish):
1. Rub pieces with salt on all sides — be generous.
2. Layer in a container, salting between layers.
3. Weight on top (press).
4. After 1–3 days brine forms — normal.
5. Keep cool.

Fish salt ratios:
— Light salt: 1 part salt to 6–7 parts fish. Ready in 24–48 h. Keeps ~3–5 days.
— Medium: 1:5. Keeps ~1–2 weeks.
— Heavy: 1:3. Keeps ~1–3 months.

Meat:
— Medium cure: ~3% salt = 30 g per 1 kg.
— Heavy: 5–7% = 50–70 g per 1 kg.
— Pieces not thicker than 2–3 cm so salt penetrates.

Wet brine:
~300–350 g salt per liter water (strong solution). Submerge product, keep submerged. Fish: 1–3 days. Meat: 5–10 days.

Storage:
At room temp: lightly salted — up to ~1 week; heavily salted — 1–3 months. Cold extends all.


SMOKING

Principle: smoke carries phenols and tars that kill surface bacteria and form a protective rind. Combines heat and chemical preservation.

Cold smoke (15–30°C):
— Product not fully cooked by heat — should be pre-salted.
— Duration: 12–72 h continuous smoke.
— Storage: 2–4 weeks if cool.
— Flavor: milder, “refined.”

Hot smoke (60–120°C):
— Cooks while smoking.
— Duration: 1–6 h.
— Storage: ~3–7 days.
— Flavor: strong, juicier meat.

Improvised smokers:
Option 1 (pit): pit ~40×40×40 cm, fire pit ~1.5 m away, trench connects. Smolder in trench, smoke flows to food pit. Cover with branches and leaves — slows smoke escape.

Option 2 (barrel/box): metal barrel with lid. Bottom: sawdust or chips. Rack above. Heat from below. Smoke accumulates inside.

Option 3 (over coals): tripod over coals. Food wrapped in non-resinous twigs or hung. Cover with cloth — holds smoke.

Wood for smoke:
Good: alder, apple, cherry, plum, oak, ash. Avoid: pine, spruce, birch with bark — excess tar, bitter/harsh. Damp chips = more smoke; dry = more heat.


FERMENTATION AND PICKLING

Principle: lactic acid bacteria acidify the food, suppressing other bacteria.

Sauerkraut:
1. Shred cabbage.
2. ~1 tbsp salt per 1 kg cabbage.
3. Massage until juice releases.
4. Pack tight — liquid must cover.
5. Cover loosely (gas must escape).
6. Room temp: 3–7 days.
7. Ready when sour.

Storage: months if cool (0–+4°C). Excellent vitamin C source after fermentation — historically used at sea against scurvy.

Berries in water:
Cranberry, lingonberry — high acid. Cover with cold water, pinch sugar and salt. Keeps months.


CONFIT AND FAT STORAGE

Animal fat as preservative:
Cooked meat covered with melted fat — seals surface from oxygen and bacteria. Keeps ~2–4 weeks cool, longer if frozen.

Rendered fat:
Slowly render fat from trimmings on low heat. Strain into clean jar. Keeps ~2–6 months.


STORAGE IN EARTH

Root vegetables (potato, carrot, beet):
Dig pit 60–90 cm deep (below frost line where applicable). Layer straw or leaves. Store vegetables, cover straw, fill with soil.

~0–+4°C can hold for months in winter in many climates.

Fermented goods:
Same pit idea. Buried crocks — classic cellar method in cold climates.


GENERAL RULES

1. Cleanliness — contamination at start ruins preservation.
2. Salt helps almost all methods, including drying and smoking.
3. Cold extends shelf life of everything.
4. Inspect stores — mold, off smell.
5. Label — date on every container.
