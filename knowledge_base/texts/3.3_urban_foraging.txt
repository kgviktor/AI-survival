CHAPTER 3. FOOD
3.3 Urban foraging: plants, insects, birds

URBAN FORAGING IS A REAL RESOURCE

Cities hold more wild food than people think. Parks, vacant lots, roadsides, yards — edible plants grow everywhere. In a long crisis this meaningfully supplements diet. Critical rule: know what you collect. Some urban plants are toxic.


COMMON EDIBLE URBAN PLANTS

Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale):
Among the most nutritious urban edibles. Whole plant: leaves (salad, soup), flowers (fry, boil, tea), root (roasted coffee substitute). Leaves best in spring — bitter in summer. Soak in cold water ~30 min to reduce bitterness. Rich in vitamins A, C, K and calcium.

Stinging nettle (Urtica dioica):
Excellent protein (~3–4 g/100 g), iron, vitamins. Young tops (~10–15 cm) in early spring — tenderest. Boil 1–2 min or blanch to remove sting. Soup, “spinach,” juice. Wear gloves when picking.

Plantain (Plantago major / lanceolata):
Leaves raw or cooked. Coarse — better in soup or boiled. Vitamin C and fiber. Grows on paths and lawns.

Chickweed (Stellaria media):
Low creeping plant in gardens and shade. Tender leaves and stems — good raw in salad. Mild flavor.

Goosefoot / lamb’s quarters (Chenopodium album):
Triangular leaves; cook like spinach or eat young leaves raw. Seeds protein-rich — cook as porridge. Silver bloom on leaves washes off. Historic famine food.

Shepherd’s purse (Capsella bursa-pastoris):
Small rosette leaves in early spring — salad. Triangular seed pods — peppery taste. Vitamin C.

Sorrel (Rumex acetosa):
Sour leaves — soup, salad, stewed. Avoid large amounts with kidney issues (oxalic acid). Easy to recognize.

Wood sorrel (Oxalis):
Three-part clover-like leaves; pleasant sour taste. Small amounts raw, in salads.

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla):
Leaves and flowers for tea; mild anti-inflammatory.

Fireweed / rosebay willowherb (Chamerion angustifolium):
Leaves for tea; young spring shoots boiled like asparagus. Pollen is nutritious.


PLANTS YOU MUST NOT CONFUSE

Poison hemlock (Conium maculatum):
Looks like parsley/carrot tops. Mouse-like smell when crushed. Red-purple blotched stem. Deadly. Rule: do not eat white umbel plants (carrot family) unless 100% sure.

Water hemlock (Cicuta spp.):
Grows in wet places; looks like celery. Chambered root with yellow sap — sweet bait. Deadly. Never eat unknown roots from wet margins.

Lily of the valley, deadly nightshade, henbane, jimsonweed:
Occur in city parks. Lily: white bells, red-orange berries. Nightshade: black shiny berries. Henbane / datura: large showy flowers. None are food.


INSECTS

Why they matter:
High protein (40–70% dry weight), fats, B12. When animal protein is scarce — valuable.

Generally safer choices:
— Beetle grubs (e.g., May/June beetle larvae): under trees; white C-shaped; cook.
— Caterpillars without bright warning colors: cook.
— Grasshoppers/locusts: remove head and legs; fry.
— Earthworms: rinse in clean water ~30 min; boil or fry.
— Ants: acidic taste; fried — milder.

Safety rules:
Always cook — kills parasites. Avoid bright “warning” coloration. Do not eat dead insects of unknown cause. Avoid strong biters/stinkers — may contain toxins.

Finding in cities:
Under rocks and logs — beetles, worms. Rotten stumps — larvae. Soil at tree bases — grubs. Lights at night — assorted insects.


BIRDS

Pigeons and sparrows:
Pigeons are the most accessible urban game — ~150 kcal/100 g, good protein. Sparrows are small — several for a meal.

Catching:
— Drop trap: box propped on stick with cord; bait with bread/grain; pull cord when bird is inside.
— Snare: fine wire or cord on a perch they use.
— Slingshot: with practice.

Dressing:
1. Pluck or skin (faster with skin on).
2. Open belly; remove guts (gut tube, gall bladder).
3. Keep heart, liver, gizzard (clean gizzard) — edible.
4. Boil hard: 30+ minutes.

Urban birds eat garbage — higher parasite risk; long boil mandatory.

Ducks and waterfowl:
In parks near water; more meat than pigeons; same handling rules.


RODENTS

Undesirable but possible in extremis:
Rats and mice carry diseases (leptospirosis, plague, HPS in some regions). Hard boil 30+ min. Do not eat rats that look sick or behave oddly.

Rabbits/hares near suburbs:
Safer than commensal rodents; cook thoroughly.


SEASONALITY IN THE CITY

Spring (April–May):
Best season — nettle, dandelion, chickweed, shepherd’s purse.

Summer:
Goosefoot, sorrel, berries (rosehip, sloe, rowan — often need processing). Insects peak.

Fall:
Rose hips (vitamin C), rowan, sloe. Acorns (bitter — leach, but caloric). Burdock and dandelion roots.

Winter:
Minimal. Pine/spruce needles — vitamin C tea. Inner birch bark — last resort.


COLLECTION SAFETY

Avoid:
— Road edges (heavy metals, exhaust)
— Industrial sites
— Herbicide/pesticide strips (yellowed, stunted growth)
— Fuel-spill areas

Wash collected food in clean water before eating. Cooking reduces many surface contaminants.
