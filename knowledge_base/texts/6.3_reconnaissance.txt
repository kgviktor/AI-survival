CHAPTER 6. SECURITY
6.3 Reconnaissance and observation without being seen

STEALTH OBSERVATION BASICS

Observation gathers information without contact. The more you know before you act, the safer your decisions.

Golden rule:
“Watch longer than feels necessary.” First ~10 minutes give ~half the picture; the next hour adds much of the rest. Most mistakes come from rushing conclusions.

“Stop-look-listen” before every exit:
— Freeze ~3–5 min at the threshold.
— Listen for normal vs alarm sounds.
— Then move.


CAMOUFLAGE AND MOVEMENT

Clothing:
— No bright colors or shine.
— Gray, dark blue, brown, olive drab.
— Nothing that jingles or rustles (metal in pockets, noisy synthetics).
— Tape/cloth on shiny gear.

Movement:
— Slow across open ground; fast through it.
— Move cover to cover (tree — building corner — fence).
— Never silhouette on sky or light wall.
— Hug building shadows, not mid-sidewalk.
— Cross intersections quickly after a careful peek.

Observation position:
— Do not peer around a corner at standing head height — predictable.
— Look from low ground or over high cover — unexpected angles.
— Lying: only eyes and forehead above cover line.
— Hold still while watching — motion is seen before shape.

Night:
— Slower than day.
— Step toe-first, roll to heel — quieter steps.
— Every ~10–15 steps: stop, listen ~30 s.
— Light only if essential; cup lens with hand.


OBSERVATION TECHNIQUES

Static observation post (OP):

Site selection:
— ~180°+ field of view.
— Protected from rear approach.
— Masked: brush, shadow, doorway gap.
— Escape route.

Sectors:
— Divide view into zones (left, center, right, near, far).
— Scan each every ~5–10 min.
— Slow sweep beats staring at one spot.

Logging:
— Time — what — where (bearing, distance) — how many — armed? — direction of movement.
— Brief notes if safe.

Group rotation:
— ~2 h max continuous watch — attention drops.
— Quiet handover: “Sector clear / movement north — three — ~200 m — westbound.”

Night vision:
— Do not stare directly at dim objects — use ~10–15° off-center (rods).
— Shapes and motion, not detail.
— Hearing dominates: voices, footsteps, engines, dogs.


SIGNS OF HUMAN PRESENCE

People reveal themselves by:
— Fire and smoke (especially night).
— Smell: food, smoke, sweat, urine — noticeable tens of meters in still air.
— Sound: voices, cough, metal clink, branch snap.
— Motion and glint (metal, glass, synthetics in sun).
— Tracks: trampled grass, mud prints, trash, feces.
— Dogs barking — often human activity nearby.

Your shelter reveals itself by:
— Light leaks at night — control curtains and cracks.
— Chimney/window smoke.
— Noise: talk, children, tools.
— Cooking smells.
— Heavy traffic at one entrance.
— Antennas or roof gear.

Reduce signature:
— Cook low in building to disperse smoke.
— Sound discipline: whisper, soft soles, pad metal.
— Light discipline: blackout curtains; no visible glow.
— Brush away tracks at entry.


ROUTE RECON BEFORE MOVEMENT

Entering unknown area:
1. High observation ~30–60 min (roof, upper floor).
2. Judge activity: who is there, how do they behave.
3. Pick route with most cover, fewest open kills zones.
4. Mark danger points (intersections, open squares, overwatch buildings).
5. Plan alternate withdrawal route.

Recon by one scout (last resort):
— One person forward, rest in cover.
— Voice or signal distance.
— On alarm scout breaks contact; group covers or pulls back.

Safer route signs:
— Long quiet period.
— No fresh tracks.
— Normal animal behavior (birds singing, dogs not on edge).

Dangerous route signs:
— Silence where noise expected (birds stopped).
— Smoke, odd smells.
— Fresh tracks, dumped trash.
— Feeling watched — intuition often processes cues faster than conscious thought.
