CHAPTER 11. TOOLS AND REPAIR
11.5 Survival chemistry: soap, candles, simple chemicals

WHY BASIC CHEMISTRY

Long crises exhaust soap, candles, disinfectants, glues, coatings. Knowing simple processes lets you replace some from feedstock.

**Safety:** caustics and acids — gloves, eye protection, ventilation; **always add lye to water, never water to lye** (splash risk).


SOAP (SAPONIFICATION)

Principle: fat + strong base → soap + glycerol.

Solid bar: **NaOH** (sodium hydroxide) + fat.
Soft/liquid tendency: **KOH** (potassium hydroxide) — different chemistry, different product.

Fats:
— Lard, tallow, any cooking oil.

NaOH:
— Sold as drain cleaner in many places — **pure crystals only**, no additives — read label; laws vary.

Example cold-process **starting point** (verify with saponification calculator for your exact fat):
— ~500 g rendered pork fat.
— ~70 g NaOH (example only — **calculate** for your fat).
— ~150–170 ml water.

**Excess NaOH = corrosive soap** — use a **lye calculator** online or print tables now.

Saponification factors (approximate grams NaOH per 1 g fat):
— Lard ~0.141
— Sunflower oil ~0.134
— Coconut oil ~0.190

Process:
1. **Slowly** dissolve NaOH in water; it heats — wait until ~40–45 °C.
2. Melt fat to ~40–45 °C.
3. Stream lye into fat while stirring.
4. Stir to **trace** — pudding texture, drizzle leaves mark.
5. Pour into lined mold; cover; insulate ~24 h.
6. Cut; **cure 4–6 weeks** — uncured soap is caustic.

Ash-lye soap (soft, variable):
— Hardwood ash leachate (potassium carbonate solution) boiled long with fat — primitive soft soap.
— **Egg float test** in strong lye — horizontal float sometimes used as crude concentration check — still inconsistent; expect weak or harsh batches.


CANDLES

Wax:
— Stump candles, paraffin blocks, blended with stearic for hardness.

Wick:
— Braid ~16–18 strands cotton thread; pre-wax straight wick.

Method:
1. Double-boiler melt wax — **not** direct flame (fire risk).
2. Dip or mold around centered wick (pencil across top).
3. Pour ~80 °C into can / PVC / cup mold.
4. Cool slowly; demold.

~20–30% stearin in paraffin: harder, whiter, less soot.

Tallow candles:
— Animal fat alone is soft; add ~10–20% beeswax if available.


BIRCH TAR

Dry distillation of birch bark in closed tin with small drip hole → tar.

Uses:
— Leather waterproofing (test stain).
— Traditional wood preservative and insect repellent.
— **Medical claims unproven** — avoid deep wounds; vapors irritating.


VINEGAR

Alcohol + acetobacter + oxygen → acetic acid.

Rough path:
1. Fruit juice → ferment to wine (yeast, ~1–2 weeks).
2. Open wide vessel, cloth cover, warm ~24–28 °C, ~4–8 weeks → ~4–6% vinegar for pickling/cleaning.


DISINFECTANTS

Bleach (5% sodium hypochlorite):
— ~10 ml per liter water — surface wipe (fresh mix).
— **Emergency water:** ~2 drops 5% bleach per liter, mix, wait ~30 min — only when no better option; turbid water needs pre-filter.

Alcohol:
— **~70% v/v** best for skin antiseptic; dilute stronger ethanol with clean water.

Drugstore hydrogen peroxide:
— Stock sparingly; hard to DIY at useful concentration.

Walnut hull tea:
— Unripe walnut / green hull soak — weak traditional antiseptic; not a substitute for modern care when available.


GLUES AND RESINS

Hide glue:
— Long boil bones/skin → reduce to thick gel — hot apply, water-sensitive.

Pine pitch glue:
— Melt resin; mix ~10–20% wood ash; optional ~5–10% tallow — flexible pitch for hafting, seam fill.

Starch paste:
— ~2 Tbsp starch in ~250 ml water; heat stir to clear — paper/cloth temporary bond.


POTASH FROM ASH

Boil down strong lye from hardwood ash → crude potassium carbonate — soap making, garden K, historical glassmaking (not a home “glass factory” skill).


SIMPLE TANNING (OVERVIEW)

Oak-bark tan (long soak):
— Flesh hide; soak; de-hair in lime or wood-ash soak (caustic — careful); rinse; months in strong tannin bark liquor; oil and work leather.

Brain / “Indian” soft tan:
— Emulsified brain worked into damp scraped hide; stretch-dry; smoke optional — soft buckskin-like result.

**Zoonosis and chemical hazards** — use gloves; learn full procedure before relying on it.
