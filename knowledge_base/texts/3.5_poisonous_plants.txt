CHAPTER 3. FOOD
3.5 Poisonous plants — recognition and first aid

INTRODUCTION

Poisonous plants kill more people in survival than animals. Reason: plants look harmless; many are tasty; symptoms are delayed; no antidote for many toxins. This chapter is not a full botanical atlas — it is a survival guide: what kills fastest, how to recognize, what to do.

GOLDEN RULES

1. If not 100% sure — do not eat.
2. “Almost like” a known edible — still no.
3. One berry “to try” can kill.
4. Children and pets are more vulnerable — smaller dose.
5. Cooking does not neutralize all toxins (e.g., some alkaloids survive boiling).


HOW PLANT POISONING WORKS

Routes:
— Ingestion (main).
— Skin contact (some cause dermatitis).
— Smoke from burning toxic plants (e.g., poison ivy/oak family — severe respiratory damage).

Speed:
— Minutes to hours: irritants, some neurotoxins.
— Hours to days: many alkaloids, liver toxins.
— Delayed liver/kidney failure: some mushrooms (not “plants” but often grouped with foraging risk).


CATEGORIES OF PLANT TOXINS

Alkaloids — bitter, often toxic in small amounts. Examples: nicotine-like, tropane (hallucination + organ failure), pyrrolizidine (liver).

Glycosides — release cyanide or cardiac glycosides when chewed/digested.

Oxalates — burning mouth, kidney damage if large dose.

Photosensitizers — skin blisters in sun after contact/eating.

Lectins — GI damage; often reduced by proper cooking (e.g., kidney beans must be boiled).


DEADLY AND DANGEROUS PLANTS (TEMPERATE / WIDESPREAD PATTERNS)

Lily of the valley (Convallaria majalis)
— White bell flowers, two leaves, sweet scent. ALL parts toxic — cardiac glycosides.
— Confused with: wild garlic (Allium — garlic smell! Lily of the valley has NO garlic smell).
Symptoms: nausea, arrhythmia, can be fatal.
Aid: induce vomiting only if conscious and recent; emergency care; activated charcoal if available and instructed.

Hemlock (Conium maculatum)
— Umbel like wild carrot; purple-spotted hollow stems; musty smell. Neurotoxin — paralysis, respiratory failure.
— Confused with: wild carrot, parsley (careful!).
Aid: emergency — respiratory support; no home antidote.

Foxglove (Digitalis)
— Tall spike of tubular purple/pink flowers. Cardiac glycosides — tiny amount can stop the heart.
Aid: emergency; do not induce vomiting if unstable; professional care only.

Oleander, yew (common ornamental/trees in many regions)
— Oleander: all parts; yew: seeds/arils — highly toxic cardiac/neuro effects.
Aid: emergency immediately.

Castor bean (Ricinus)
— Seeds contain ricin — extreme toxicity if chewed.
Aid: emergency; supportive care.

Poison ivy / oak / sumac (Toxicodendron)
— Urushiol — severe dermatitis; smoke from burning = medical emergency.
Aid: wash skin with soap and lots of water ASAP; do not burn plants.

Nightshades (Solanaceae)
— Deadly nightshade (Atropa), jimsonweed (Datura), black nightshade (some species/regions unripe berries risky) — tropane alkaloids: delirium, death.
— Potato green parts and sprouted green potatoes — solanine.
Aid: emergency; calm, darkness, reduce stimulation; professional care.

Water hemlock (Cicuta, North America) / related umbellifers
— Often called most toxic plant in NA; seizures minutes after ingestion.
Aid: emergency — seizures are life-threatening.

Monkshood / wolfsbane (Aconitum)
— Beautiful hooded flowers; roots especially toxic — numbness, arrhythmia, death.
Aid: emergency; supportive.

Autumn crocus (Colchicum) — not a true crocus
— Severe multi-organ toxicity; delayed.
Aid: emergency.

Cherry / apricot / peach pits and apple seeds (large amounts)
— Amygdalin → cyanide if crushed/chewed in quantity.
Aid: fresh air; emergency; possible antidotes in hospital.

Rhododendron / azalea (some species)
— Mad honey / grayanotoxins in some regions; honey from those flowers — poisoning.
Aid: emergency if severe bradycardia.

LOCAL KNOWLEDGE
— In your region, learn the top 10 toxic plants from a local poison center or extension guide. Names and species vary by continent.


FIRST AID FOR PLANT POISONING

If conscious and ingestion just happened (minutes):
1. Identify plant if possible (photo, sample for medics — use gloves).
2. Call emergency / poison control.
3. If instructed: rinse mouth; small sips water if swelling not worsening.
4. Activated charcoal only if advised by professional — not for all toxins.

Do NOT:
— Drink milk “to bind poison” (unreliable; may delay care).
— Random “antidotes” from folklore.
— Induce vomiting if drowsy, seizing, or corrosive burn suspected.

Skin contact:
— Wash 15+ minutes with soap and water; wash tools and under nails.
— Launder clothes separately.

Eye:
— Flush 15–20 min with clean water; emergency care.

Delayed symptoms (hours, liver pain, dark urine, jaundice):
— Seek care immediately — some toxins need hospital monitoring days.


PREVENTION IN SURVIVAL

1. Eat only plants you identified with a reliable regional guide + all key features (leaf, flower, stem, habitat, season).
2. Teach children: “pretty berries” rule — none without adult ID.
3. Do not experiment on starving days — judgment fails.
4. Separate foraging bags — toxic samples for ID never mix with food.
5. When in doubt, use the Universal Edibility Test only as last resort and never for mushrooms or known deadly families — many guides discourage it; zero doubt policy is safer.
