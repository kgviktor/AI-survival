CHAPTER 14. VEHICLE: SERVICE AND REPAIR
14.10 Fuel: range math, biodiesel, alcohol as fuel

FUEL RANGE MATH

Basic:
— Your consumption (L/100 km or MPG): add **20–30%** for load, off-road, bad roads.
— Liters needed = distance × (L/100 km) / 100.

Example:
— 10 L/100 km baseline.
— Trip 300 km.
— With margin: 300 × 12 / 100 = **36 L**. Add **20%** ≈ **43 L**.

Factors that raise use:
— Full load: **+10–20%**.
— Off-road: **+50–100%**.
— Mountain roads: **+20–40%**.
— Winter warm-up: **+15–25%**.
— High speed (e.g. &gt;120 km/h sustained): **+20–30%**.

**Jerrycan reserve:**
— Keep **20–40 L** extra when traveling in uncertainty.
— Long routes: plan **500+ km** margin beyond nominal distance when feasible.

**Gasoline storage:**
— Metal cans often permeate less than thin plastic.
— Stabilizer (e.g. Sta-Bil): slows oxidation.
  — With stabilizer: often **12–24 months** usable if kept cool/dark.
  — Without: **3–6 months** typical before noticeable degradation (varies by fuel).
— Cool, shaded, away from sparks/heat.

**Diesel storage:**
— Generally more stable than gasoline.
— **Summer diesel in winter** can wax/gel — use **winter-grade** or anti-gel additive for cold storage.
— **Biocide** for long-stored diesel — microbial growth in water phase.

**Water in tank:**
— Partial tanks condense moisture.
— Keep tank **full** when storing.
— Fuel dryer additives (isopropanol types) for trace water issues — follow label.


BASE FUEL FOR GENERATORS

If a **~2 kW** generator runs ~**1 kW** load: often **~0.5–0.7 L/h** (model-dependent).

— 10 h/day ≈ **5–7 L/day**.
— Month ≈ **150–210 L**.

**Bulk storage:**
— IBC totes (~1000 L) on pallets — industrial supply.
— **200 L** drums common.
— **20–30 L** cans for rotation and transport.

**Crisis refuel ideas:**
— Hand-crank pumps where legal/safe.
— Underground station tanks may hold fuel after grid loss — **authorized access only** in normal law.
— Manual pump/siphon from another vehicle tank — **fire/explosion/legal** risks — only where lawful and safe; never sparks, never inhale vapors.


ALTERNATIVE FUELS

BIODIESEL

Principle:
— Vegetable oil + methanol + catalyst (alkali) → biodiesel + glycerin layer.
— Reaction: **transesterification**.
— Runs in diesel engines; often blended with pump diesel.

Feedstock:
— Any vegetable oil: sunflower, rapeseed, soy.
— Used fryer oil: cheap but must be **filtered/dewatered**.
— Animal fat: works; worse cold-flow.

Components:
— **Methanol:** ~**20%** of oil volume by common small-batch recipes. **Toxic, flammable** — outdoor work, no ignition sources.
— Catalyst: **NaOH** or **KOH**.
  — Rule-of-thumb **~3.5–7 g KOH per liter** fresh oil (titrate for used oil in serious practice).

Simple lab-scale batch (**1 L** — illustrative; scale chemistry carefully):

1. Check for water: heat a sample — bubbling suggests water; dry oil first.
2. Heat oil to **~55 °C** (don’t overheat).
3. Dissolve **~3.5 g KOH** in **~200 mL methanol** (**exothermic** — add slowly, ventilation).
4. Slowly add methoxide to oil with mixing.
5. Mix **30–60 min**.
6. Settle **8–12 h**.
7. Bottom: dark glycerin; top: lighter biodiesel.
8. Drain glycerin.
9. **Wash** biodiesel with water (multiple gentle washes) to remove residual catalyst.
10. **Dry:** gentle heat to drive off water.

**Use:** neat **B100** often OK above **~+10 °C**; in frost blend with diesel (**B20–B50**). Neat biodiesel can gel in cold.

**Safety:**
— Methanol: volatile, flammable, **toxic** (vapor hazard) — **outdoors**, PPE.
— Caustic: **gloves + eye protection** mandatory.

**Home scale:** **20–50 L** batches are sometimes done in garages; larger needs proper reactor/agitation.

DIRECT VEGETABLE OIL (SVO)

— Some **old** diesels (early VW diesels, tractors) tolerate neat oil with modifications.
— **Modern common-rail:** generally **not** recommended without professional conversion — injector/coking issues.
— **Blend** up to **~20%** oil in diesel: sometimes tolerated short-term in older mechanical pumps — **risk remains**.
— **Fuel heating** before injection reduces viscosity.


ETHANOL AS FUEL

— High octane; **less energy per liter** than gasoline → higher consumption on same tune.
— Blends **E5–E85** exist globally.
— **E10:** widely compatible with modern gasoline cars (check manufacturer for classics).
— **E85:** **flex-fuel** vehicles only.

Issues in engine:
— Can swell/degrade some rubbers; check compatibility.
— **Water absorption** — phase separation in storage.
— Can increase corrosion in wrong materials.

**Moonshine / high-proof ethanol** as emergency extender:
— **70–80%** ABV sometimes discussed as **E20–E30-equivalent** blends in folklore — tuning/richness issues; **illegal** many places; **fire** risk.
— **Legal/industrial ethanol** for fuel use is regulated — this section is **crisis theory only**, not recipe for tax evasion.

**Methanol as fuel:**
— Racing/historic use; **very toxic**; aggressive to metals/seals — not a gasoline substitute without engineered systems.


LPG / CNG

**LPG (propane/butane):** dual-fuel kits common in some regions; cylinder stockpile possible.

**CNG:** high-pressure tanks; infrastructure dependent.

**Wood gas** (historic gasifiers): wood → combustible gas for engines — slow start, heavy adaptation — historical interest and niche builds.
