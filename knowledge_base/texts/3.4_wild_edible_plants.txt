CHAPTER 3. FOOD
3.4 Wild edible plants in forest and field

GENERAL PRINCIPLES

Forest and field are a large food resource — if you know them. Without knowledge — it is a minefield. Golden rule: better hungry than eating an unknown plant. Most fatal poisonings come from “almost recognizing” a look-alike.

“Three S” method:
See (positive ID), Sure (no toxic look-alike), Skill (you know how to prepare). All three — eat. Any doubt — no.


FOREST EDIBLES

Birch (Betula):
— Spring sap (before leaf-out): tasty, sugary; ~1–5 L/tree/day.
— Young light-green leaves: tea, infusion.
— Catkins: dry, grind to flour.
— Inner bark (phloem): extreme need — boil, dry, grind; nutritious, poor taste.

Pine and spruce (Pinus, Picea):
— Needles: vitamin C tea. Use fresh green needles; steep 10–15 min; do not boil hard — destroys C.
— Spring shoot tips: chew, sour taste.
— Cone seeds (e.g., pine nuts where species allow): calorie-dense.
— Spring pollen: mix with water — nutritious.

Oak (Quercus):
— Acorns: starchy but tannic. Soak 1–3 days (change water 2–3×/day) or boil with changes — removes bitterness. Dried ground acorns = flour. Bread, porridge.

Rowan (Sorbus aucuparia):
— Berries sweeter after frost; bitter raw — cooking/frost helps. Vitamin C; jam, decoction, dried.

Blueberry, bilberry, cranberry, lingonberry:
All safe and nutritious. Learn local species by guide.

Rose hip (Rosa spp.):
After frost. Very high vitamin C. Tea from hips — remove seeds and hairs (irritate gut).

Raspberry, blackberry:
Recognizable. Leaves — mild tea. Berries — food and vitamins.

Burdock (Arctium):
— First-year roots (non-flowering plants): boil or bake; potato-like; best late season.
— Young leaves and petioles in spring: boil.
— Young stems before flowering: peel; raw or boiled.

Cattail (Typha):
Near water; brown “sausage” flower spikes.
— Rhizomes: starchy. Dig, clean, squeeze juice; dry residue = flour.
— Spring shoots (white underwater): raw or boiled; cucumber-like.
— Summer yellow pollen: mixed with water — flour stretcher.
— Immature spikes: boil — corn-like.

Barbarea, field mustard (Barbarea, Sinapis):
Young spring leaves in salad or soup; mustardy bite. Common in fields and roadsides.

Wild garlic / ramsons (Allium ursinum):
Early spring in deciduous woods; flat garlic-scented leaves. Smell is the key ID — no garlic smell, do not eat (e.g., lily of the valley). Leaves and bulbs — raw or cooked.

Wood sorrel (Oxalis):
Shady woods; clover-like triple leaves; sour — small amounts raw.

Ground elder (Aegopodium podagraria):
Compound leaves; young spring leaves in soup/salad; mature plant is bitter.


FIELD AND MEADOW

Clover (Trifolium):
Flowers and leaves edible. Flowers in tea; young leaves cooked. Dried flowers/seeds — flour. May cause gas if overeaten — moderation.

Yarrow (Achillea millefolium):
Bitter leaves — seasoning/tea; anti-inflammatory.

Fireweed (as above):
Young shoots like asparagus; fermented leaf tea (“ivan chai”).

Wild grasses:
Grass seeds — grain. Collect late summer/fall, thresh, cook as porridge. Labor-intensive but caloric.

Lamb’s quarters (Chenopodium album):
Widespread on waste ground. Leaves and young growth as spinach; seeds as grain.


PREPARING WILD PLANTS

General:
— Bitter often means tannins — boil or soak.
— Fibrous — long boil softens.
— Unknown — tiny test portion, wait for reaction.

Reducing bitterness:
1. Cold soak 30–60 min.
2. Blanch 1–2 min; discard water.
3. Double-boil: discard first water.

Nutrient retention:
— Do not overcook — vitamins break down. ~5–10 min for many leaves.
— Salt improves taste and helps break down fiber.
