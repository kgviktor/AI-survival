CHAPTER 6. SECURITY
6.2 Shelter defense: barricades, traps, alarms

LAYERED DEFENSE CONCEPT

Good protection uses multiple layers. Each layer buys time and information:

Layer 1 — Perimeter (far): noise trips, warning devices, visible “occupied” cues.
Layer 2 — Approaches: obstacles that slow and sound.
Layer 3 — Entry: hardened doors, fixed barricades.
Layer 4 — Interior: safe room, escape routes.

Goal is not to “win a siege” — goal is time to decide (leave or fight) and preserve surprise.


PERIMETER AND ALARMS

Simple trip systems:

Cord trip:
— Cord/fishing line ~15–20 cm above ground across likely paths.
— Tie cans with pebbles, bells, metal lids.
— Hard to see at night; noise on contact.
— Set ~10–15 m from shelter.

Brush and debris:
— Dry crunch (twigs, leaves, gravel) on paths — every step heard at night.

Broken glass:
— Under windows and along walls outside — instant noise cue.

Bedroom trip line:
— Fine thread across path to bowl of water or can by sleeping position — wakes you if pulled.

Yard perimeter:
— Fence/wall plus topping hazards (glass, nails, barbed wire) where legal and ethical for your situation.
— Gates are weak points — reinforce and add solid bolts.

Night lighting:
— Lights aimed at approaches.
— Backlighting: light in attacker’s eyes; you stay in shadow.


BARRIERS AND HARDENING

Doors:
— Metal doors preferred over hollow wood.
— Extra bolts, bars, hasps.
— Wood door: cross-brace ~50×100 mm in brackets — resists kicking.
— Diagonal brace floor-to-door at ~45° — simple strong block.
— If door swings out, pin/weld exterior hinges when feasible.

Furniture barricade:
— Sofas, cabinets, fridge at door.
— ~60–80 cm depth of mass slows even armed entry.
— Fill gaps — no run-up space.

Windows:
— Shutters first choice.
— If none: board the frame, not just the glass pane.
— Sandbags inside below window line — some ballistic help from street level.
— Boarded window with narrow slit — observation port.

Stairwells (upper floors):
— Remove lower steps if desperate — rope ladder for your exit only.
— Debris on stairs — noise + “abandoned” look.

Building entry:
— Seal main entry to look vacant (brick, boards, junk).
— Use side window, roof hatch, adjoining building as true entry.
— Hide signs of use at the obvious door (light, noise, tracks).


NON-LETHAL DELAY DEVICES

Goal: warn and slow, not kill.

Pit trap:
— ~50–60 cm deep on path, covered with sticks/leaves.
— Leg drops — noise, loss of momentum.
— Only where enemy approach is likely; mark for your team (secret tree blaze).

Trip line / stumble cord:
— Ankle-height cord to cans or simple stumble.

False trails:
— Beat decoy paths away from real shelter.
— Old cold fire rings on false routes.


OBSERVATION

Day:
— Window from inside low (sit or lie) — head not silhouetted.
— Angle view slightly off direct line through slit.
— Do not press against glass — silhouette visible.

Night:
— Do not stare from dark room into bright outside — ruins night vision.
— Use peripheral vision — look slightly beside the target.
— Optional distant external LP/OP.

Group watch:
— Shifts ~2–4 h.
— Two watchers: one scans, one listens (different sectors).
— Signal line to shelter (cord tension codes, radio if available).


ESCAPE ROUTES

Always have Plan B:
— Second exit: other window, roof hatch, neighbor unit, basement connector.
— Route rehearsed; whole group knows it.
— Rally point if split during evacuation.

Cache along route:
— Small stash of water, food, map ~200–500 m from main shelter — if you bolt empty-handed.

“Never trap yourself”:
— One exit only = trap. Secure second route before settling in.
