CHAPTER 9. COMMUNICATIONS AND INFORMATION
9.5 Collecting and assessing situational information

INFORMATION AS A SURVIVAL RESOURCE

Bad intel kills:
— “That route is safe” → ambush or exposure.
— “Food there” → wasted march or trap.
— “It’s over” (false) → you stay and lose.
— “Hazmat upwind” → you mask and detour in time.

Keep updating:
— Where are threats (armed groups, fire, contamination)?
— Where are resources (water, food, meds, fuel)?
— Which routes are viable?
— What are authorities doing?
— Is the trend improving or deteriorating?


SOURCES

Radio:
— National/local AM/FM: official summaries.
— Shortwave: international and regional broadcasters.
— Ham traffic: eyewitness nets (accuracy varies).
— Scanner: public-safety traffic **where legal**.
**Bias:** official channels may omit or spin; **cross-check.**

People:
— Most timely, least reliable: rumors, fear, lies.
**Check** against multiple independent witnesses.

Direct observation:
— Highest fidelity **for your line of sight**; limited range.
— Patrol scouts extend this.

Physical traces:
— Tracks, smoke, smell, building damage, animal behavior — honest but need interpretation.

Notes left by others:
— “Danger,” “water this way” — verify age and motive.

Internet / messengers (early crisis):
— Fast; also rumor firehose; state and non-state disinfo.

Off-grid mesh (where deployed):
— Meshtastic, goTenna-class — no central server; **~1–5 km** mesh pockets; still spoofable.


CREDIBILITY (NATO-style reporting shorthand — adapted)

**Source reliability**
A — Confirmed reliable (your eyes; vetted partner).
B — Usually reliable.
C — Unreliable history but maybe right now.
D — Unknown.
E — Known liar / panic source.

**Information credibility**
1 — Confirmed by independent sources.
2 — Probably true.
3 — Possibly true.
4 — Doubtful.
5 — Improbable.

Example: **A1** → act urgently if stakes demand. **D4** → ignore until corroborated.

Field simplification:
— Eyewitness vs hearsay vs rumor?
— Distance from event?
— How old is the report?
— Does it match other channels?


COMMON ANALYSIS ERRORS

Telephone game:
— “One armed man” → “platoon” after four retellings. **Trace to origin.**

Confirmation bias:
— Believing what you hope — “it’s getting better” while risk rises. **Hunt disconfirming data.**

False urgency:
— “Decide now!” — classic manipulation. **Pause and verify** when safe.

False authority:
— “They said on radio…” — compare to other sensors.


SITUATION LOG

Why:
— See trends, not vibes.
— Evidence-based decisions.

Row format:
`[Date/time] | Source | Fact | Grade (e.g. B2)`

Examples:
“Mar 15 09:40 | national radio | aid point open at City Hall | A2”
“Mar 15 11:00 | northbound traveler | armed checkpoint ~3 km north | C3”
“Mar 15 13:15 | own eyes | east horizon smoke, burnt smell | A1”

Map sketch:
— X = threat, O = resource, arrow = confirmed safe movement, ? = unverified.

Analysis habits:
— Convergence from independent sources → higher confidence.
— Single extreme outlier → verify before betting lives.
— Timestamp everything — intel rots fast.


COLLECTION PRIORITIES

First 24 h:
— Utilities status (water, power, fuel).
— Are official services functioning?
— Threat class: random vs organized; short vs prolonged?

Days 2–7:
— Armed presence map; open routes; food/water; recovery vs collapse signals.

Weeks+:
— Power balance; “safe” zones; emerging rules of the new normal.

**Never** assume the picture is complete — it changes hourly.
