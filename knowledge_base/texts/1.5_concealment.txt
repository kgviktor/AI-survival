CHAPTER 1. SHELTER AND A SAFE LOCATION
1.5 Camouflage and concealment of shelter

WHY CONCEALMENT CAN BEAT COMFORT

In urban survival around other people, a discovered shelter is more dangerous than an uncomfortable one. Looting bands look for signs of occupancy — smoke, light, noise, trash. Even peaceful groups may demand resources or take the site. Rule: the best shelter is one nobody knows exists.


SIGNS OF OCCUPATION THAT GIVE YOU AWAY

Outside:
— Light at night (from windows or board gaps)
— Smoke (from fire, especially from chimneys or windows)
— Cooking smells
— A path or flattened grass to the entrance
— Tracks in snow or mud at the door
— Trash near the shelter
— Fresh footprints on dusty floors at the threshold
— Sounds: talking, coughing, footsteps, hammering

Noticeable from inside but visible outside:
— Cobwebs cleared at the entrance (looks lived-in)
— Clean tracks in interior dust
— Cooking odor leaking out
— Noise in a vent pipe

Key principle: from outside the building should look abandoned and unappealing.


OUTDOOR CAMOUFLAGE

Maintain an “abandoned” look:
Do not clean trash and debris at the entrance — it is cover. If you tidy inside, haul waste far away and scatter it; do not pile it nearby. Leave a few deliberate junk pieces by the door. Cobwebs at the entrance suggest vacancy — recreate them with thread if you cleared them.

Entrance:
Avoid the obvious main door if alternatives exist. Side doors, first-floor windows, service entries are quieter. Mask approach tracks: scatter debris, leaves, or cloth scraps at the threshold. Change entry points periodically if you can.

Tracks:
In snow: brush with a branch broom. Walk existing paths others used when possible. Approach the shelter indirectly. In mud: use hard surfaces (asphalt, stone) for the last meters. Exit the same way — fewer new prints.

The area around:
Do not set up visible outdoor “home” features (benches, clotheslines, planters). Keep creature comforts inside. Remove organic waste 100+ m away and vary dump directions.


LIGHT DISCIPLINE

Night light is the most visible giveaway — visible for long distances.

Full blackout:
Seal every window to zero light leak. Black film is best. Multiple dense fabric layers. Cardboard beats thin fabric — fabric can glow with a bright source inside. Check from outside at night.

Working with light:
Shift activity to daylight. At night, minimum light, only when needed. Use the dimmest sources: candle instead of bright flashlight; cup a flashlight in your palm. Light aimed at the floor is less visible than light on walls.

Red light:
Red is harder to see from outside and preserves night vision. Red LED or red film over a white light.

Use interior rooms without windows:
Closet, hallway, bathroom — zero external visibility. Move as much night activity there as possible.


SOUND DISCIPLINE

Sound carries far, especially at night in a quiet city.

Sources to control:
— Speech: low voices, no shouting
— Metal clatter from tools
— Coughing and sneezing: cover your mouth
— Footsteps on hard floors: rags/carpets dampen noise
— Door hinges: lubricate
— Generators and machines: as far from the perimeter as possible, or below ground if feasible

Sound damping:
Heavy fabrics on walls and windows absorb noise. A closed door between noise sources and exterior walls. Work in central rooms, not corner bays.

Silence periods:
Define “quiet hours” (e.g., night or when movement is heard nearby). Everyone knows the signal for total silence.


ODOR DISCIPLINE

Cooking smoke and food smell often attract others first.

Cooking:
Cook with windows and doors closed. Air out during quiet periods when nobody is nearby. Cook less often and in smaller batches.

Smoke:
Hardwood fuels smoke less than wet wood. Wet wood = heavy smoke. Chimney/stack should be tall enough for smoke to disperse before ground level. Do not smoke right at exits — smell lingers.

Trash and waste:
Organic waste rots and smells — remove immediately. Do not stockpile garbage at the shelter.


OPERATIONAL SECURITY (OPSEC)

Do not advertise your shelter location:
“Need-to-know” only — only residents know the exact spot. If you meet others, do not reveal your address. A general neighborhood is OK; a pinpoint is not.

Watch even trusted people:
Good people under stress may leak information. Periodically check that your exits are not under observation.

Cover story:
If seen leaving a building, have a benign reason you were there — not a route to your shelter.


OBSERVING APPROACHES

Organization:
Every 2–4 hours, scan outside from your observation post. View from the window edge, not the center — no silhouette. Keep a simple log: who, how many, when, direction.

Signs the shelter may be compromised:
— Unfamiliar people stop nearby
— The same passerby twice
— Someone staring at your windows
— Fresh outside tracks that were not there yesterday

If suspicious:
Do not rush outside — wait and watch. Prep a fast exit. Hide the most valuable items in case of a search.


LOW-TECH AIDS

Perimeter noisemakers:
Tripwire with cans ~20–30 cm off the ground on approaches. Gravel or glass in pinch points (crunch). Small bells or metal pendants.

Mirrors:
A small mirror lets you see around a corner without exposing yourself.

Withdrawal routes:
Two or three pre-scouted paths, memorized for darkness. Rally points if the group splits.
