CHAPTER 7. NAVIGATION AND MOVEMENT
7.2 Wilderness navigation: sun, stars, natural signs

COMPASS AND MAP IN THE FIELD

Compass is the core wilderness tool.

Azimuth:
— Angle clockwise from north to your direction of travel: 0–360°.
— N = 0°/360°, E = 90°, S = 180°, W = 270°.

Walking an azimuth:
1. Read bearing from map (angle from true or grid north per map legend).
2. Set that bearing on the compass bezel/orientation lines.
3. Rotate body until needle aligns with north mark.
4. Follow the direction-of-travel arrow.
5. Re-check every ~200–500 m — drift accumulates.

Azimuth drift:
— People curve left or right unconsciously.
— At obstacle: offset 90°, sidestep equal distance, offset back 90°, resume line.

Magnetic declination:
— Magnetic north ≠ true north; offset varies by location (~5–20° in many northern areas — check your chart).
— For rough bush navigation, small errors may be acceptable; for precision, apply correction.


SUN

Approximate track:
— Rise E-ish, set W-ish (seasonal swing).
— Solar noon: sun highest in south (Northern Hemisphere).
— Summer: sunrise/ sunset north of E/W; winter: south of E/W in mid-latitudes.

Watch method:
— Analog watch, correct for DST: hour hand to sun; bisector between hour and “12” (or “1” summer) → south in northern mid-latitudes.

Shadow stick (more reliable):
1. Vertical stick in sun.
2. Mark shadow tip — that end is roughly west (morning) or track movement for east-west line.
3. Wait ~15–20 min, mark new tip.
4. Line between marks ≈ east–west.
5. Perpendicular to that line ≈ north–south.


STARS

Northern Hemisphere:
— Polaris near north celestial pole — steady north bearing.
— Find via Big Dipper “pointers” ×5 extension, or between Dipper and Cassiopeia “W.”

Cloudy night:
— Remember bearing before dark.
— Follow large linear features (tree line, shore, power cut).
— Often safer to camp and wait for light than wander blind.

Southern Hemisphere:
— Southern Cross method extended ~4.5× long axis toward south celestial pole — learn local method.


NATURAL SIGNS (USE SEVERAL — NONE ALONE)

Plants:
— Moss denser on more shaded side — often north in open north-temperate forest — unreliable in dense woods or wet hollows.
— Birch bark texture/color asymmetry — weak cue.
— Tree stump rings slightly wider on sunnier side.
— Crown denser sunward.
— Ant hills often sun-side of tree bases — regional.

Snow:
— Faster melt on sun-facing slopes.
— Deeper lingering snow in shade.
— Sun crust patterns on wind-exposed slopes.

Water:
— Streams run downhill to larger water and people.
— Ridge lines divide watersheds.

Wind:
— Prevailing direction is regional — learn local climate.
— Flagging trees on exposed edges may show dominant wind.


PACE COUNTING AND DISTANCE

Calibrate yourself:
— Walk a measured 100 m track, count paces — many adults ~120–135 double-steps (count every second step).

Terrain correction factors (rules of thumb):
— Uphill: shorten effective pace ~10–20%.
— Thick brush: add ~20–30%.
— Night: add ~10–15%.
— Heavy load: add ~5–10%.

Visual distance (human figure, clear air, rough guide):
— ~50 m: facial detail.
— ~100 m: hand motion visible.
— ~200 m: silhouette, no face.
— ~500 m: human silhouette.
— ~1000 m: vague human shape.


READING TOPO MAPS

Basics:
— Contour spacing: tight = steep slope.
— Blue: water.
— Green: vegetation.
— Yellow/white: open ground.
— Built-up: block patterns.

Elevation:
— Labeled contour = that height AMSL; between lines you interpolate.

Scale:
— 1:100,000 → 1 cm = 1 km.
— 1:50,000 → 1 cm = 500 m.
— 1:10,000 urban → 1 cm = 100 m.


IF LOST — STOP

S — Stop (no panic sprint).
T — Think (last known point, intended route).
O — Observe (high points, water, sun).
P — Plan (climb for view, follow stream down, follow linear feature, or resection on map if skilled).

Resection:
— Take two bearings to known peaks — map intersection estimates position.

Signals:
— Three of anything (whistle blasts, fires, gunshots) = distress (when culturally understood).
— Smoke column visible for miles.
— Mirror flash visible for many km in sun.
