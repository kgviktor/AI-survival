CHAPTER 1. SHELTER AND A SAFE LOCATION
1.2 Assessing a building’s structural safety

WHY THIS MATTERS

Building collapse is a leading cause of death in urban disasters. A building can look stable outside and fail without warning. Reading instability signs gives you time to leave before it happens.

Rule: spend 3–5 minutes assessing before you enter and settle in. That time investment can save your life.


EXTERIOR INSPECTION

Foundation:
Walk the building perimeter at the base. Foundation cracks wider than 5 mm are a serious sign. Foundation settlement (one corner lower than the rest) suggests unstable soil. Recent displacement signs: exposed soil, shifted stones at the base.

Walls:
Vertical cracks in load-bearing walls are less dangerous (thermal expansion). Diagonal cracks at ~45° from window and door corners indicate foundation settlement — serious. Horizontal cracks in walls are the most dangerous and suggest bending of a load-bearing member. Outward bowing (wall not plumb) — leave immediately. Fresh cracks: light edges, no dust or dark weathering inside.

Overall building lean:
Place any straight vertical object against the wall and compare. Lean beyond 2–3° from vertical means damage. Check the roofline: it should be straight; sag or kink suggests floor/roof damage.

Roof:
Roof sag is visible along the ridge line. Missing roof sections sharply reduce upper-floor capacity. Fire damage on the roof may have weakened structural members.


INTERIOR INSPECTION

Load-bearing elements:
Identify load-bearing walls — usually thicker (25–40 cm vs 10–15 cm partitions) and often perpendicular to the facade. Columns and beams are primary supports. Cracks on columns in any direction — leave immediately.

Ceilings and floors:
Floor sag is visible — the line between walls should be straight. Cracks around the room perimeter (where ceiling meets walls) suggest the slab is pulling away from walls. Sound test: tap the floor — dull spots may indicate voids or rotted joists.

Floors:
Floors should be level. Sloped floors mean the building has deformed. Local squeaks when walking — test strength before loading weight. Step on suspicious spots and gradually shift weight. Darkened wood near walls — moisture rot and fall-through risk.

Doors and windows:
Skewed door and window frames are clear deformation signs. Doors that will not close or are jammed shut mean the building has twisted. Corner cracks at window openings — stress concentration.


SIGNS OF RECENT FIRE

After fire, load capacity drops sharply:
— Steel beams heated to ~500 °C can lose up to ~50% strength.
— Concrete can crack during rapid cooling (water extinguishing).
— Charred wooden beams are dangerous even if they look intact outside.

How to assess:
Tap a beam — healthy wood sounds bright; charred interior sounds dull. Deformed metal (sag, twist) after fire — do not load it.


BEHAVIOR DURING INSPECTION

Move along load-bearing walls, not through the middle of large spans.
Test each stair tread before shifting your weight onto it.
Do not jump or create vibration in a damaged building.
If you hear a new sound (cracking, groaning under load) — do not ignore it; leave.

“One person” rule:
When scouting a new building, one person enters while others wait outside. If there is no signal within an agreed time (5–10 minutes) — do not enter.


BUILDING CONDITION SCALE

Condition A (safe):
No visible cracks or only minor cosmetic ones. Load-bearing elements are plumb. Doors and windows operate normally. No signs of active fire or flooding.

Condition B (use with caution):
Isolated cracks up to 5 mm in non-load-bearing walls. Slight door/window skew. Signs of old (not active) flooding. Use lower floors; avoid upper floors.

Condition C (temporary only; keep exit clear):
Cracks in load-bearing walls, sagging floors/ceilings, visible deformation, fire damage. Use only if no better option exists, with constant readiness to evacuate.

Condition D (do not enter):
Obvious lean, partial collapse, active fire, sounds of ongoing deformation, gas smell.


SPECIAL CASES

After an explosion:
Blast can damage structures in non-obvious ways — a building may look intact but be weakened. Walls near the epicenter are especially risky. Wait 30–60 minutes after a blast before entering — secondary collapses are common.

After an earthquake:
Aftershocks continue for hours or days. Do not enter a damaged building until the main shaking sequence ends. Each shock can collapse already weakened members.

After flooding:
Water undermines foundations and weakens soil support. A building may seem stable during flood but become unstable after water recedes. Let foundations dry and re-inspect before returning.
