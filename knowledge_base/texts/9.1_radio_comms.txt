CHAPTER 9. COMMUNICATIONS AND INFORMATION
9.1 Radio communications: radio types, bands, procedures

WHY RADIO IN A CRISIS

Phone networks often fail first (overload, tower power loss, deliberate shutdown). Radio:
— Works without carrier infrastructure.
— Needs no internet or cellular.
— Range from ~1–2 km (handheld PMR) to hundreds of km (HF/shortwave).
— Batteries are the only fuel.


RADIO BANDS (OVERVIEW)

VHF (~136–174 MHz):
— Propagation: line of sight + mild refraction.
— Urban range: ~1–5 km handheld; open terrain ~5–30 km.
— With repeater: ~50–100+ km possible.
— Use: professional radios, public safety.

UHF (~400–512 MHz):
— Penetrates buildings and clutter better than VHF.
— Range similar to VHF, sometimes slightly shorter handheld.
— Use: many portables, PMR446 (EU), FRS (US).

PMR446 (~446 MHz — license-free in much of Europe):
— 8 channels (or 16 with CTCSS/DCS subtones).
— No license in participating countries.
— Power: 0.5 W typical.
— Range: ~500 m–3 km terrain-dependent.
— Common brands: Motorola TLKR, Midland, Alan-class units.

Regional license-free UHF (example: some countries use ~433 MHz LPD-style allocations):
— Very low power, many channels where regulated — **check your national table**.

CB (~27 MHz “citizen’s band”):
— Channel plan **varies by country** (e.g. EU vs US).
— Ground-wave range ~3–30 km; sporadic skip can go far.
— Often license-free or light rules — **verify locally**.
— Mobile CB is common among truckers worldwide.

HF / shortwave (~3–30 MHz):
— Ionospheric skip → intercontinental range.
— Transmitting usually needs a license; **listening is widely allowed** (check law).
— Use: amateur radio, military, broadcast.

Marine VHF (~156–174 MHz):
— Channel 16 (156.8 MHz): international calling and distress.
— Coastal and inland waterways where used.


RADIO TYPES

Handheld (HT):
— Compact; Li-ion pack or AA/AAA.
— ~0.5–5 W typical.
— Whip or removable antenna.

Mobile (vehicle):
— 12 V powered; ~5–50 W → much more range than handheld.
— Roof-mounted antenna helps a lot.

Base station:
— Mains powered; ~25–100 W; tall antenna for max range.

Satellite phones (Iridium, Inmarsat, Thuraya-class):
— Work with sky view; expensive hardware/airtime.
— Iridium offers global coverage.
— Starlink-style terminals: internet where satellites are visible.


COMMON SURVIVAL-ORIENTED RADIOS

Baofeng UV-5R family (UV-82, UV-9R, etc.):
— Inexpensive; ~5–8 W; VHF + UHF.
— Programmable to many channels.
— Power: Li-ion or AA shell adapters.
— Cons: needs programming; UI not beginner-friendly; **transmitting outside allowed bands is illegal in most places** — program only lawful channels for your jurisdiction.

License-free PMR446 / FRS-type handhelds:
— Simple operation; no license where permitted.
— ~0.5 W — limited range.
— AA/AAA — easy to stock.

CB mobile/handheld:
— Longer ground-wave range than tiny license-free handsets.
— Mobile use possible; trucker traffic = traffic and road intel.


CHANNELS AND FREQUENCIES TO REMEMBER

Emergency / calling (verify locally — plans differ):
— **156.8 MHz** — marine VHF Ch 16 (international distress/calling).
— **121.5 MHz** — aviation emergency (receive-only for most users).
— **CB Ch 9** — widely used as emergency/help in many CB plans.
— **CB Ch 19** — trucker traffic in US-style plans; EU plans differ.
— **PMR Ch 1** — first channel of your license-free plan (e.g. 446.00625 MHz EU PMR).

CTCSS / DCS:
— Opens squelch only for matching tone — **not encryption**; anyone can still hear you on FM.
— Reduces chatter on busy channels.

Group SOP:
— Pre-agree channel + tone + sked time.
— Write on paper: e.g. “PMR3, tone 7, daily 20:00 local.”


RADIO PROCEDURE AND SECURITY

Basic etiquette:
— “Over” at end of transmission — await reply.
— “Out” — end of contact.
— “Roger” / “Copy” — acknowledged.
— “Say again” — repeat.
— “Break” or callsign — interrupt if urgent.

Calling:
1. “[Name], this is [Name], over.”
2. Wait 10–15 s.
3. Repeat if no answer.

Brevity:
— Short messages; no filler.
— Pre-agree codewords for common situations.

**Security:** analog voice is public.
— Never broadcast exact shelter location.
— Use codes: “at base” not a street address; “Point A” not a real name.
— Minimize transmissions — each one is intel for a listener.


TECHNICAL NOTES

Antenna:
— Longer whip usually helps.
— Quarter-wave wire: λ = 300/f(MHz) m; λ/4 = λ/4.
  — ~145 MHz: ~51 cm
  — ~433 MHz: ~17 cm
  — ~27 MHz CB: ~2.8 m
— Vertical polarization common for portables/vehicles.
— Height wins range.

Programming Baofeng-class radios:
— CHIRP (free) from PC.
— Or manual: menu → frequency → save to channel.
— Build a lawful channel list: local PMR/FRS, marine RX if allowed, CB if you own CB, agreed simplex channels.

Power budget:
— Li-ion: top up before storage.
— AA adapter for emergencies.
— Power-save mode sleeps between RX bursts.
— RX ~0.5–1 W electrical; TX 5 W may pull ~1.5–2 A from battery.
