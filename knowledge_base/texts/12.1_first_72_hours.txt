CHAPTER 12. GROUP AND SOCIAL SURVIVAL
12.1 First 72 hours: step-by-step priority plan

WHY THE FIRST 72 HOURS MATTER

The first three days are usually peak chaos and peak risk. Many crisis deaths happen then: panic, bad choices, loss of warmth or water, walking into threats.

Goal: survive, stabilize, avoid irreversible mistakes.

**Stop — think — act:**
— First instinct is often wrong.
— Pause. Five slow breaths.
— Assess. Then move.


HOUR 0–1: IMMEDIATE ACTIONS

1. Physical safety **now:**
— Confirm you are not under immediate threat.
— Move away from fire, collapse, crush crowd, armed contact.
— Get cover (walls, building, any hard screen).

2. Self triage:
— Bleeding? Stop it first (minutes matter).
— Hypothermia? Get warmth.
— Gas? Exit the structure.

3. Gather people:
— Family, neighbors in sight.
— Help the injured.
— Calm panic in one line: “I’m here. We’ll handle this.”


HOUR 1–3: INITIAL SITUATION PICTURE

Five questions:

1. **What happened / is happening?**
— Tech disaster, natural event, civil conflict, infrastructure blackout?
— Short (hours to days) vs long (weeks+)?
— Scale: block, city, region, nation?

2. **Am I safe here?**
— Shelter solid or must relocate?
— Known safer locations nearby?

3. **What resources do I have?**
— Quick scan: water, food, meds, clothes, tools, comms.
— What is in this unit/building **right now**?

4. **Who is with me?**
— Head count. Kids? Sick? Elderly?
— Who can work actively?

5. **How do I get information?**
— Battery radio on **now**.
— Phone while it works: news, texts to family.
— Neighbors: what do they know?


HOUR 3–6: URGENT NEEDS

Priority 1 — **Water:**
— Fill everything while taps still pressurize.
— Tub, buckets, pots, bottles.
— Plan ~10–15 L/person/day minimum.

Priority 2 — **Warmth:**
— Heating status? If none, **one warm room** for the group.
— Layer all clothing; move sleeping bags/blankets into that room.

Priority 3 — **Comms / intel:**
— SMS often works when voice fails.
— FM/AM radio — official emergency channels.
— Write down what you learn.

Priority 4 — **Food:**
— 72 h without food is miserable but usually not immediately fatal.
— Inventory: how many days at current burn?
— **Do not** feast through stocks day one — duration unknown.


HOUR 6–24: STABILIZE

Shelter:
— Damaged (cracks, fire, flood) → leave.
— Safe and stocked → usually better than blind flight.
— Bug-out only if threat clearly exceeds travel risk.

Lock down:
— Doors and windows secured.
— Watch rotation (~2 h shifts if possible).

Inventory:
— Count food, water, meds, light, tools, clothes — **on paper**.
— Divide into daily ration units so remaining days are visible.

Medical:
— Hidden injuries on everyone.
— Chronic meds: how many days left?
— Wounds: clean, dress, watch infection.

Tasking:
— Even pairs: cook, watch, listen street.
— Work reduces panic.


HOUR 24–48: RECON AND CONTACTS

Neighborhood recon (careful):
— **Pairs only**; announce route and **return time**.
— Map water, other survivor clusters, threats.

Neighbors:
— Knock, ID yourself, trade information for information.
— Note skills: medic, electrician, mechanic, people with stocks.
— Do not advertise full inventory to strangers.

Stay-or-go:
— **Stay** if location is defensible, stocks adequate, no clear better site.
— **Go** if threat ramps, stocks fail, or a **specific** safer destination exists.
— Decide as a group if you are a group.


HOUR 48–72: WEEK-AHEAD PLAN

By hour 72 you want:

1. Situation picture: what, scale, trend.
2. Secured, known base.
3. Water: **≥7 days** if possible.
4. Food: **≥3–7 days** counted.
5. Roles and watch schedule running.
6. Comms: radio sked; meet plan for missing people.
7. Medical: acute issues addressed.
8. **Written log:** facts, inventory, locations.

COMMON EARLY MISTAKES

— Panic moves.
— Splitting the group without rally plan.
— Wasting calories on random travel.
— Burning all food day one.
— Ignoring small wounds.
— Underestimating night cold.
— Trusting strangers unvetted.
— No perimeter watch at night.
