CHAPTER 3. FOOD
3.6 Hunting: snares, traps, primitive weapons

HUNTING WITHOUT A GUN — REALISTIC GOAL

Taking an animal bare-handed or with a stick is almost impossible. Snares and traps work while you sleep — “passive hunting.” Principle: set 10–20 traps and wait. If 2–3 of 20 fire, that is already meat.

Target animals in temperate climates:
— Hare, rabbit (priority — widespread, good size)
— Squirrel (forest)
— Beaver (near water)
— Birds: grouse, partridge, ducks
— Small rodents only as last resort


CHOOSING TRAP SITES

Signs of animal activity:
— Runs: flattened grass, tracks, droppings
— Burrows: near the hole and the trail from it
— Feeding: gnawed bark, browsed shrubs
— Watering: tracks at water early morning

Rule of three points:
Burrow — trail — feeding/watering. Place the trap on the trail, in a pinch point (between bushes, at a fallen tree) where the animal must pass through one specific spot.

Scent:
Animals smell humans. Wear gloves when handling traps or rub them with soil, pine duff. Do not touch unnecessary area around the trap.


SIMPLE SNARE (LOOP)

The simplest and most effective trap.

Material:
Steel wire 0.5–1 mm diameter (ideal), copper wire, strong cord. Nylon fishing line is strong and low-odor — good choice. Rope is worse — visible and smells.

Hare loop:
— Loop diameter: 10–12 cm (fist-sized)
— Height above ground: 8–10 cm (hare head height when walking)
— Knot: sliding (tightens on pull)
— Anchor: to branch, driven stake, tree trunk

Sliding knot:
Twist one end of wire into a small ring (several wraps). Pass the other end through the ring — you get a loop that tightens under tension.

Placement:
Pick a spot on a run where bushes narrow. Brace a side branch at the right height. Hang the loop vertical, slightly narrowing the trail. Anchor firmly — the animal must not drag the snare away.

Bird loops:
Several loops 5–7 cm on the ground on a feeding spot. Scatter grain or berries between loops.


DEADFALL TRAP

Crushing trap: a weight drops on the animal.

Simplest variant (“figure 4”):
Three sticks form a “4” holding a flat rock or log. When the bait is disturbed, the structure collapses and the weight falls.

How to build:
1. Horizontal stick: on the ground, bait tied at the end.
2. Vertical stick: props the horizontal.
3. Diagonal stick: holds the rock, rests on the vertical.
Everything holds by balance only. Bait — acorn, mushroom piece, grain.

Weight: 3–5 kg rock enough for small rodent. For hare — 10–15 kg.

Cage fall trap:
Box propped on a stick with cord; bait tied to the stick. Pull bait — stick falls, box covers. For birds and rodents. Needs checking often.


PIT TRAP

For larger animals (fox, boar, deer in extreme need).
Depth: fox — ~1 m, boar — 1.5–2 m. Diameter: slightly larger than the animal.
Camouflage: branches, leaves, grass on top. Bait on edge or center.
Labor often not worth it for a small group — use only when truly necessary.


SLINGSHOT AND SLING

Slingshot:
Forked branch + rubber from inner tube. Projectile — stone, steel ball. Effective range: 10–20 m. Needs practice. Small birds and rodents.

Sling:
Leather/cloth pocket with two cords. 50–100 g stone spun and released. Historically used on large game. Effective range up to ~50 m. Needs serious practice.

Throwing stick (rabbit stick / non-returning):
Heavy curved branch 60–80 cm. Thrown horizontally into a flock of birds — can hit several. Simplest way to take birds without gear.


SPEAR

Straight pole 1.5–2 m, sharpened at one end. Use hard wood (oak, ash, birch). Harden tip: heat in fire and cool — harder. For fish in shallow water, finishing wounded game. Against large predators — last resort only.


DRESSING GAME

Hare/rabbit:
1. Skin: cut ring around hind legs, strip like a sock toward head. Easy on fresh kill.
2. Guts: slit belly lengthwise, remove intestines and stomach. Keep heart, kidneys, liver — edible and nutritious.
3. Rinse or wipe with snow.
4. Boil 30–40 minutes.

Bird:
1. Pluck or skin with feathers (faster).
2. Remove guts through cut near tail.
3. Keep gizzard (clean contents), heart, liver.
4. Boil 20–30 minutes.

Rule: always cook wild game thoroughly. Parasites (e.g. Trichinella, Toxoplasma) die at ~70°C internal meat temperature. Do not eat raw.


TRACKING

Species from tracks:
— Hare: two large hind prints ahead, two small front — characteristic “four”
— Fox: single-file almost straight (cat walk), round pads
— Dog: two rows, prints larger, more rectangular
— Wild boar: two spread hooves, large track
— Deer: two pointed hooves

Freshness:
— Fresh track sharp, edges not melted
— Snow: edges start melting after several hours
— Mud: dry edges = old, wet = fresh

Direction:
— Toes/hooves point the way
— Mud “squished” in direction of travel
— Uneven spacing = walking, even = running
