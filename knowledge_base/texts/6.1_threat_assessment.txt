CHAPTER 6. SECURITY
6.1 Threat assessment: people, looters, groups

BASELINE

Most people in a crisis are not enemies. They are scared, disoriented, and trying to survive like you. A violent minority is genuinely dangerous. The task is to tell them apart quickly, avoid dangerous contact, and avoid creating needless conflict with safe people.

“Presumed neutral” rule:
Treat strangers as potentially neutral until threat signs appear. Hostility toward everyone burns resources and makes enemies where you could have allies.


HUMAN THREAT LEVELS

Level 1 — Low:
— Singles or pairs without weapons seeking food/water/shelter.
— Refugees, families with children, elderly.
— Scared, confused, disorganized.
Tactic: neutral contact or silent avoidance.

Level 2 — Medium:
— Armed singles or small groups (2–5) without clear organization.
— Opportunistic looters: strike when safe, avoid resistance.
— Aggressive if they feel superior; back off if you show readiness.
Tactic: avoidance, show defensive readiness, hide resources.

Level 3 — High:
— Organized armed groups with hierarchy and clear criminal intent.
— Systematic behavior (patrols, turf control, “tax” collection).
— Will use violence preemptively.
Tactic: full avoidance, stealth, recon beforehand.

Level 4 — Critical:
— Armed formations in active combat zones.
— Structured gangs controlling territory.
Tactic: leave the area or maximum concealment.


DANGER INDICATORS

Visual environment:
— Organized armed patrols.
— Checkpoints controlling movement.
— Fresh violence (bodies, destruction consistent with attack).
— Burned buildings, systematic looting signs.

Contact with people:
— Active probing: many questions about your shelter, stocks, group size.
— Forced conversation while others watch (distraction tactic).
— Weapon display without clear reason.
— Trying to enter shelter or get directions “to you.”
— Surveillance: same person/silhouette in multiple places.
— Nervousness disproportionate to situation.
— Vague offers (“help,” “join us”).

Body language:
— Hands hidden or moving toward waistband.
— Flanking movement.
— Eyes on your gear, not your face.
— Group splits — attempt to surround.


ASSESSING A GROUP

When observing a group:

Size: 2–3 may be manageable; 10+ is serious if hostile.
Weapons: visible arms and how they carry them (ready vs slung).
Organization: chaotic mob vs coordinated leader-led team.
Behavior: scanning everything (threat) vs moving with clear purpose (neutral).
Gear: lots of kit/food — may not need to rob you.
Women and children present — lowers odds of immediate attack (not zero).


DETECTING SURVEILLANCE

While moving:
— Stop on pretext (tie shoe, read map) — follower must stop or pass awkwardly.
— Walk a loop or square (four turns) — random person will not mirror route.
— Fake entering a building; watch the street.
— Change pace: sudden fast, then slow.

If surveillance is likely:
— Do not lead them to shelter.
— Enter multi-exit building; leave another way.
— Lose them in crowds or alleys.


AREA INTELLIGENCE

Before going out or relocating:
— Watch from a safe position without exposing yourself.
— Ask trusted contacts you already know.
— Route signs: tracks, engine noise, smoke, voices.
— Night observation: lights, sound directions.

Threat map (mental or on paper):
— Where/when you saw armed people.
— Looting signs.
— Friendly/neutral encounters.
— Safer vs unsafe routes.
— Where conflicts happened.

Update daily — situations change.


TIME FACTOR

Crisis timeline (very general):
— First 24–72 h: chaos, panic, random clashes, first looting.
— 1–2 weeks: easy resources gone, aggression rises, groups form.
— Month+: territorial division, hierarchies, “new order.”

Time of day:
— Night: more covert hostile activity; movement riskier.
— Very early morning (~03:00–05:00): often quietest; good for discreet movement.
— Day: more witnesses, less open violence in some contexts, but more encounters.
