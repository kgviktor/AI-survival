CHAPTER 9. COMMUNICATIONS AND INFORMATION
9.2 Gathering information: receivers, news and monitor frequencies

WHY INFORMATION MATTERS

Situational awareness drives survival decisions:
— Where it is dangerous vs safer.
— Whether evacuation routes are open.
— Official guidance.
— Trend: improving or worsening.
— Where food, medical aid, shelter may exist.

Without information you decide blind.


RECEIVER TYPES

AM/FM broadcast radio:
— Cheapest, most common.
— FM (~88–108 MHz region-dependent): local stations, good urban audio.
— AM (~530–1700 kHz region-dependent): longer ground wave; often better at night.

Multiband portable (AM/FM/SW):
— Shortwave ~1.6–30 MHz: international and regional broadcasters.
— Examples often cited: Tecsun PL-330, PL-880; Grundig/Sangean-class travel receivers.
— **Schedules and frequencies change** — use station websites or the *World Radio TV Handbook*.

Air band (VHF ~118–137 MHz):
— ATC and aircraft comms; weather and traffic picture near airports.
— Needs a receiver with air band (many portables include it).

Wideband scanner / communications receiver:
— Monitors many services where legal (police/fire vary **heavily by country** — know the law).
— Dedicated receivers: Uniden Bearcat-class, Icom IC-R6, Yaesu VR-500 family, etc.
— A Baofeng in scan mode can sweep amateur/simplex ranges you are allowed to monitor.

Survival receiver checklist:
— **AA/AAA power** preferred over proprietary-only packs.
— At least **AM + FM + SW** for global news resilience.
— Good SW sensitivity matters.
— Ruggedness; **headphone jack** for discreet listening.


USEFUL MONITORING TARGETS (GENERAL)

Local broadcast FM/AM:
— Often last mass medium up in many crises.
— National/regional PSAs and emergency crawls may appear.

International shortwave (examples — **verify current schedules**):
— BBC World Service, VOA, DW, RFI, etc. publish frequency lists online.
— Frequencies and hours change seasonally.

Marine distress:
— **156.8 MHz** — VHF Ch 16.

Aviation emergency:
— **121.5 MHz** — ELT/emergency guard (region rules vary).

Government / civil-defense VHF (highly **country-specific**):
— Some nations use fixed VHF/UHF channels for sirens or civil-defense nets — research your national allocation if you use a scanner.

Amateur (ham) bands (receive is usually easy; transmitting needs license):
— ~144–148 MHz (ITU Region varies): local FM, repeaters, nets.
— ~7.0–7.2 MHz (40 m) and ~14.0–14.35 MHz (20 m): long-distance SSB/CW.
— EMCOMM: many countries have agreed net frequencies — look up local ARES/RACES/society guides.


RECEPTION TECHNIQUES

Directional pickup:
— Rotate AM ferrite rod for null/peak.
— SW: 10–20 m wire outdoors or along eaves — major SNR gain.
— FM: extend whip vertical; try position/height.

Grounding:
— Receiver ground to cold water pipe, radiator, or driven stake can help SW noise floor (safety: understand lightning risk to long antennas).

Headphones:
— Stealth; keep one ear open for surroundings.
— At night: dim display; low volume — light and sound discipline.

Time of day:
— HF/SW and MW often propagate farther **after local sunset** into night.
— FM local: less diurnal, terrain-limited.


LOGGING AND TRIANGULATION

Log every significant report:
— Date/time, source, one-line fact, credibility tag.
— Example: “Mar 12, 14:30 — national radio — corridor X closed — medium confidence.”

Compare sources:
— One station ≠ truth.
— State media may downplay; foreign media may hype.
— Eyewitness radio traffic can be sharper — or wrong.


STORAGE

Spare cells:
— Keep **20–30+ AA** or your radio’s cell type separate from the radio.

Protection:
— Zip/dry bag daily; Faraday storage if EMP/lightning paranoia applies — remove only to listen.

Rechargeables:
— NiMH AA + small solar charger extends listen endurance off-grid.
