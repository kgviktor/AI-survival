CHAPTER 7. NAVIGATION AND MOVEMENT
7.6 City evacuation: route planning

EVACUATION TYPES

Planned (days of warning):
— Gradual threat — time to pack, choose route, coordinate.
— Most controllable.

Urgent (hours):
— Sudden combat, fire, chemical alert.
— Grab go-bag; priority: documents, water, warmth, meds.

Immediate (minutes):
— Direct lethal threat now.
— What is on you + bag by door — go.

Prepare in advance:
— Go-bag packed by exit.
— Routes walked/driven in peacetime.
— Rally points if separated.
— Documents in one known place.


PRE-PLAN ROUTES ON THE MAP

1. Primary highway exits from city.
2. Secondary arterials and collector roads.
3. Dirt tracks, cuts, forest roads (OSM and local maps).
4. Bridges — memorize locations and alternates (fords, other bridges).
5. Rivers/canals — crossing points without bridges.
6. Underground utilities — last resort, often illegal and extremely hazardous.

Three ready routes:
— A: fastest main roads.
— B: secondary street bypass.
— C: foot route through yards/alley mesh if roads blocked.

Rehearse:
— Drive or walk once; note landmarks, chokepoints, detours.
— Save offline maps/prints.


ROUTE CHECK BEFORE YOU STEP OUT

Chokepoints (easy to block):
— Bridges, tunnels, one-lane gaps between buildings, ramp metering to highways.

If blocked:
— Know 2–3 alternate crossings per water barrier.
— Foot detour or direction change.

Intel:
— High-ground watch over route.
— Talk to recent arrivals from that vector.
— Radio/news if any.
— Gunfire, smoke, crowd flow direction.


MOVING THROUGH THE CITY DURING EVAC

On foot:
— Alleys and yards vs main boulevards early in crisis.
— Walk along building walls, not mid-street.
— Fast across intersections after peeking.
— Avoid open plazas/parks early — exposure and overwatch.

By vehicle:
— Main highways often jam first ~24–48 h.
— Secondary roads preferred.
— If stuck, do not block middle — edge the convoy.
— Leave with full tank when possible.

Combo:
— Drive to urban edge, then foot — carries more gear until roads die.

Timing:
— Very early (~03:00–05:00): low activity, thinner traffic.
— Or late evening for darkness cover.
— Avoid mid-morning crush when everyone tries to leave at once.


GROUP COORDINATION

Rally points:
— A: near home (porch, adjacent block).
— B: district edge (park, major corner).
— C: city exit landmark.
— Wait windows ~30–60 min each, then proceed to next if missing persons not located safely.

If split:
— Next rally point — do not solo back into danger for gear unless calculated.

Limited mobility:
— Preassign who assists whom; plan step-free paths for wheels.
— Elderly: slower pace, more breaks.
— Toddlers: carry systems (carrier) — they cannot match adult distance.

Pets:
— Carriers/leashes; dogs may bark — stealth tradeoff.


STASHES AND LAYOVERS

Long routes (~50+ km+):
— Prearranged rest stops (friend’s cottage, known building).
— Small caches ~20–30 km apart: water, food — everyone knows locations.

Rest discipline:
— Concealed rest site; security watch.
— Fire only if safe — smoke signature.
— Short sleep blocks first 24 h if forced march.

“Do not camp on the prize”:
— Obvious resources (bridgehead, store, station) attract others — pass ~1–2 km beyond before sleep.


AFTER LEAVING THE CITY

First-night mistakes:
— Stopping ~5–10 km out — still in scavenger range.
— Obvious bivouac sites.
— No recovery sleep — bad decisions next day.

Minimum distance first camp (when urban violence is driver):
— Often cited ~15–25 km — ~4–6 h foot for fit adult — adjust to threat.

“Far enough” signs:
— City noise gone.
— Empty route.
— Structures not freshly looted along path.
