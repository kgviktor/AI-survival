CHAPTER 4. HEAT, WARMTH, AND FIRE
4.6 Body insulation: clothing, layers, sleep systems

PHYSICS OF BODY HEAT LOSS

Body loses heat four ways:
— Convection: wind strips warm air at skin (~40% in many conditions)
— Conduction: contact with cold surfaces (~25%)
— Radiation: IR to environment (~25%)
— Evaporation: sweat and breath (~10%)

Wind doubles or triples cooling rate. Wet clothing loses ~80–90% of insulation. Cold ground steals heat faster than cold air.


LAYERING PRINCIPLE

Three layers — classic:

Base layer:
Job: move sweat off skin. Wet skin = cold skin. Best: merino wool, synthetics (polypropylene, polyester). Cotton is worst next to skin — holds moisture, dries slowly, cools. “Cotton kills” in cold/wet survival. Crisis without proper base: thin synthetic or wool. Linen better than cotton; still not ideal when damp.

Mid layer (insulation):
Job: trap warmth. Fleece, wool, down. Fleece: light, fast-drying, some warmth when damp. Down: best warmth-to-weight; useless when soaked. Synthetic fill (e.g. polyester clusters): heavier than down but retains some warmth damp — often better for harsh survival.

Shell (wind/water):
Job: block wind and precip. Should allow some moisture escape (or you wet the midlayer from inside). In crisis: plastic poncho or trash bag with holes — crude wind shell.

Layer management:
Remove midlayer when working hard — avoid soaking with sweat. Add when stopped. Wet body at rest chills fast.


CRITICAL BODY AREAS

Head:
Up to ~30–40% heat loss can occur from head/neck in cold without cover. Hat first priority. Balaclava or anything covering ears and back of neck.

Neck:
Large vessels near surface. Scarf or high collar saves a lot of heat.

Hands:
Mittens warmer than gloves (fingers share warmth). Deep cold: thin gloves inside mittens. Bare hands in severe cold — limit to ~5–10 min work bursts.

Feet:
Cold from below (conduction through sole). Thick sole insulates from ground. Two socks: thin synthetic + wool. Do not lace boots too tight — cuts circulation, feet freeze.

Groin and armpits:
Major vessels — warm packs here help warm blood reaching extremities.


IMPROVISED CLOTHING

Newspaper insulation:
Several layers of newsprint under outer clothes — surprisingly effective; air pockets between sheets.

Polyethylene under clothes:
Thin plastic bag on torso under layers — cuts convection. Downside: sweat trapped. Use in wind/cold when not working hard.

Trash bag rain shell:
Large bag with head/arm holes — weak but better than nothing in rain.

Fur from scrap:
Cat/dog pelts — primitive but works. Felt, heavy wool from furnishings.

Stuff voids:
Pack dry leaves, moss, paper between layers — trapped air insulation.


SLEEP SYSTEMS

Main principle:
Insulation under you matters more than on top. Body weight compresses bottom insulation to nearly nothing. Pad under sleeping bag is critical.

Sleeping bag:
Use “comfort” rating, not extreme marketing. Down: light, best warmth/weight; useless wet. Synthetic: heavier; warmer when damp — often better for survival.

Without a sleeping bag:

Method 1 — blanket “matryoshka”:
3–4 blankets. One folded on floor (bottom insulation). Lie on it; others on top. Tuck edges — block drafts.

Method 2 — leaf bed:
Large sack (sheet, cloth) stuffed with dry leaves. Leaves between blanket layers. ~15–20 cm leaf layer gives good insulation.

Method 3 — debris shelter:
Pole frame, leaves and branches. Small space body warms. Example: ~-10°C outside, inside debris hut may reach ~+10…+15°C from body heat alone.

Pad (ground sheet):
Foam panels best. Cardboard ~5–7 layers works. Bough bed ~15 cm — classic. Never sleep on bare frozen ground.

Sleep position:
Curled — less radiating surface. Hands at chest, knees toward belly. Uncurl if too warm — ventilate.


WIND PROTECTION

Wind destroys insulation:
Even a thin windshell matters. Rule: wind block often beats adding thick loft. At ~-10°C with ~10 m/s wind, effective temperature feels like ~-20°C.

Wind breaks:
Rock, wall, dense brush — any obstacle cuts wind speed. Forest interior often 3–5× calmer than open field.

Windshell materials:
Any dense fabric. Polyethylene — excellent. Leather — traditional. Metalized film — reflects IR (emergency blankets).
