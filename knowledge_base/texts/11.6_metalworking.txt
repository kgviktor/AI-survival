CHAPTER 11. TOOLS AND REPAIR
11.6 Metalworking and primitive forge work

WHY IN A CRISIS

Metal skills build and fix tools, weapons, and hardware when supply chains stop. Forging is foundational civilization tech.

Levels:
1. Cold work — cut, bend, rivet — start here.
2. Heat treat — harden/temper steel tools.
3. Hot forge — shape under hammer.
4. Casting — melt and pour — needs high heat and molds.


COLD WORK

Bend:
— Sheet: clamp and fold over wood block with hammer.
— Tube: pack sand before bend to prevent kink.
— Rod: hand or vise.

Cut:
— Hacksaw; chisel for angle iron/pipe; angle grinder if powered; tin snips ~≤2 mm sheet; file to finish.

Drill:
— Brace / hand drill; corded/cordless if power; **oil the bit**; pilot small then enlarge.

Rivet:
— Drill both pieces; insert nail/rivet/bolt; peen tail on anvil.

Threads:
— Die on rod, tap in hole — half turn forward, quarter back to break chips; oil.


STEEL HEAT TREAT (HIGH-CARBON STEEL)

Why: forged or annealed steel is soft; hardening enables edge tools.

Harden:
1. Heat to nonmagnetic / orange (~800–900 °C for many carbon steels) in forge or very hot wood charcoal.
2. **Quench** oil (safer, softer) or water (harder, crack risk) — match steel type if known.

Temper (reduce brittleness):
1. Clean scale.
2. Reheat gently to ~200–250 °C — straw color on polished surface.
3. Air cool.

**Color temperature guide (approximate, ambient light affects):**
— Dark red ~700 °C → cherry ~800 °C → bright red ~900 °C → orange ~1000 °C → yellow ~1100+ °C.

**Does not harden** like carbon steel: most stainless for kitchen, aluminum, copper.

Carbon steel hints:
— Magnetic.
— Grinder spark test: long bright forked sparks = higher carbon.


PRIMITIVE FORGE SETUP

Minimum:
— **Fire** with forced air (raises charcoal/coke to welding heat).
— **Anvil** — rail chunk, thick plate, or hard stone interim.
— **Hammer** ~1–3 kg.
— **Tongs**.
— Quench oil or water bucket.

Charcoal forge:
— Brick or steel box; **tuyere** pipe low; bellows or blower.
— Fuel: **blacksmith coke** best; hardwood charcoal works with strong blast.

Bag bellows:
— Two boards, leather hinge “lung,” one-way flap valves — alternate stroke continuous blast.

Charcoal making:
— Pit burn: wood pile, ignite, smother with soil when charred; cool ~12+ h before opening.
— Or sealed drum with small vent — safer control.

Basic operations:
— **Draw** — lengthen bar under hammer, rotate 90° often.
— **Upset** — heat end, hammer on anvil face to thicken.
— **Hot bend** — over horn or edge.
— **Punch hole** — hot drift on backing plate with hole.

Priority outputs:
— Knives from leaf spring / old file (check law on weapons).
— Chisels, punches from rail or tool steel scrap.
— Brackets, staples, hooks, heavy nails from rod.

Lead **fumes toxic** — outdoor ventilation; molten lead burns are severe.


OTHER METALS

Aluminum:
— Soft cold-form; melts ~660 °C — sand casting possible for simple shapes.

Copper / bronze:
— Cu ~1085 °C; bronzes ~850–1000 °C — cookware, wire, not knife steel.

Tin:
— ~232 °C — solder patches, coating.

Lead:
— ~327 °C — historical weights; **toxic vapor and ingestion risk**.


SAFETY

— **Eye protection** always — sparks and scale.
— Leather gloves — not melting synthetics near fire.
— Ventilation for zinc/brass/lead fumes.
— Fire extinguisher / sand / dirt nearby.
— **Hot steel looks like cold steel** — tongs only.
