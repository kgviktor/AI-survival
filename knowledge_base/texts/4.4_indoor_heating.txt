CHAPTER 4. HEAT, WARMTH, AND FIRE
4.4 Indoor heat sources: candles, alcohol stoves, improvised stoves

INDOOR HEAT — DIFFERENT RULES

Open fire indoors without proper ventilation kills. Carbon monoxide (CO) builds invisibly — no smell, no color. Every indoor heating method needs ventilation. That is the main rule of this topic.


CANDLES

Heat output: one candle ≈ ~80 W. In a tiny space ~1–2 m³ (tent, blanket “burrow”) several candles add noticeable warmth.

Flower-pot candle heater:
Several candles under an inverted clay flower pot. Pot stores and radiates heat more evenly than bare flames.

Metal can variant:
Can with holes as shell — candle inside. Side holes for air, top holes for heat exit.

Candle safety:
— Never leave burning candle unattended
— Stable base — tipped candle = fire
— Clearance from combustibles: ~30 cm sides, ~50 cm above
— Avoid strong drafts — flicker can overheat edges

Saving candles:
Paraffin candles are cheap. DIY: rendered fat + cotton wick. Fat candle in can: fill with animal or plant fat, wick from twisted cloth. Burns for hours.


ALCOHOL STOVES

Can stove:
Aluminum can, wick of fiberglass cloth or cotton. Fuel: denatured alcohol, isopropanol (verify purity and additives — some denaturants are toxic). ~100 ml alcohol ≈ 30–40 min burn. Heat ~500–800 W — more than a candle.

Precautions:
— Alcohol flame is nearly invisible by day — do not refill while lit.
— Keep fuel away from stove.
— Metal can gets hot — place on non-combustible surface.

Pressure-style small stoves:
Two aluminum disks with perimeter jets — vapor pressure design. More efficient than simple wick, more fabrication.


IMPROVISED STOVES

Tin can “burzhuyka” / hobo stove:
Large metal drum or stacked cans.

From cans (temporary):
1. Large can (~5 L) body.
2. Hole near bottom for air.
3. Hole near top for stovepipe.
4. Stack open-bottom cans as chimney — exit through window.
5. Load fuel through top (remove lid).

Efficiency: modest but it heats. Chimney exhausting outside is the critical CO control.

Brick rocket stove:
L channel — horizontal fuel feed, vertical heat riser. Pot on riser top. High efficiency, low fuel use. Can be surrounded with brick and mass (clay/stone) for storage heat.

Brick masonry stove:
With bricks and clay mortar — full stove with flue through wall or window. Thermal mass holds heat — radiates hours after fire dies. Warm-up ~1.5–2 h; holds ~4–8 h.

200 L metal drum:
With chimney stub — workable wood stove. Radiates well. Set on bricks — not directly on wood floor.


HEATING WITH STONES

Hot rocks indoors:
Heat large flat stones in fire 30–40 min. Move with shovel/tongs. Hold heat ~4–6 h.

Foot-of-bed rocks:
Heated rocks wrapped in cloth — bed warmer. Avoid smooth river cobbles — may crack. Dry field rock safer.

Bricks as thermal battery:
Ordinary bricks heated in fire hold heat long. Several hot bricks in shelter noticeably raise temperature.


GAS STOVES AND HEATERS

Camping gas stove:
Main job cooking, but adds significant heat in small space. Produces CO — ventilation mandatory.

Catalytic heaters:
Propane/butane or naphtha; flameless catalytic combustion — lower CO than open flame. Some models marketed for ventilated indoor use. Still need airflow.

Gas in cold:
Butane canisters weak below about -5°C. Propane works to about -40°C. Isobutane blends — compromise. Warm canister in pocket or armpit.


SUMMARY COMPARISON

Method           | Heat   | Fuel use | CO risk | Complexity
Candles          | Low    | Low      | Low     | Simple
Alcohol stove    | Medium | Medium   | Low     | Simple
Hot stones       | Medium | None*    | None    | Medium
Gas (stove)      | High   | Medium   | Medium  | Simple
Rocket stove     | High   | Low      | High*   | Medium
Can/drum stove   | High   | Medium   | High*   | Medium
Brick stove      | High   | Medium   | High*   | Hard

* With proper chimney to outside — risk drops to low.

Recommendation for shelter:
Any stove with flue outside + hot rocks for residual heat. Candles and alcohol — very small spaces or supplement only.
