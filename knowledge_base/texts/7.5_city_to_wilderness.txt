CHAPTER 7. NAVIGATION AND MOVEMENT
7.5 City → wild transition: when to go, what to take, routes

WHEN TO LEAVE THE CITY

A core crisis decision — wrong either way costs heavily.

Reasons to STAY:
— Secure shelter and adequate stocks.
— No wilderness skills or kit.
— Deep winter without skills/gear — hypothermia risk.
— Family with young children or elders, no transport.
— Threat appears temporary and manageable.

Reasons to LEAVE:
— City is a combat zone or violence is rising uncontrollably.
— Supplies gone and no safe resupply.
— Disease outbreak, radiation, or other environmental hazard.
— You have a definite destination (relatives, rural retreat).
— Transport and a planned route exist.
— Order collapse + rising predatory violence.

Rule: “where exactly” beats “go or stay.”
Walking into nowhere without skills fails fast. Walking with a plan and skills has a chance.


PACKING — “72 HOURS + BEYOND”

Immediate evacuation (“bug-out”) baseline:

Documents and money:
— ID, vital papers in waterproof bag.
— Cash — cards may fail.
— Photos/scans on phone if available.

Water and food:
— ~2–3 L water.
— Dense food for ~3 days: nuts, chocolate, crackers, cans, ration bars.
— Water treatment (tabs, filter).

Shelter and warmth:
— Sleeping bag or at least survival blanket/bivy.
— Tarp or poncho shelter.
— Spare clothes + rain shell.

Fire:
— Three lighters, waterproof matches, ferro rod.

Med:
— Compact kit: bandage, antiseptic, analgesic, antibiotic if prescribed/stocked.

Light:
— Headlamp + spare batteries; candles.

Nav and comms:
— Compass, paper maps.
— Battery radio (AM/FM; WX if local).
— Phone + power bank.

Tools:
— Fixed blade + folding backup.
— Multitool.
— ~10–15 m cordage.
— Tape, plastic sheet.

Hygiene:
— TP, soap, toothbrush, hand sanitizer; personal needs for women.

Weight targets (rough):
— Fit adult men often cap ~15–20 kg for long foot movement.
— Many women ~10–15 kg sustainable.
— Children: often cited ~10–15% body weight max — adjust to child.
— Overweight pack = slower travel and foot injuries.


CHOOSING EXIT ROUTES (PLAN AHEAD)

Study before crisis if possible:
— All major roads out in every direction.
— Bypasses: dirt tracks, utility cuts, rail embankments (trespass laws apply).
— Rivers and bridges — chokepoints; know fords or alternate crossings.

Direction factors:
— Where allies/kin live.
— Move away from known threat axis.
— Terrain: forested cover vs open fields.
— Water along route.
— Population density along corridor.

Route layers:
— Primary fastest.
— Alternate direction.
— Bail-out stops if you cannot finish the leg.

Daily distance (untrained, loaded, rough guide):
— Road ~20–25 km/day.
— Off-road ~10–15 km/day.
— With small children or elderly ~10–15 km on road.

Rest rhythm:
— ~10 min every ~1–1.5 h.
— Longer midday break.
— Camp ~1.5–2 h before dark.


URBAN–RURAL EDGE (~5–20 KM FROM CENTER)

Dacha/suburb belts:
— Gardens, wells, abandoned seasonal homes — resources and risk (other people).

Industrial zones:
— Materials but chemical/toxic hazards.

Forest belts and parks:
— Transition cover — others use them too.


ENTERING WILD LAND

— Target woods with water source map knowledge.
— Do not enter forest without compass and map skill.
— First ~1–2 nights often easier near forest edge — still some urban salvage access.


FIRST NIGHTS OUT — PRIORITIES

Survival timing (rules of thumb):
— Minutes without air.
— Hours without shelter in extreme cold/wet.
— ~3 days without water (context-dependent).
— Weeks without food.
Shelter and warmth often come first at bivouac.

Campsite (see shelter chapter):
— Not right at water (bugs, fog, flood).
— Wind shelter.
— No dead overhead branches (“widow makers”).
— Off obvious trails.

Common beginner errors:
— Overestimating pace — exhaustion day two.
— Camping too late — poor site and poor sleep.
— Ignoring hot spots on feet until they stop you.
— Pack too heavy — discard trail trash.
— Too little water first days — performance crash.

Foot care:
— Dry socks daily; tape at first hot spot.
— Dry boots overnight; air feet; lubricate skin if available.
