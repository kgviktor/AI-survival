CHAPTER 5. MEDICINE AND FIRST AID
5.1 Basic medical kit: what to find and what substitutes

KIT PHILOSOPHY

In a crisis a medical kit is one of your most valuable assets. Core idea: treat what kills fast (bleeding, infection, dehydration) and what disables people (pain, inflammation). Everything else is secondary.

The “not everything in one box” rule:
Do not chase a perfect list — chase priorities. Better lots of bandaging plus one antibiotic than a shallow “everything” set of weak OTC items.


PRIORITY 1: STOP BLEEDING

Kills in minutes — top category.

What to have:
— Tourniquet: commercial (CAT, SOFTT-W) or improvised wide cloth strip ~5–7 cm. Place proximal to wound on limb for arterial bleeding.
— Hemostatic gauze/powder: Celox, QuikClot, or very tight packing with plain gauze.
— Israeli-style pressure bandages: several.
— Roller gauze: wide (10–14 cm) and narrow (5–7 cm), at least ~5 of each.
— Sterile gauze: for wound packing.
— Wide adhesive tape: secures dressings.
— Nitrile gloves: several pairs.

Substitutes:
Tourniquet: wide belt, cut sleeve, ~5–7 cm cloth + windlass stick. Gauze: any clean cotton cloth. Gloves: plastic bags on hands.


PRIORITY 2: ANTIBIOTICS

Infection is the second fastest killer after bleeding.

Basic set (examples — verify local guidelines and allergies):
— Ciprofloxacin 500 mg: broad spectrum, gut infections, wound infections, pneumonia. ~30 tablets.
— Amoxicillin/clavulanate 875 mg: skin infections, bites, ENT, UTIs. ~20 tablets.
— Metronidazole 500 mg: anaerobes (abscesses, some wound patterns), giardia, amebiasis. ~20 tablets.
— Azithromycin 500 mg: pneumonia, some respiratory/STI patterns. ~10 tablets.

Alternatives:
Doxycycline — broad, often cheaper. Levofloxacin — fluoroquinolone alternative to cipro. With no pharmacy antibiotics — honey (weak topical antiseptic), garlic (topical), vinegar (topical) are not replacements for systemic infection.


PRIORITY 3: ANALGESICS

Pain disables people.

— Ibuprofen 400 mg: pain + anti-inflammatory + fever. ~50 tablets.
— Acetaminophen/paracetamol 500 mg: fever, gentler on stomach for some. ~30 tablets.
— Ketorolac 10 mg: strong short-term pain (prescription in many places). ~20 tablets.
— Tramadol: severe pain — fractures, procedures — prescription only.

Substitutes:
Willow bark: salicin (aspirin precursor). Decoction — mild analgesic. Clove oil: topical dental pain.


PRIORITY 4: ANTISEPTICS

— Chlorhexidine 0.05%: general wound antiseptic. 3–4 × ~100 ml bottles.
— Hydrogen peroxide 3%: mechanical debris lift (bubbles). Few bottles — not for repeated long-term use on healing tissue.
— Iodine 5% (solution or stick): skin and wound edge — not deep cavity flooding.
— 70% ethanol: tools and intact skin.

Substitutes:
40% vodka — weaker than 70% ethanol but some use. Honey — topical. Sugar — packed in wound (hyperosmotic effect). Saline wash (~1 tsp salt per 250 ml water).


PRIORITY 5: REHYDRATION

— Oral rehydration salts (ORS): 10–20 packets, or ingredients to mix your own.
— Activated charcoal: ~100 tablets — some overdoses, diarrhea adsorption.
— Loperamide: reduce diarrhea when appropriate (not with bloody dysentery).


PRIORITY 6: TOOLS

— Trauma shears (blunt tip): cut clothes and bandages.
— Tweezers: splinters, ticks.
— Scalpel or sharp knife: abscess opening when trained.
— Suture needles/thread (or skin stapler): wound closure.
— Thermometer.
— Syringes: irrigation under pressure.
— Flashlight.
— Nitrile gloves: ~10+ pairs.

Substitutes:
Knife for scalpel. Boiled thread (nylon/cotton) and needle — boil/sterilize carefully. Plastic bags for gloves.


PRIORITY 7: MISCELLANEOUS

— Antibiotic eye drops (e.g. ofloxacin class): conjunctivitis.
— Antihistamines (loratadine, diphenhydramine): allergy, insect bites.
— Antifungal cream (clotrimazole): skin fungus, thrush external care.
— Antidiarrheal for specific pediatric protocols if stocked.
— Mild sedatives (valerian, etc.) — stress sleep only; not for severe mental health crises alone.
— Vitamin C — scurvy risk in prolonged crisis with no produce.

Chronic disease extras:
Diabetes: insulin, strips, glucose.
Hypertension: 6+ month backup meds.
Asthma: inhalers.
Epilepsy: anticonvulsants.


WHERE TO FIND MEDS IN CRISIS

Search order:
1. Pharmacies (may be looted — try storerooms)
2. Hospitals and clinics
3. Veterinary suppliers (many drugs identical or parallel to human)
4. Homes (most people have small cabinets)
5. Shops (OTC)

Veterinary labeling:
Ciprofloxacin, amoxicillin, metronidazole often sold for animals — frequently same drug substance. Know dosing conversion risks — human dosing references still apply per weight/indication.

Storage:
Waterproof sealed container. Cool, dark. Inventory list on lid. Check expiry yearly.
