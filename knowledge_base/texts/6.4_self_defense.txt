CHAPTER 6. SECURITY
6.4 Self-defense: principles and improvised weapons

MAIN PRINCIPLE

The best self-defense is avoiding the fight. Most situations can be resolved before force: leave, hide, talk. Physical fight is last resort — no guarantees. “Winners” often take serious injuries too.

Priority ladder:
1. Avoid — do not enter high-risk situations.
2. Leave — at first threat signs, go.
3. De-escalate — talk, show neutrality.
4. Distance — do not let them close.
5. Defend physically — only as last resort.


ATTACKER PSYCHOLOGY

Most attacks are not random. Offenders select for vulnerability:
— Alone easier than a group.
— Distracted, unaware.
— Appearing weak.
— Visible valuables or food.

Look less like prey:
— Confident posture, steady gaze.
— Purposeful movement.
— Environmental awareness (looks around, notices people).
— Part of a group.

Show readiness without aggression:
— Calm eye contact — firm, not staring down.
— Open palms forward: “Stop — I’m not looking for trouble.”
— Low steady voice, not shrill.
— Step back for distance.
Many opportunists back off if you are clearly not easy prey.


DISTANCE AND STANCE

Danger zones:
— Grappling range (~<1.5 m): strikes, grabs.
— Edged weapon range (~1.5–3 m).
— Firearms: effectively any range in open ground.

“Do not let them close” rule:
— Keep ~2–3 m from strangers in tense talk.
— Step back if they advance.
— Continued closing = red flag.

Stressed contact stance:
— Bladed profile (smaller target).
— Lead hand forward (often left forward if right is power hand).
— Feet shoulder-width, slight knee bend, weight balanced.
— Chin down — protects throat.


BASIC STRIKES

Effective targets:
— Eyes: fingers, sand, irritant — temporary blindness.
— Throat: palm-heel or punch — disrupts breathing.
— Groin: knee or kick — works on larger opponents.
— Knees: side strike — breaks balance.
— Nose: palm-heel upward — pain, tears.
— Ears: clap both hands — disorients.

After any strike:
Create pause → leave immediately. Do not stand to “finish.”

If grabbed:
— Strike vulnerable points first.
— Move into the grab (reduces leverage) vs only pulling away.
— Body twist can strip some holds.

From behind:
— Tuck chin (throat protection).
— Head butt backward.
— Heel stomp to instep.
— Grab and hyperextend fingers.


IMPROVISED WEAPONS

Any object that extends reach or concentrates force can be a weapon.

Impact:
— Cane, crowbar, heavy flashlight — two-hand grip, committed swings.
— Hammer, wrench.
— Fire extinguisher cylinder.
— Pan, pot.
— Brick, rock — last resort.

Shield:
— Pack or bag in front — deflect strikes and slashes.
— Chair — shield and striker.
— Board.

Distance / irritants:
— Hair spray, air freshener to eyes — crude pepper-spray analog.
— Hot liquid to face — shock and pain.
— Ash, sand, flour, salt in eyes — blinding distraction.
— Commercial pepper spray if you can obtain it legally.

Knife:
— Attacker with knife changes everything — escape beats dueling if possible.
— Your knife: deterrence and last-resort escape from grab — not dueling fantasy.
— “Knife distance” ~3 m: a committed rush can close faster than many people can run backward — lateral movement and barriers.

Rope, belt, cable:
— Swung end with buckle as flail.
— Restraint if justified and legal.


SCENARIOS

Solo attacker:
— Maintain distance, firm words.
— If attacked: hit vulnerable point → escape.

Group (2+):
— Do not let them surround — back to wall/corner.
— Hit nearest hard to open a gap → run through immediately.

Robbery:
— If they want property — surrender property (replaceable).
— Exceptions: children, dependents, or disarming you when lethal follow-up is likely — judgment call.
— If violence is clearly coming regardless, your plan may differ — training matters.

Night break-in:
— Do not confront unarmed if you can secure family in safe room.
— Loud verbal challenge: “I’m armed, leave” — some flee immediately.
— Fall back to hardened room with phone/exit plan.
— Channel intruder through narrow doorway — no flank room.


MENTAL READINESS

Many people hesitate to harm others — normal. In life-threatening assault, survival instinct can override.

Strong motivators:
— Self-preservation.
— Protecting others (often stronger trigger).

Prep:
— Decide in advance: “If attacked, I strike and run.”
— Visualize specific actions — cuts reaction time under stress.

Aftermath:
— Adrenaline crash: shake, nausea — normal.
— Debrief with someone trusted.
— After-action review for learning, not self-attack.
