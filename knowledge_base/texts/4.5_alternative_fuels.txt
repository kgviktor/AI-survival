CHAPTER 4. HEAT, WARMTH, AND FIRE
4.5 Alternative fuels in town and field

WHEN WOOD IS SCARCE

In urban areas wood is not always the main fuel. Knowing what burns — heat output and smoke toxicity — keeps you from freezing or poisoning yourself.


URBAN FUELS

Furniture and lumber:
Tables, chairs, doors, flooring — become firewood. Lacquered/painted wood — toxic smoke; outdoors or strong ventilation only. Particleboard, plywood: glues release formaldehyde and other toxics — outdoors only.

Paper and cardboard:
Newspapers, books, packaging — fast burn, few coals. Good for starting. Dense cardboard lasts longer. Roll tight — burns much longer than loose. Cardboard “logs”: roll tight, tie with cord — ~20–30 min.

Petroleum oils (motor oil, used oil):
High heat. Outdoors or strong exhaust only — very toxic smoke. DIY burner: metal tray + cloth wick + oil. Mix ~1:1 with sand — slower, safer burn.

Ethanol and isopropanol:
Relatively clean burn; little CO at small scale. Denatured alcohol (may contain toxic denaturants — do not drink). Isopropanol (pharmacy) — usable in alcohol stoves with care. Low smoke — possible indoors with minimal ventilation if flame is small and room is not sealed.

Candle wax and paraffin:
Clean burn. Old candles, paraffin blocks — good fuel. DIY candles from any fat — smokier but work.

Vegetable oils:
Olive, sunflower, any cooking oil — burns in oil lamp. Some smoke; far less than heavy oils. Simple lamp: shallow dish + cloth wick + oil.

Gas (cylinders):
Propane, butane — most convenient. Shops, construction sites, homes may have cylinders. Do not discard “empty” — often residual fuel.


FIELD / BIOFUELS

Herbivore dung (dried):
Cow, horse, deer patties when dry — excellent slow fuel. Used for generations where wood is rare. Collect dry, store dry. Burn is mostly carbon smoke, little odor when dry.

Peat:
Brown layers near bogs. Dry peat burns slow and long. Hard to ignite — add small wood to start.

Coal:
May be found at depots, homes with coal heat. High heat, long burn. Needs draft — may go out in poor stove. Strong CO risk — only with chimney.

Charcoal:
Bagged lump or briquettes for grilling. Easy lighting, even heat, low smoke. Stock if you find it.

Peat briquettes, compressed logs:
Hardware stores. Long burn, low smoke.


UNUSUAL OPTIONS

Wax stubs and fat for lamps:
Any rendered fat — oil lamp. Hemp/linseed oil often smokes less than some others.

Medical 96% ethanol:
Very clean burn — use only in proper stove; open pouring is dangerous.

Vehicle fuel (gasoline, diesel):
Gasoline: extreme fire risk, volatile at room temp. Never routine indoor use. Outdoors only as extreme last-resort accelerant — never pour on flame. Diesel: less volatile; outdoor oil-burner use only. Toxic smoke.

Antifreeze:
Some types burn; very toxic when burned. Outdoor last resort only.

STORING FUEL

Wood:
Dry stack under cover. Season at least ~1 year when possible (green wood ~50–60% moisture; well dried ~15–20%).

Liquid fuels (alcohol, oil):
Dark glass or metal, tight cap. Away from heat and flame. Label contents and date.

Gas cylinders:
Moderate temps (not deep freeze, not >50°C). Away from heat sources.

Coal:
Dry storage. Wet coal loses performance somewhat; recovers when dry.


FUEL ECONOMY

1. Good shelter insulation = less fuel to heat.
2. Smaller heated volume warms faster.
3. Lidded pot ≈ half the fuel to cook.
4. Batch cooking — one big boil beats many small ones.
5. Retained-heat cooking — boil, insulate pot, let finish.
6. Efficient stove/rocket design — ~30–70% vs ~5–15% open fire.
