CHAPTER 4. HEAT, WARMTH, AND FIRE
4.1 Making fire: friction, flint, improvised methods

WHY FIRE-MAKING IS A CORE SKILL

Lighters run out; matches get wet. Ability to start fire with primitive methods is insurance you cannot lose. Even carrying several reliable lighters and knowing how to protect them is a sound strategy.

Reliability hierarchy:
1. Gas lighter (several spares)
2. Waterproof matches in a container
3. Ferrocerium rod — works when wet, lasts very long
4. Optical (lens, mirror) — only in sun
5. Friction (bow drill, fire plow) — needs skill and practice


BEFORE IGNITION: TINDER AND KINDLING

Fire needs three things: fuel, oxygen, heat. The most important and often neglected is tinder: ultra-fine combustible material that catches from a spark or small ember.

Best natural tinder:
— Tinder fungus (Fomes fomentarius): soft gray interior catches spark and smolders long
— Fluffy seeds: fireweed, thistle, dandelion, cottonwood fluff
— Dry moss: especially tundra and forest types
— Dried herbivore dung (horse, cow): excellent tinder
— Thin birch bark (inner layer): volatile oils, burns even when damp
— Chaff from grains, dust from punky wood
— Dry nesting material from bird nests
— Waxed paper, pencil shavings

Improvised tinder:
— Cotton, especially rubbed with petroleum jelly (prepare ahead)
— Char cloth: fabric charred in a closed tin
— Dry charcoal powder

Kindling — next layer:
Twigs ~5–10 mm, thin bark, splinters. Must be dry. Snap with fingernail — should crack, not bend.


LIGHTER AND MATCHES

Gas lighter:
Most reliable when you have it. Carry several. Cold issues: piezo may fail below about -10…-15°C; flint wheel often works better. Warm lighter in fist 1–2 min — fuel vaporizes again. Nearly empty: tilt ~45° to use last drops.

Matches:
Ordinary ones get wet. Waterproof: dip matches in candle wax, let dry. Store in sealed container (pill bottle with lid). “Storm” matches burn hot and long.


FERROCERIUM ROD

Most practical “primitive” spark tool. Sparks in any weather and humidity.

Gear: ferro rod + steel scraper (or knife spine).

Technique:
1. Prepare tinder — ready to receive sparks.
2. Hold rod steady, scrape along it with pressure — or pull rod back against scraper.
3. Aim sparks into center of tinder.
4. When ember glows — blow gently (horizontal, not straight up).
5. When tinder flames — transfer to a “bird’s nest” of kindling.

Bird nest:
Shape kindling like a nest: tinder in center, coarser material around. Place ember in center, close nest, blow from below.


FRICTION: BOW DRILL

Hardest primitive method; needs practice. Works with no manufactured tools.

Parts:
— Spindle: straight dry stick ~40–50 cm, ~1.5–2 cm diameter, pointed lower end
— Fireboard: flat dry board ~2–2.5 cm thick, soft wood
— Bow: curved branch with cord (shoelace, leather strip)
— Handhold / socket: hard wood or stone with pit for spindle top

Wood for spindle and board (soft, same species preferred):
— Willow, poplar, alder, basswood/linden, maple
— Same wood for board and spindle works best

Prepare board:
Cut a shallow pit spindle-sized. Near the edge cut a V-notch (~30°) — char dust falls through.

Technique:
1. Kneel on board pinning it.
2. Under notch place leaf or bark to catch char dust.
3. Wrap bowstring once around spindle.
4. Spindle tip in pit, top in socket (hold with teeth or left hand against shin).
5. Saw bow horizontally — steady pressure, ~30–40 strokes/min.
6. Speed up near the end.
7. Success: denser smoke, black dust — ember.
8. Stop. Remove spindle. Tilt board — ember drops on catch.
9. Transfer ember to tinder, form nest, blow.


FIRE PLOW

Easier than bow drill, less efficient.
Sharpened stick, board with longitudinal groove ~30–40 cm. Rub stick back and forth in groove with pressure. Dust piles at end; ember forms. Soft wood (willow, poplar).


OPTICAL METHODS

Lens (glasses, magnifier, water bottle):
— Focus sun on tinder spot ~3–5 mm.
— Hold steady until smoke.
— Only bright sun.
— Water-filled bottle weaker than glass; needs very fine tinder.

Mirror or polished metal:
Reflect sun through a small hole or notch to concentrate heat.


KEEPING FIRE

Fire is hard-won — conserve it.

Coals in a can: coals in a tin with a few small holes. Smolder for hours. Carry or keep overnight.

Slow burn: green wood + limited air = long slow fire.

Overnight: bury coals in ash — limits oxygen. Morning: scrape ash, blow — may revive after 8–12 h.

Char cloth for next time: char cotton in closed can on fire (no air — chars, does not ash). Catches ferro sparks better than most natural tinder.
