CHAPTER 4. HEAT, WARMTH, AND FIRE
4.2 Fire in difficult conditions: rain, wind, snow

CORE PRINCIPLE

Fire in bad weather comes from preparation, not luck. Who prepares — almost always succeeds. Who hopes for luck — gets cold. Three pillars: dry tinder, sheltered ignition, correct fire lay.


RAIN

Main problem: wet tinder and kindling. Rain does not stop burning — it stops starting.

Dry tinder always:
Keep tinder in waterproof bag or bottle. Birch bark burns when damp — wax and oils. Collect spare bark anytime. Inner dead bark under outer bark often stays dry in rain.

Dry kindling in rain:
— Under low spruce/pine branches: dry needles and twigs
— Lower dead branches on trees: often dry under crown
— Split a wet fallen log — inside is dry
— Peel bark from standing dead tree — often dry under bark

Shelter while lighting:
Shield ignition with body. Bark or branch as mini roof. Light under dense evergreen. Small tarp slit over the spot.

Sequence in rain:
1. Find/build shelter first — do not light in open rain.
2. Prepare tinder (from dry stash).
3. Light under cover.
4. Build up fast — tiny fire dies in rain; move to sustainable size quickly.

Fuel in rain:
Green wood smokes more. Seek standing deadwood — died standing, not in wet soil, top still under canopy. Split wood — interior drier than surface.


WIND

Two problems: wind blows out ignition, and it speeds burn (too intense, wastes fuel).

Wind shield for lighting:
— Stone, log, pack wall — lee side
— Small pit ~15–20 cm — wind passes over
— Use body as shield — back to wind when lighting

Fire in wind:
Dakota / pit fire — best: two pits connected by tunnel. Main chamber for fire, second pit on windward side draws air. Wind feeds draft; flame less visible outside.

Fire orientation:
Open side toward you so heat reflects toward shelter. Side wind — stone/log wall reflects heat.

Burning in wind:
Wind dries fuel — can help damp wood. Stack tighter — concentrate heat. Watch sparks on dry vegetation.


SNOW AND HARD FREEZE

Problem 1: wet snow kills small fire.
Problem 2: frozen ground pulls heat down.
Problem 3: cold fingers — fine motor skills fail.

Platform under fire:
On snow, fire sinks, melts, drowns. Build platform of green logs (3–4 parallel) or stones ~10–15 cm thick. Never fire directly on deep snow without platform.

Avoid water-saturated river stones:
Can explode when heated — steam pressure. Dry field or hill rock safer.

Lighting in deep cold:
Warm hands before fine work (armpits, inside jacket). Birch bark is excellent cold tinder. Ferro works at any cold. Gas lighter: warm in fist.

Fuel in winter:
Standing dead often dry — frost drives off moisture. Conifers (pine, spruce) have resin — burn even if slightly damp. Split wood — fresh split drier than surface.

Fire at -20°C and below:
Needs larger fire — heat loss is fast. Start with extra fuel stockpiled. Birch and oak release more heat per mass than alder or aspen — seek when possible.


GENERAL RULES FOR BAD CONDITIONS

Three-try rule:
If first try fails — do not waste all tinder. Fix the problem (shelter, dryness, airflow), retry.

Kindling layers:
In hard conditions do not skimp. Tinder → matchstick splinters → pencil twigs → finger twigs → fuel. Skipping steps — fire goes out.

Birch — survivor’s friend:
Birch bark burns in almost any condition. Bark, twigs, logs — most reliable when available.

Wet wood last resort:
If only wet wood — start on dry material, add wet gradually. Wet wood smokes — outdoor use. Wet wood beats no wood.

Emergency fire kit in pocket:
Lighter + a few strips of birch bark + a few hexamine tablets (or similar). “Fire reserve” — do not use except real need.
