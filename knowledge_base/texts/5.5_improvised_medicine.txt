CHAPTER 5. MEDICINE AND FIRST AID
5.5 Improvised medicine: plants and available materials

HOW TO USE THIS SECTION

Botanical and folk measures do not replace pharmacy drugs — they supplement or fill gaps when meds are gone. Effects are weaker, onset slower, dosing less precise. In prolonged crisis they can still help.

Use plants mainly for:
— Mild/moderate problems (URI, minor cough, scrapes, mild stomach upset).
— Support alongside primary treatment.
— Comfort and symptom relief.
Do not rely on herbs alone for life-threatening illness.


ANTISEPTICS AND WOUND CARE

Honey:
— Raw honey (especially darker types) has antibacterial properties in wound care literature.
— Apply thin layer, cover with dressing. Hyperosmotic — draws fluid, suppresses bacterial growth.
— Useful for some chronic ulcers, shallow burns (after cooling).
— Medical-grade manuka is standardized; ordinary honey still used traditionally.

Plantain (Plantago major):
— Chew or crush leaves — mild antimicrobial/anti-inflammatory.
— Apply pulp to scrapes, insect bites, small cuts.

Garlic:
— Allicin is antimicrobial.
— Topical: crushed clove on superficial sites only — burns mucosa.
— Oral: some use for mild GI upset — small amounts; GI irritation and bleeding risk at high dose.

Conifer resin (pitch):
— Heat to flow, apply to minor wounds — traditional “tar plaster.”
— Often mixed with fat and ash/soot for field salve.

Mugwort (Artemisia vulgaris):
— Infusion used traditionally for antimicrobial wash (1–2 tbsp dried herb per cup water).
— Internal use for parasites is folkloric — verify plant ID; related species vary in safety.

Chamomile (Matricaria chamomilla):
— Strong traditional anti-inflammatory/antiseptic for mucosa and skin.
— Infusion: 1–2 tsp flowers per cup, steep ~15 min — rinse wounds, conjunctivitis rinse (sterile preparation ideal), gargle.
— Oral: mild GI upset, cramps.

Oak bark:
— Astringent — traditional wash for wounds, gargle for sore throat, sitz bath for diarrhea support.


ANALGESIA AND FEVER

Willow (Salix spp.):
— Bark contains salicin (aspirin precursor).
— Decoction: ~2 tbsp shredded bark per cup, simmer 10–15 min.
— Mild pain/fever/anti-inflammatory effect.
— Avoid if aspirin allergy, bleeding risk, ulcer.

Peppermint (Mentha × piperita):
— Menthol — mild topical cooling/anesthetic feel.
— Crushed leaves on temples for headache; tea for nausea/cramps; steam inhalation.

Clove (Syzygium aromaticum):
— Eugenol — strong topical dental analgesic.
— Powdered clove or clove oil on cotton at tooth — short relief (~30–60 min). Oil can burn skin — tiny amounts.

Tansy (Tanacetum vulgare):
— Historically used for pain/fever — contains toxic thujone and other compounds. Modern guidance: avoid internal use; misidentification and overdose are dangerous.


COUGH AND CHEST

Linden/lime flowers:
— Diaphoretic, mild anti-inflammatory.
— Infusion: 1–2 tbsp per cup — hot drink for colds, cough, fever.

Licorice root (Glycyrrhiza glabra):
— Expectorant, soothing — decoction for dry cough.
— Caution: can raise BP, cause sodium retention — avoid in hypertension.

Pine buds:
— Traditional expectorant tea — long simmer; steam inhalation.

Onion + honey syrup:
— Grated onion mixed 1:1 with honey overnight — folk cough syrup, small spoonfuls.

Thyme (Thymus vulgaris):
— Antiseptic, expectorant infusion for bronchitis.


GI UPSET

Activated charcoal:
— Adsorbent for some toxins/diarrhea: ~1 g per 10 kg body weight.
— Separate timing from other meds by ~2 h.

Strong black tea:
— Tannins — mild astringent for diarrhea.

Flax seeds:
— Mucilage — soothing for gastritis: 1 tbsp seeds in hot water, steep 30 min, drink including seeds.

Ginger:
— Anti-nausea, carminative — tea from grated root.

St. John’s wort (Hypericum perforatum):
— Oil macerate for minor burns/skin (photosensitivity risk).
— Oral traditional use for low mood — drug interactions many (SSRIs, warfarin, etc.) — avoid mixing with pharmaceuticals.


REHYDRATION (HOMEMADE ORS)

WHO-style ORS:
— 1 L clean water
— 6 level tsp sugar
— 0.5 level tsp table salt

Enhanced (optional):
— Pinch baking soda
— Pinch potassium salt OR small boiled potato for potassium

Rice water:
— Boil rice in 6–8× water 20–30 min — starch slows motility in diarrhea; add pinch salt.


OTHER USES

Aloe vera gel:
— Superficial burns and sunburn — apply gel from leaf several times daily.

Cabbage leaves:
— Crushed fresh leaves traditionally for bruises, swollen joints — cool compress effect.

Sodium bicarbonate:
— Gargle sore throat: 0.5 tsp in cup warm water.
— Heartburn: 0.5 tsp in water short-term.
— Not for long-term acid control without medical advice.

Salt:
— ~0.9% saline: 9 g per liter — wound rinse (ideally sterile/boiled cooled).
— Gargle: ~1 tsp per cup.


HARVESTING AND STORAGE

— Pick dry weather, away from roads and contamination.
— Dry shaded, airy — direct sun can degrade actives.
— Paper or cloth bags — not sealed plastic (mold).
— Most dried herbs ~1–3 years if stored cool/dark.

Label:
— Name, date, use notes.
— Do not store toxic look-alikes with food herbs.
