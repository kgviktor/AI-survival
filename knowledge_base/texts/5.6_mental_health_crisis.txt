CHAPTER 5. MEDICINE AND FIRST AID
5.6 Mental health in crisis: stress, shock, depression

WHY THIS MATTERS

In extremes the mind is a resource like food and water. Panic kills faster than hunger. Depression paralyzes decision-making. Acute stress reactions can disable people at the worst time.

Understanding reactions helps you:
— Stay functional in the first hours and days.
— Support group members.
— Spot dangerous states early.


ACUTE STRESS AND PANIC

Physiology:
“Fight or flight” — adrenaline and cortisol. Faster pulse, dilated pupils, impaired rational thought, boosted strength short-term.

Normal: initial fear in danger is adaptive. Problem: loss of control.

Panic signs:
— Rapid breathing (hyperventilation).
— Tremor, weak legs.
— Tunnel focus on threat only.
— Freeze — stops moving or responding.
— Impulsive irrational acts.

Managing your own panic:

4-7-8 breathing:
— Inhale nose 4 s.
— Hold 7 s.
— Exhale mouth 8 s.
— Repeat 3–4 cycles.
Slows heart rate, engages parasympathetic system.

Grounding 5-4-3-2-1:
— Name 5 things you see, 4 you can touch, 3 you hear, 2 smells, 1 taste.
Brings attention to the present, breaks anxiety loops.

Physical task:
— Give yourself a simple physical job — push-ups, walking, carrying — burns off adrenaline through action.

Helping someone panicking in a group:
— Calm, quiet, confident voice. Shouting “calm down” fails.
— Eye contact; use their name.
— One simple task (“hold this,” “walk there”).
— Hand on shoulder if culturally appropriate — grounding touch.


ACUTE STRESS REACTION (“PSYCHOLOGICAL SHOCK”)

Right during or after catastrophic events.

Signs:
— Stupor, blank stare, disorientation.
— Aimless motor restlessness.
— Unstoppable crying.
— Irrational aggression.
— Rarely hallucinations in extreme cases.

Actions:
1. Safety first — move out of immediate danger.
2. Do not leave them alone.
3. Short phrases: “You are safe,” “I am here,” “We have a plan.”
4. Warmth and drink (tea) — basic physiological care.
5. Allow crying — do not force “stop.”
6. Do not rush “be normal now.”

When dangerous:
— Unresponsive stupor to stimuli.
— Suicidal statements.
— Escalating violence.
— Refusing food and water >24 h.


POST-TRAUMATIC STRESS (PTSD-STYLE SYMPTOMS)

May appear weeks to months after trauma.

Signs:
— Flashbacks — vivid intrusive memories.
— Nightmares.
— Avoidance of reminders.
— Hypervigilance, startle, poor sleep.
— Emotional numbness.
— Irritability, anger.

Field care:
— Supportive talk — debrief trauma when safe.
— Regular exercise — strongly helps symptoms in many people.
— Predictable daily structure reduces anxiety.
— Avoid isolation — stay connected to group.
— St. John’s wort: traditionally used for mild low mood — many drug interactions; not a substitute for care.


DEPRESSION IN LONG CRISIS

Losses, hopelessness, chronic fatigue → depression.

Signs:
— Loss of interest, passivity.
— Fatigue despite sleep.
— Sleep too little or too much.
— Appetite loss.
— Guilt, worthlessness.
— Thoughts of death.

How to help:
— Structure: clear daily schedule — avoid endless idle time.
— Small achievable tasks; praise completion.
— Daily physical activity — among the best non-drug supports.
— Social role — person should feel needed.
— Nutrition: B vitamins, vitamin D, omega-3 deficiency worsens mood.
— Suicidal thoughts: never leave alone; reduce access to means (weapons, ropes, large med doses).


SUICIDE RISK

High-risk signs:
— Direct statements (“I want to die”).
— Goodbyes, giving away possessions.
— Sudden calm after deep depression (may indicate decision).
— Prior attempts.

Response:
1. Ask directly: “Are you thinking of hurting yourself?” — asking does not plant the idea; it opens talk.
2. Listen without judgment.
3. Do not leave alone.
4. Remove means when possible.
5. Connect to reasons and people that matter; concrete next steps.


SELF-CARE UNDER CHRONIC STRESS

Chronic stress in crisis is expected. Goal is management, not “feel nothing.”

Basics:
— Sleep: even 5–6 h in relative safety — sleep debt wrecks judgment.
— Food: hunger increases irritability and errors.
— Exercise daily — lowers cortisol.
— Rituals: predictable routines (morning tea, perimeter check) reduce anxiety.
— Talk or journal — externalize fear.

Under acute pressure:
— “Stop — breathe — think — act.”
— Before big decisions in panic: wait 60 s, ask “What matters most right now?”

Group leadership and climate:
— Clear roles reduce anxiety.
— Regular updates (“what we know, what we do next”) cut uncertainty.
— Acknowledge fear — then focus on tasks.
— Celebrate small wins: water found, roof patched — morale fuel.

Children in crisis:
— Regression is common — older kids may act younger.
— Give age-appropriate useful jobs.
— Explain simply — unknown is scarier than hard truth.
— Physical reassurance (hugs) matters.
— Keep meal and sleep routines when possible.
