CHAPTER 1. SHELTER AND A SAFE LOCATION
1.6 Natural shelters: types, construction, site selection

WHEN YOU NEED A NATURAL SHELTER

Leaving the city for the wild is a common crisis pattern. Forests, fields, and mountains do not provide ready walls and roofs. Building shelter from natural materials is a core survival skill. Rule of threes (rough guide): without shelter you can die in ~3 hours in extreme cold or soaking rain; up to ~3 weeks without food; up to ~3 days without water.


SITE SELECTION

What to look for:
— Natural shielding: hillside, large boulders, dense spruce with low branches
— Wind protection: wind can multiply heat loss several times over
— Dry ground: draws and bottoms collect water, cold air, and fog
— Water nearby but not on top of it: ~50–100 m from a stream gives water without mosquitoes right at your door and reduces flood risk
— Tree or rock face for support when building

What to avoid:
— Hilltops and open ground: wind, lightning
— Draws and bottoms: cold air pooling, fog, flooding
— Standing dead timber nearby: fall hazard
— Slopes steeper than ~30°: landslide/avalanche risk
— Heavy animal trails: competition for the spot
— Camps directly under lone tall trees: lightning

“STOP” rule:
Stop, Think, Observe, Plan. Do not build on the first flat spot. Ten minutes of scouting saves hours of rebuilding.


TEMPORARY SHELTER: TARP / TENT FLY

From plastic sheeting, tarp, or any sheet material:
Fastest shelter — about 10–15 minutes.

You need: film/tarp ~2×3 m or larger, ~4–6 m cord, two trees.

A-frame (two-slope):
Tie a ridgeline between two trees at ~1–1.2 m height. Drape the tarp over the line evenly. Stake the lower edges to the ground with pegs or rocks. ~45° wall angle balances rain protection and ventilation.

Lean-to (single slope):
One edge high (~1.5–1.8 m), the other at ground level. Open side faces away from the wind. Simpler and faster than an A-frame.

Pegging without stakes: wrap a tarp corner around a rock or grass bundle and tie cord around the “toggle” — holds well.


BOUGHS AND SPRUCE-BRANCH LEAN-TO

Spruce with dense low branches:
Ready-made shelter. Under a large spruce with branches to the ground stays dry even in heavy rain. Open space under branches, add a bough bed — minimum shelter in ~5 minutes.

Building a bough lean-to:
1. Find two trees ~2–2.5 m apart.
2. Lay a horizontal pole between them at ~1–1.2 m.
3. Lean spruce branches at ~45° from the pole to the ground like ribs on one side.
4. Start from the bottom; overlap each higher row like shingles.
5. Roof thickness at least ~30–40 cm for rain protection.

Ground insulation:
Critical. A ~10–15 cm layer of boughs under you. Dry leaves (oak holds heat better than birch). Fern — soft and insulating. Without insulation, the ground drains heat faster than you can replace it.


LEAN-TO / WIGWAM (POLE SHELTER)

Better for longer stays:

1. Find a straight ridge pole ~3–3.5 m long.
2. Rest one end in a tree crotch or on a forked stake ~1.5 m high.
3. Add side ribs every ~30–40 cm along the main pole.
4. Fill gaps with smaller sticks.
5. Thatch with boughs (from inside, bottom to top — shingle style).
6. Floor bedding ~15–20 cm thick.

Close the ends:
One end fully closed with boughs. The other — entrance with a flap of branches.


DUGOUT SHELTER (PARTIAL EARTH SHELTER)

Among the best natural options for long stays in cold climates:

Site choice:
~10–20° slope for drainage. Clay soil holds shape better than sand. Do not dig in a stream bed or draw.

Build steps:
1. Cut horizontally into the slope to ~1–1.5 m depth. ~1.5×2.5 m is enough for two people.
2. Entrance: short downward passage below floor level — cold air sinks and drains out; warm air stays inside.
3. Roof: cross-poles, branches on top, then soil and sod. Minimum ~20–30 cm earth over the roof.
4. Vent: small hole in the ceiling at the far wall. Without airflow you cannot safely use fire inside.
5. Bed: raised pole frame + boughs.

One person: ~4–6 hours. In winter, interior can hold ~+5…+10 °C without fire, from body heat alone.

Pros:
Hard to see from outside, quiet inside, minimal heat loss, full wind protection.

Cons:
Digging time, flood risk if drainage is poor, needs a vent.


SNOW SHELTERS

Snow is an excellent insulator (dense snow can beat foam board in conductivity).

Snow cave:
1. Find a drift of firm snow at least ~1.5 m high.
2. Dig horizontally, then turn the entrance downward — the entrance should be below the living floor.
3. Interior sized to body plus small margin.
4. Walls at least ~20–30 cm thick.
5. Vent hole in the roof — stick or ski pole.
6. Mark the outside entrance (colored item, branch) — otherwise you cannot find it at night or after new snow.

At ~−30 °C outside, interior can be ~−5…0 °C from body heat alone. With a small stove or candle, ~+5 °C.

Risks:
Condensation wets gear — ventilation is critical. Burial: never leave the roof vent blocked from outside.

Snow trench (quick):
Dig a body-sized trench; cover with boughs or tarp. Less comfortable, ~20–30 min build. Good emergency bivouac.


SHELTER ORIENTATION

Entrance:
Place the opening perpendicular to the prevailing wind or at ~45°, not dead into the wind. In northern latitudes, favor a south-facing wall for solar gain. Avoid a north-facing door in deep winter.

Fire placement:
Outdoor fire — on the windward side of shelter so heat and smoke pass to the side. Indoor fire (only with ventilation): stone reflector behind the fire can aim heat toward the shelter.


LEAVE THE SHELTER IMMEDIATELY IF

— Nearby water is rising
— Smoke smell is increasing (wildfire)
— Large dangerous animals are active nearby (bear, wild boar)
— Trees are cracking in high wind next to the shelter
— Growing sound of rushing water (flash flood, mudslide)
