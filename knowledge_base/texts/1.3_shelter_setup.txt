CHAPTER 1. SHELTER AND A SAFE LOCATION
1.3 Setting up long-term shelter

THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN TEMPORARY AND LONG-TERM SHELTER

Temporary shelter (hours to 1–2 days): wait out danger, minimal setup, ready to leave at any moment.

Long-term shelter (weeks to months): requires planning, organization, and meeting basic needs on site. It is hard to relocate later — choose carefully.

Long-term shelter criteria (in order of importance):
1. Structural reliability over time
2. Access to water (source within ~500 m or stored inside)
3. Safe heating capability (ventilation for any fire)
4. At least two independent exits
5. Concealment from outsiders
6. Space to store supplies


ZONING THE SPACE

Dividing shelter space into functional zones reduces chaos and improves safety.

Sleep zone:
The most protected, quiet spot. Away from entrances and windows. Raised sleeping (30–50 cm off the floor): warmer, fewer insects, less damp. Pallets, boards on bricks, stacked furniture. Blankets/mattresses found in the building as bedding.

Storage zone:
Dry, cool place. Away from the entrance (not the first thing a stranger sees). Organize: food separate from tools, meds separate. Label everything (what, how much, date).

Cooking zone:
Near a window or vent opening — mandatory. Fireproof base (brick, metal). Away from flammable storage.

Observation point:
A window or opening with good approach visibility. Observe from the side of the window, not silhouetted in the center.

Sanitation zone:
As far as possible from sleep and food. Lidded container for a toilet. Disinfectant kept nearby.


HARDENING THE ENTRANCE

Main door:
A metal door beats wood, but any door can be reinforced. Interior bar: a beam wedged under the handle into the floor (chock). Multiple locking points along the door height beat a single lock. Quiet latch: lubricate hinges and hardware.

Early warning:
Simple tripwire with cans across approaches. Glass bottles on windowsills: they fall and make noise. Debris scattered in a hallway: crunch underfoot.

Backup exit:
Mandatory. Window with a rope ladder or rope. Passage to an adjacent space through a found or made opening. Keep the backup route clear and unblocked at all times.


STOCK ORGANIZATION

Rotation principle:
New stock goes to the back; you use from the front (older first). You always know what expires soon.

Inventory:
Notebook or sheet: what you have, quantities, dates. Update after each use. Inventory lets you estimate how long supplies last and when to scavenge again.

Water storage:
Minimum ~14 liters per person (2 L/day × 7 days). Label fill dates on all containers. Split storage across several containers — not one giant vessel.

Food storage:
Closed containers — pest protection. Cool, dark place. Split into an emergency reserve (ER) and “current use.” Do not touch ER while current stocks exist.


HYGIENE AND SANITATION IN SHELTER

Daily routines:
Carry trash and food waste well away from shelter — smells attract animals and people. Ventilate at least 15–20 minutes per day. Keep order — in chaos you lose critical gear and stress rises.

Pest control:
Rats/mice: store food only in sealed containers. Seal floor gaps. Traps near entrances. Insects: fine-weave fabric window screens reduce entry. Dampness = mold: ventilation and plastic sheet flooring.

Toilet:
Lidded container. Cover each use with ash or soil. Empty and bury far from shelter and water sources.


PSYCHOLOGICAL COMFORT

Long-term shelter is not just physical space. Mental state directly affects decisions and survival.

Personal space:
Even in a group, everyone needs “their corner.” Personal items and a small area they control reduces anxiety.

Daily schedule:
Fixed wake, meals, rest, watch duties. Routine creates a sense of normality in abnormal conditions.

Activity:
Idle time in shelter leads to depression faster than fatigue. Maintain physical activity; give people tasks.

Small comforts:
A found book, cards, a clean mug for tea — disproportionately boost morale. Do not ignore small things.


MOVING BETWEEN SHELTERS

When to relocate:
— Discovered or compromised
— Structural condition worsened
— Local resources exhausted within reachable range
— Threat profile changed (new groups, fighting)

Prepare the move:
Scout the next shelter in advance, not mid-flight. Take only essentials — priority: water, food, meds, tools. Move at off-peak times (dawn/dusk — not deep night or midday heat). Remove signs you were there.
