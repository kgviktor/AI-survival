CHAPTER 2. WATER
2.6 Waterborne infections: prevention and care

WATERBORNE DISEASE — A TOP SURVIVAL THREAT

Historically, diarrheal disease in wars and disasters killed more people than combat. Diarrhea + dehydration can kill without medical care in 1–3 days. Main pathogens:

Bacterial:
— Cholera (Vibrio cholerae): “rice-water” stool, rapid dehydration
— Typhoid (Salmonella typhi): high fever, rash, intestinal bleeding
— Dysentery (Shigella): bloody diarrhea, pain, fever
— E. coli O157: bloody diarrhea, kidney risk

Viral:
— Hepatitis A: liver injury, jaundice, fatigue for weeks
— Norovirus: vomiting and diarrhea, 1–3 days, severe dehydration

Protozoal:
— Giardiasis (Giardia): bloating, fatty stools, chronic course
— Cryptosporidiosis: watery diarrhea, prolonged course, chlorine-resistant
— Amebiasis: from asymptomatic to bloody diarrhea and liver abscess


PREVENTION

Three rules:
1. Clean water (treat and boil)
2. Clean hands (soap after toilet and before food)
3. Safe waste disposal (bury/isolate from water)

Also:
— Heat treats most foodborne pathogens
— Do not eat from dirty surfaces
— Flies spread gut infections — cover food
— In outbreaks, avoid sharing cups without washing


WHEN TO SUSPECT WATERBORNE ILLNESS

Timing/symptoms:
— 12–72 hours after drinking suspect water
— Diarrhea (3+ loose stools/day)
— Nausea, vomiting
— Abdominal pain, cramps
— Fever (not always)
— Several people who shared food/water

Red flags — seek professional care if possible:
— Blood in stool
— Fever >39 °C
— No stool but ongoing severe abdominal pain
— Signs of severe dehydration (below)


ASSESSING DEHYDRATION

Mild (~3–5% body weight loss):
Thirst, dark urine, dry mouth, fatigue. Treatment: increase fluid intake.

Moderate (~5–10%):
Very dark urine or none, dry skin, dizziness, fast pulse, irritability. Treatment: oral rehydration solution (ORS).

Severe (>~10%):
Confusion, sunken eyes, skin pinch returns slowly (>~2 s), little urine, weak rapid pulse. Life-threatening — urgent care.

Skin pinch test:
Pinch skin on the back of the hand. Well hydrated: instant flattening. Moderate: 1–2 s. Severe: stays tented 3+ s.


ORAL REHYDRATION THERAPY (ORT)

Foundation of diarrhea dehydration treatment.

Standard WHO-style ORS:
— 1 L clean (boiled) water
— 6 level teaspoons sugar (~18 g)
— 0.5 level teaspoon salt (~2.5 g)

Mnemonic: per liter — “big spoon of sugar, pinch of salt.” Sip small amounts often — ~50–100 mL every few minutes. If vomiting: try 5–10 mL every minute.

How much ORS:
Adult with moderate dehydration: 2–4 L in the first 4–6 hours. After each loose stool: add ~200–300 mL.

Alternatives:
— Rice water: starchy cooking water — approximate ORS if ingredients are limited
— Coconut water: natural electrolytes
— Dilute juice or compote without pulp + pinch of salt

Avoid during diarrhea:
— Large amounts of sugary drinks (can worsen diarrhea)
— Milk (lactose often worsens gut infection)
— Caffeinated tea/coffee (diuretic)
— Alcohol (dehydrating)


DRUG TREATMENT

Antibiotics:
For severe bacterial diarrhea or bloody stool.
— Ciprofloxacin 500 mg twice daily for 3–5 days — broad-spectrum common choice
— Azithromycin 500 mg once daily for 3 days — alternative if resistance suspected
— Metronidazole 500 mg three times daily for 7–10 days — Giardia and amebiasis

Viral infections (norovirus, rotavirus):
Antibiotics do not help. ORS and time (often 1–5 days).

Antimotility drugs:
Loperamide (Imodium) slows gut motility.
— Do NOT use with bloody diarrhea or fever >38.5 °C — risk of serious complications
— May help non-bloody infectious diarrhea: fewer trips, easier to manage

Activated charcoal:
Binds some toxins in poisoning; limited proven benefit in infectious diarrhea.

Probiotics:
Lacto/bifido cultures may speed recovery. Yogurt, kefir — natural sources.


HEPATITIS A

Source: contaminated water and food (especially raw shellfish).
Incubation: ~2–7 weeks.

Symptoms:
Jaundice (yellow skin and eyes), dark urine, pale stools, fatigue, right-upper-quadrant pain.

Treatment:
No specific cure. Rest; avoid alcohol and heavy fat (liver load). Adequate food and fluids. Recovery often 2–6 weeks; full recovery may take months.

Prevention:
Vaccine is best (long protection). Boil water; heat-treat food; handwashing.


GIARDIASIS

Source: contaminated water (even mountain streams).
Symptoms: bloating, fatty stools, alternating diarrhea and normal stools, chronic course.
Treatment: metronidazole 250 mg three times daily for 5–7 days, or tinidazole 2 g once.


TYPHOID FEVER

Symptoms: high fever (39–40 °C) rising over ~a week, abdominal pain, relative bradycardia, rose spots on abdomen.
Treatment: ciprofloxacin 500 mg twice daily for 10–14 days or azithromycin.
Complications: intestinal bleeding or perforation — needs surgery.


CHOLERA

Symptoms: sudden large-volume watery stools (“rice water”), often little abdominal pain, rapid dehydration.
Treatment:
— ORS is the core (severe cases may need many liters in the first hours)
— Doxycycline 300 mg once shortens illness
— Severe vomiting — IV fluids (Ringer’s or normal saline) if available

Cholera can kill in hours from dehydration — ORS first.


MINIMAL KIT FOR WATERBORNE ILLNESS

— Ciprofloxacin 500 mg (e.g., 30 tablets)
— Metronidazole 500 mg (e.g., 20 tablets)
— Loperamide (~20 capsules)
— Commercial ORS packets or sugar + salt for mixing
— Activated charcoal (50+ tablets)
— Thermometer

Storage: airtight, cool, dark. Check expiry yearly.
