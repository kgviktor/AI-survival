CHAPTER 6. SECURITY
6.6 Chemical and radiation threats

PART 1: RADIATION

UNDERSTANDING RADIATION

Types:
— Alpha: weak penetration — stopped by paper or dead skin layer. Dangerous if inhaled or swallowed.
— Beta: moderate penetration — blocked by clothing layers. Skin and internal hazard if ingested.
— Gamma: high penetration — needs mass shielding (lead, concrete, earth, water thickness).
— Neutron: nuclear detonations — very penetrating; specialized shielding.

Units:
— Sievert (Sv) / millisievert (mSv): biological dose.
— Background: often ~0.1–0.3 µSv/h (varies by location).
— Elevated: >~1 µSv/h — investigate.
— Dangerous extended stay: >~10 µSv/h (context-dependent).
— Acute lethal range: roughly >4–5 Sv short exposure (radiation sickness).


INDIRECT SIGNS OF CONTAMINATION

You cannot see or feel ionizing radiation without instruments. Clues:
— Nuclear detonation: flash brighter than sun, then blast, then mushroom cloud.
— Power-plant accident: often invisible; instruments + official alerts.
— “Dirty bomb”: explosion plus unexplained illness clusters downwind.

Dosimeter:
— Consumer meters (many brands/models exist) are relatively affordable — invaluable if you live near nuclear risk.
— Without meter: follow official guidance and cluster symptoms in population.


NUCLEAR DETONATION — IMMEDIATE ACTIONS

If you see the flash (sound delayed = you are not at ground zero):
1. Drop flat immediately; get behind solid cover.
2. Cover head; face down — blast wave next.
3. Wait for blast to pass.
4. If able, move perpendicular to fallout drift (know wind if possible).

Damage zones (very rough — weapon and terrain dependent):
— Total destruction near ground zero.
— Severe damage and fires for many km in large weapons.
— Fallout plume downwind can threaten far downrange.

Evacuation vs shelter:
— Perpendicular to plume drift often cited — not straight into the cloud’s path.
— If far enough, sheltering in heavy building may beat running through fallout.


SHELTER IN PLACE (FALLOUT)

Principle: mass between you and radiation reduces dose.

Good shielding materials:
— Concrete (~30 cm can reduce dose roughly an order of magnitude — thickness matters).
— Brick (~similar scale).
— Earth/berm — very effective per thickness.
— Water — useful if thick enough.

Better places in a building:
— Center of structure (more walls/floors between you and outside).
— Basement (earth shielding).
— Mid floors of high-rise sometimes preferred over ground (roof deposition) or top (gamma from roof materials) — context-specific.

Sealing:
— Tape gaps, vents, windows, doors to reduce dust entry.
— Damp towels at cracks — particle capture.
— Turn off forced ventilation that pulls outside air.

Time:
— First ~24–48 h often worst for short-lived isotopes in fallout.
— Dose rates typically fall over days to weeks as isotopes decay — follow measurements.

Supplies in shelter:
— Water in sealed containers (filled before event).
— Food in sealed packaging.
— Dosimeter if available.
— Potassium iodide (KI): ~130 mg adults, ~65 mg children (exact dosing per official drug insert) — only helps thyroid vs radioactive iodine-131 if taken per timing guidance; not “radiation cure-all.”

Decon after exposure risk:
— Remove outer clothing at threshold; bag it.
— Wash skin and hair thoroughly with soap.
— Clean under nails.

Food/water after fallout:
— Drink sealed water only.
— Avoid open/local produce from contaminated zone until cleared.


ACUTE RADIATION SYNDROME (SIGNS)

Stages:
— Prodrome (hours): nausea, vomiting, weakness — significant dose >~1 Sv acute.
— Latent “improvement” days/weeks.
— Manifest illness: hair loss, bleeding, infections, marrow failure.
— Outcome depends on dose and care — hospital needed for significant exposures.

Field care: supportive (fluids, infection control, pain control) — not a substitute for evacuation.


PART 2: CHEMICAL THREATS

MAJOR CLASSES

Nerve agents (sarin, VX class):
Signs: pinpoint pupils, salivation, twitching, seizures, rapid collapse.
Protection: full respiratory isolation (proper mask/filter). Atropine autoinjector if trained and available.

Blister agents (sulfur mustard class):
Signs: delayed blisters, eye/airway burns.
Protection: skin cover + proper mask.

Choking agents (chlorine, phosgene):
Signs: cough, pulmonary edema; chlorine smells like bleach; phosgene may smell like “cut hay.”
Protection: uphill and upwind; heavy gases pool low — terrain matters. Wet cloth helps some aerosols/chlorine somewhat — not reliable vs nerve agents.

Blood agents (cyanide class):
Signs: rapid collapse, seizures; “bitter almond” smell not reliable (many cannot smell it).
Protection: proper mask; urgent medical care.

Incapacitating agents (e.g. BZ-class hallucinogens in military context):
Signs: confusion, inability to function.

Riot agents (CS, pepper OC):
Signs: burning eyes, tears, cough — usually not lethal at field concentrations.
Care: fresh air, water rinse.


SIGNS OF CHEMICAL RELEASE

— Odd smells (almond, garlic, hay, bleach).
— Oily spots on surfaces.
— Sudden bird/animal die-off.
— People collapsing, seizing, coughing in cluster.
— Small blast + low cloud — possible deliberate release.

If chemical suspected:
1. Hold breath.
2. Leave immediately upwind and uphill when possible.
3. Strip outer clothing once clear.
4. Wash skin and eyes copiously with water.
5. Seek medical care.

Improvised protection:
— Multiple layers of wet cloth over nose/mouth — helps some particulates and coarse aerosols; not reliable vs nerve agents.
— Tight-fitting eye protection helps with irritants.
— Maximize skin cover.

Proper respirators:
— Military/full-face CBRN kits with correct filter types and fit test — beard breaks seal.
— Filters have limited service life — replace per specs or if breakthrough odor.


SHELTERING FROM CHEMICALS

— Tape/seal like radiation shelter concepts.
— Wet towels at cracks add particle barrier.
— Sealed room without air exchange has limited oxygen — small room ~tens of minutes per person is a rough concern; plan ventilation breaks only when safe upwind.


EVERYDAY INDUSTRIAL TOXICS

— Ammonia (refrigeration): pungent; lighter than air in some conditions — low spots vs high varies; follow plume.
— Chlorine (water treatment): heavier than air — higher floors may help; still evacuate per guidance.
— Carbon monoxide: odorless — see chapter on ventilation/CO (4.8).
