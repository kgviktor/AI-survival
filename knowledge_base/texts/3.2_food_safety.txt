CHAPTER 3. FOOD
3.2 Food safety and judging shelf life

WHY THIS MATTERS

Food poisoning without medical care is a serious threat. Salmonella, botulism, staphylococcal toxin can kill. Yet many foods past “best by” dates are still safe. Rule: a date label is not the moment food becomes dangerous — it is often the maker’s quality guarantee cutoff.


DATE LABEL TYPES

“Use by” / “Expires”:
Strictest. Used for items with real post-date risk: milk, meat, fish, ready meals. After the date, risk can rise — still judge by sight and smell.

“Best before”:
Quality date, not safety. Grains, cans, pasta, dried fruit are often safe for years after. Nutrition and taste may fade, not necessarily safety.

“Sell by”:
For the store, not the consumer. Food is often fine for days after.


ASSESSING FOOD WITHOUT LABELS

“VSG” — Visual, Smell, cook thoroughly:

VISUAL:
— Visible mold? Surface mold on bread/cheese — you can sometimes trim. Deep mold — discard.
— Canned goods: swollen lid or sides — BOTULISM RISK. Discard without opening.
— Meat color: beef browning is not always dangerous; green or gray — dangerous.
— Fish: dark gills, cloudy eyes — not fresh.

SMELL:
— Sour/off dairy — spoiled.
— Ammonia or rot on meat — discard.
— Sour smell when opening cans — suspicious; do not eat.
— Rancid oil — oxidized; not usually acutely toxic but unpleasant.

COOK (heat):
— Heat kills most pathogens. Rolling boil 3–5 minutes is strong protection.
— Exception: botulinum toxin breaks down after ~10+ minutes boil, but spores may survive.


SPECIFIC FOODS

Cans (if stored well):
— Acid foods (tomato, fruit): often OK ~12–18 months past date
— Meat/fish: ~2–5 years past date in practice
— Vegetables: ~2–4 years
Danger signs: swollen can, spray on opening, sour smell, foaming.
Botulism: may have no smell; swollen can = discard.

Grains and legumes:
Nearly indefinite if dry. Pests (weevils) — toast on a skillet or rinse; product is usable. Mold from dampness — discard or cut away only if clearly localized and you are desperate.

Flour and pasta:
Flour: ~1–2 years; longer if dry. Check: bugs, mold, sour smell. Dry pasta: ~2–3 years+, often longer if stored well.

Sugar and salt:
Indefinite if dry. May cake — break up.

Honey:
Does not spoil; may crystallize — warm to reliquefy. Ancient honey remained edible.

Vegetable oil:
Unrefined goes rancid faster (~6–12 months). Refined: ~1–2 years. Rancid oil is unpleasant and less nutritious.

Eggs:
Float test: sinks horizontal — fresh (~1–7 days). Sinks but stands up — older (~2–3 weeks) but often usable. Floats — discard. No water: crack into a cup — no foul odor means likely OK.

Cheese:
Hard cheese: surface mold — trim ~2–3 cm around and use the rest. Soft cheese (cottage, ricotta): any mold — discard.

Bread:
Visible mold — discard the whole loaf; hyphae spread deeper than you see. Exception: intentionally mold-ripened cheeses if you know them.

Meat and fish:
Highest risk. If doubtful — do not eat, or boil hard (30+ minutes). Thawed frozen meat: cook soon or discard.


SPECIFIC TOXINS

Botulism:
Most dangerous food toxin. Anaerobic conditions — home-canned goods, improperly dried meat without airflow. Swollen can is the key sign. With home canning, be skeptical. Boiling ~10+ minutes destroys toxin (not necessarily spores).

Aflatoxin (mold on nuts/grain):
Peanuts, nuts, corn with mold may contain aflatoxin — potent toxin/carcinogen. Visible mold on nuts — discard all. Bitter nut — discard.

Solanum alkaloids (nightshades):
Green or sprouted potatoes and green tomatoes. Symptoms: nausea, vomiting, cramps. Cut away all green and sprouted parts with margin.


UNIVERSAL EDIBILITY TEST FOR UNKNOWN PLANTS

If you do not know it — structured test (takes ~8 hours):
1. Touch to lips — burning/numbing = stop
2. Tiny piece on tongue — burning/bitter = stop
3. Chew but do not swallow — burning/bitter = stop
4. Swallow a small amount — wait 8 hours
5. No reaction — eat a small portion
6. Wait another 8 hours — still OK = relatively safer

Never test on an empty stomach — hunger masks symptoms.


USING STOCKS IN ORDER

Principle: eat the most perishable first.

Immediate (0–24 h):
Fresh dairy, meat, fish (when cold chain fails).

Short term (1–7 days):
Cooked dishes, open cans, bread.

Medium term (weeks–months):
Sealed cans, grains, pasta, dried fruit.

Long term (years):
Salt, sugar, honey, white rice, dry legumes.
