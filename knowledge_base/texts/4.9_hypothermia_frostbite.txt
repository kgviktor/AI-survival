CHAPTER 4. HEAT, WARMTH, AND FIRE
4.9 Hypothermia and frostbite: signs and first aid

HYPOTHERMIA

Definition: core body temperature below ~35°C (normal ~36.6°C). Below ~35°C function declines. Near ~30°C — unconsciousness. ~28°C and lower — cardiac arrest risk.

Risk factors:
— Wet clothing (~25× faster heat loss than dry in wind)
— Wind
— Exhaustion
— Alcohol (vasodilation — heat loss; false warmth)
— Low food intake
— Very young and elderly


STAGES OF HYPOTHERMIA

Mild (~35–32°C):
— Violent shivering
— Pale skin
— Fast pulse and breathing
— Clumsy coordination
— Conscious, normal speech
— Often treatable in the field

Moderate (~32–28°C):
— Shivering stops (bad sign — thermoregulation failing)
— Muscle rigidity
— Slow confused speech
— Weak slow pulse
— Sleepiness, apathy
— Paradoxical undressing (~20% of cases — brain dysfunction, feeling “hot”)
— Needs active rewarming

Severe (<~28°C):
— Unconsciousness
— Very slow or absent pulse
— Minimal breathing
— Dilated pupils
— Death without treatment

Rule: “Nobody is dead until warm and dead.” Cold-water victims with no vital signs have been revived after slow rewarming. Do not give up prematurely.


FIRST AID — HYPOTHERMIA

Mild (conscious, shivering):

1. Remove from cold (shelter, out of wind/water).
2. Remove wet clothing — gently, no rough handling.
3. Wrap in dry insulation: blankets, dry clothes, sleeping bag.
4. Warm packs at groin, armpits, neck — large vessels.
5. Warm drink: tea, broth — not alcohol.
6. Caloric food (chocolate, sugar).
7. Horizontal rest — avoid heavy exertion (cold heart is vulnerable to arrhythmia).

Moderate / severe:

Handle gently:
— Do not shake or jostle — cold heart may fibrillate.
— Transport horizontal if possible.
— External rewarming: warm (not hot) ~40°C water or compresses on trunk — not extremes on skin.
— Do not rewarm arms and legs before core — cold blood return can crash core temperature.
— If no pulse: start CPR per training; in some protocols CPR continues during rewarming in hospital.

Do not:
— Rub limbs (damages cold tissue and vessels)
— Give alcohol
— Put severely hypothermic person in hot bath — sudden vasodilation can drop blood pressure and stop the heart
— Force walking “to warm up” in moderate/severe cases


FROSTBITE

Definition: tissue damage from local freezing. Skin below about -0.5°C begins to freeze.

Common sites: fingers, toes, ears, nose, cheeks — peripheral areas with marginal circulation.

Risk factors:
— Tight boots (poor circulation)
— Smoking (vasospasm)
— Wet clothing
— Wind
— Prior frostbite (scarred tissue)
— Alcohol


DEGREES OF FROSTBITE

First degree (superficial):
— Pale or bluish skin
— Numbness, tingling
— On rewarming: redness, burning, swelling
— Tissues survive; full recovery

Second degree:
— Pallor, numbness
— In ~24–48 h: clear blisters
— Painful rewarming
— Healing ~2–4 weeks; possible minor sequelae

Third degree:
— Deep tissue freeze
— After thaw: dark blue or black areas
— Blood-filled blisters
— Numbness
— Tissue death possible; amputation risk

Fourth degree:
— Full-thickness including bone
— Irreversible
— Amputation often required


FIRST AID — FROSTBITE

Do:

1. Remove from cold. Shelter.
2. Remove wet clothing and jewelry (rings — swelling will trap them).
3. Wrap affected parts in dry cloth — do not actively thaw until safe to stay thawed.
4. Rewarm in water ~37–40°C for ~20–40 min. Monitor temperature — not hot.
5. After thaw: loose sterile dressing; separate fingers/toes.
6. Pain meds (ibuprofen, acetaminophen) — rewarming hurts.
7. Warm drinks.

Do not:
— Rub with snow — ice crystals cut cells
— Rub with hands — mechanical injury
— Hold to open fire — numb skin burns easily
— Pop blisters — they protect tissue
— Smoke — vasospasm slows recovery
— Thaw if refreezing likely — thaw–refreeze cycle is far more damaging

“Frozen in the field” rule:
If you must walk out on frostbitten feet, some protocols favor staying frozen until shelter — thawed frostbite is very painful and walking may be impossible. Balance against hypothermia risk and distance.


PREVENTION

Clothing:
— Proper layers (see 4.6)
— Dry feet critical
— Spare socks in pack
— Do not overtighten boots in cold

Activity:
— Move to maintain limb circulation
— Wiggle toes every ~20–30 min in severe cold
— Wiggle ears, blink — monitor sensation

Food:
— Do not go out hungry in deep cold
— Warm drinks help core temperature

Buddy checks:
— Watch partner’s cheeks and ears — white patches appear to others before self-notice.
— Mutual checks every ~30–60 min below about -20°C.
