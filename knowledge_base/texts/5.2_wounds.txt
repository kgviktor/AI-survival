CHAPTER 5. MEDICINE AND FIRST AID
5.2 Wounds: care, bandaging, infection prevention

WOUND TYPES AND GENERAL APPROACH

Incised: clean edges, moderate bleeding — easiest to close.
Lacerated: torn edges, more tissue damage — harder to suture.
Puncture: small entry, deep track — risk of deep injury and anaerobic infection (depth = low oxygen = tetanus risk).
Chop: large tissue loss, may involve bone.
Gunshot: entry ± exit — very high infection risk.
Abrasion: superficial skin loss.
Bite (animal, human): highest infection risk — mouths harbor bacteria.


FIRST PRIORITY: STOP BLEEDING

Arterial (bright red, spurting):
— Tourniquet PROXIMAL on limb immediately.
— Note time applied (on forehead, on paper).
— Max ~2 h on limb (necrosis risk rises).
— No tourniquet — maximal pressure dressing + direct arterial pressure if trained.

Venous (dark red, steady flow):
— Direct pressure through sterile gauze.
— Hold 5–10 min without peeking.
— Elevate limb above heart if practical.

Torso and neck — no tourniquet:
— Tight packing (gauze) into wound.
— Occlusive dressing for penetrating chest wounds (three sides taped, one side open as valve).

Wound packing:
1. Gloves on.
2. Pack tightly with sterile gauze or clean cloth.
3. Firm pressure with fist/body weight ≥3 min.
4. Do not remove packing until higher care — unless protocol says replace.


SECOND PRIORITY: CLEAN THE WOUND

Key to preventing infection.

Step 1 — Pressure irrigation:
— Draw saline or clean water in 20 ml syringe.
— Squirt forcefully into all corners of wound.
— Medium wound may need ~200–500 ml total.
— Goal: wash out dirt, bacteria, foreign material.

Step 2 — Mechanical removal:
— Tweezers/forceps for visible glass, gravel, cloth.
— Do not probe deep blindly — vessels and nerves.
— Remove obvious necrotic tissue (black/gray, foul).

Step 3 — Antiseptic:
— Chlorhexidine 0.05%: irrigate.
— Hydrogen peroxide 3%: one-time debris lift — not repeated (delays healing).
— Iodine: wound edges and skin, not deep flood.
— Alcohol: skin around only — tissue-toxic inside wound.


THIRD PRIORITY: CLOSURE

When to suture/staple:
— Clean incised wounds <~6 h old.
— Edges gaping and will not approximate.

When NOT to close (heal by secondary intention / delayed closure):
— Wounds >~6–8 h old.
— Animal and human bites.
— Heavily contaminated (soil, feces, rust).
— Puncture and gunshot (anaerobic risk).
— Any infection signs.
Keep open, drain if needed, dress frequently.

Closure methods:
1. Sutures: nylon or silk ~2/0–4/0, cutting needle. Simple interrupted: entry ~0.5–1 cm from edge, full dermal bite, symmetric exit. Square knots.
2. Staplers: fast for linear skin lacerations.
3. Adhesive strips (Steri-Strips): small superficial cuts.
4. Medical cyanoacrylate: tiny clean cuts — avoid deep/contaminated.

Improvised “bush thread”: horsehair, boiled household thread (nylon). Needle — boil and/or alcohol wipe.


BANDAGING

Basic dressing:
1. Central sterile gauze pad (2–4 layers) on wound.
2. Roller bandage to hold.
3. Tape outer layer.

Pressure dressing (moderate bleeding):
1. Thick gauze on wound.
2. Wrap snugly with even pressure.
3. Check circulation distal (pulse, color, sensation).

Change frequency:
— Clean healing wound: every ~1–2 days.
— Infected or draining: 2–3× daily.
— If wet — change immediately.

Signs of normal healing:
— Edges approximating, no spreading redness.
— Small serous (clear) drainage.
— No foul odor.

Infection — treat aggressively:
— Increasing redness, heat, swelling.
— Purulent drainage (yellow/green).
— Red streaks toward body (lymphangitis — sepsis risk).
— Fever, chills.
— Bad smell.


INFECTION PREVENTION

— Wash hands before each dressing change.
— Do not touch wound bed bare-handed.
— Do not seal airtight if pus needs to drain.
— Change dressings on schedule.

Antibiotics for wounds — consider when:
— Bites, heavy contamination, gunshot/puncture, early infection signs, immunocompromised.
Example adult pattern (not universal): amoxicillin/clavulanate 875 mg ×2/day ×7 days.
Alternative: ciprofloxacin 500 mg ×2/day + metronidazole 500 mg ×3/day (covers many gut/anaerobe patterns — verify indication).

Tetanus:
High risk: soil, rust, manure, deep punctures. Without vaccine status / immunoglobulin in field — maximal wound cleaning and watch for trismus, rigidity, spasms over ~3–14 days.


SPECIAL SITUATIONS

Scalp wounds:
— Bleed heavily even when small — many vessels.
— Direct pressure usually works.
— Assess consciousness and head injury signs.

Penetrating chest wound:
— Seal with occlusive dressing — three sides taped (flutter valve).
— Without seal — tension pneumothorax risk.
— Semi-sitting, injured side down if tolerated.

Evisceration (gut outside):
— Do not push back in.
— Cover with moist clean cloth.
— Emergency surgical care.

Amputation:
— Tourniquet immediately.
— Severed part — clean bag, keep cool (not frozen) with ice around bag.
— Replant time windows are short — evacuate fast.
