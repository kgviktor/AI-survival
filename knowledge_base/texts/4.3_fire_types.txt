CHAPTER 4. HEAT, WARMTH, AND FIRE
4.3 Fire lays and their uses

CHOOSING A FIRE LAY

No one fire fits every job. Each design fits a goal: cooking, warmth, signaling, stealth, long burn. Right choice saves fuel and time.


TEEPEE / CONE

Build: fuel leaned around tinder in a cone, tip up.

Use: ignition, quick heat, signal fire.

Pros:
— Good draft — burns hot
— Fast heat
— Easy to light — tinder inside, wood outside

Cons:
— Burns fast — constant feeding
— Tall flame, smoke
— Poor for cooking (uneven coals)

Build:
Tinder center. Small sticks cone over tinder. Larger wood outside. Light tinder. As outer wood falls in — add more.


LOG CABIN

Build: fuel stacked square, alternating perpendicular layers like a cabin.

Use: cooking, sustained heat, good coals.

Pros:
— Even coals — ideal cooking
— Long burn
— Pot can sit on top

Cons:
— More wood to build
— Less draft than teepee — harder to start

Build:
Bottom: two logs parallel. Next: two perpendicular. Repeat 3–4 levels. Tinder and kindling inside. Light from bottom.


STAR

Build: 4–6 logs horizontal, ends toward center. Center burns.

Use: long low-maintenance burn, overnight fire.

Pros:
— Very long burn (all night possible)
— Low fuel rate
— Simple
— Adjust heat — push logs in or pull out

Cons:
— Small flame — less radiant heat
— Less convenient for cooking

Use:
Lay star, light center. As inner ends char — push logs in. Good for sleep nearby — coals last long.


LONG FIRE / NORDIC STACK (NÄÄD)

Build: two or three ~20–30 cm logs stacked horizontally. Fire between lower logs.

Use: overnight heating fire, especially winter.

Pros:
— 6–10 h from one load
— Long even heat along log
— Little night tending
— Good to sleep beside

Cons:
— Needs large logs — work to gather
— Not for pot cooking on top

Build:
1. Two logs parallel on ground — fire in gap between them (or under upper log).
2. Light with small wood between.
3. Third log on top — holds heat, slower burn.
Variant: one log horizontal, fire on top, burns along length.


DAKOTA / PIT FIRE

Build: two pits connected by tunnel. Fire in one pit, air inlet in other.

Use: low visibility, cooking in wind, discretion.

Pros:
— Hard to see from distance (heat low, little smoke above ground)
— Strong draft in wind
— Concentrated heat — efficient cooking
— Fewer sparks

Cons:
— Digging work
— Fuel must fit pit

Build:
1. Main pit ~25–30 cm wide, ~30 cm deep.
2. ~30–40 cm upwind — smaller second pit.
3. Connect with tunnel ~15–20 cm below surface.
4. Fire in main pit.
5. Pot over main pit — heat from below.


REFLECTOR FIRE

Build: fire in front of log or rock wall that reflects heat one way.

Use: warming sleep site, maximum useful radiation.

Reflector wall:
3–5 logs vertical, tight. Height ~1–1.2 m, length ~2–2.5 m. Lean slightly toward shelter side.

Effect: heat that would go backward reflects forward — roughly doubles useful warmth in one direction. Sleep between fire and shelter wall, reflector behind you.


SIGNAL FIRE

Goal: visibility, not warmth.

Smoke (day):
Add green leaves, fresh boughs, plastic — dark smoke.
Wet grass, moss — light smoke.
Three fires in triangle — distress pattern.

Flame (night):
Teepee of dry wood — tall bright flame.

Site: high ground, clearing, shore — max visibility.


FIRE SAFETY

Clearances:
Minimum ~3–4 m from trees, brush, dry grass. Minimum ~2 m from shelter — more in wind.

Around fire:
Clear ~1–1.5 m of dry vegetation. Rock or mineral soil better than dry grass.

Extinguishing:
Drown with water — soak coals, stir, soak again. Hand ~5–10 cm above — no heat. Bury and pack with soil. Never leave fire unattended.

Watch:
In strong wind — never leave fire alone.


CHOOSING WOOD

Hard hardwoods (slow, hot, coals):
Oak, ash, birch, hornbeam — best for long burns and coals.

Soft hardwoods (fast burn):
Alder, aspen, poplar — fast, few coals. Quick heat.

Conifers (resin = flame and sparks):
Pine, spruce, larch — easy ignition, sparks, fast burn. Good for starting, not long fires. Soot on pots.

Birch: excellent balance of availability and heat in boreal/temperate forests.

Never burn:
— Elder, cherry (some species) — toxic smoke risk — verify local species
— Fresh-cut conifer — heavy tar, harsh smoke
— Painted or treated wood — toxic fumes
— Particleboard, plywood indoors — formaldehyde and binders
