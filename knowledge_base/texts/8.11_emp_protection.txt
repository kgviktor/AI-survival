CHAPTER 8. ENERGY AND ELECTRONICS
8.11 EMI/EMP: Faraday cage, what helps and what does not

WHAT IS EMI / EMP

Electromagnetic pulse — intense brief EM energy that can damage or destroy electronics.

Sources:

Nuclear HEMP (high-altitude detonation):
— Burst ~30–400 km → wide-area pulse.
— Components: E1 (nanoseconds — hard on microelectronics), E2 (microseconds — lightning-like), E3 (seconds/minutes — long lines and transformers).
— E1 is the main threat to personal electronics.

Geomagnetic storm (CME):
— Solar event → charged particle streams.
— Main risk: grid transformers and long conductors.
— Less direct risk to handhelds if not plugged into mains.
— Carrington-class event would stress modern grids severely.

Non-nuclear EMP / RF weapons:
— Local military/theoretical devices; smaller radius, similar local effects.

Lightning:
— Induces voltage in antennas and wiring.
— Not “EMP” in the strict sense but similarly destructive.


WHAT IS VULNERABLE

High:
— Smartphones, tablets (sub-micron silicon).
— Laptops, PCs.
— Modern vehicles (ECU, injection).
— Chargers, inverters, charge controllers with ICs.
— Radios.
— Anything with connected wires (antenna, Ethernet) — acts as pickup.

Medium:
— Powered-off gear without long conductors at event time.
— Batteries themselves — low risk; chargers — high.

Low:
— Simple electromechanics without microelectronics.
— Older carbureted vehicles without engine electronics.
— Mechanical watches.
— Gear inside effective shielding.


FARADAY CAGE

Principle: closed conductive shell routes external fields around the interior, not through it.

For E1-class threats aim for:
— Continuous conductive enclosure.
— Gaps smaller than ~0.5 mm for high-frequency content.
— Contents insulated from touching metal walls.
— No wires through the shield during the event.

Practical DIY:

Metal trash can with tight metal lid:
— All-metal (not plastic); lid seats well.
— Line inside with cardboard/wood so devices do not touch metal.
— Test: phone inside, call it — no ring = useful attenuation.

Aluminum equipment cases:
— Metal cases with gasket; extra outer foil layers can help.

Wood box lined inside with overlapping foil or fine copper mesh:
— Overlap seams; tape conductive overlap; metal lid with perimeter contact.

Emergency: multiple layers of heavy kitchen foil:
— 3–5 offset layers; not perfect but meaningful attenuation.
— Wrap, then cardboard/cloth so foil does not short device contacts.

Small metal tins (cookies/tea):
— For tiny items; lid must fit snugly.

Vehicle body:
— Partial shielding; gaps at glass/seals — better than nothing, worse than a sealed metal box.


WHAT TO STORE SHIELDED

Priority spares:
— Extra phones; multiband receiver.
— Battery chargers, spare solar controllers, DC-DC converters.
— Multimeter; USB hubs/chargers.
— Compact laptop/tablet backup.
— Fuses, diodes, relays.

Little point shielding:
— Gear that will stay connected to grid/antenna during an event.
— Purely mechanical tools (compass, mechanical watch).


QUICK TESTS

Cellular:
— Phone in container, closed; call from another phone — unreachable = good sign of RF block.

Wi-Fi:
— Inside: no networks visible = stronger shielding.

Improve lid seal:
— Copper tape on rim; positive latches.


REALISTIC THREAT PICTURE

High-altitude nuclear EMP is a rare catastrophic scenario.

More routine:
— Geomagnetic events of varying strength (multiple times per year at solar max).
— Local RF weapons theoretically in conflict.
— Lightning and surges damage gear often without any “EMP.”

Reasonable minimum (not paranoia):
— Shielded storage for critical spares.
— Antenna surge protection.
— Stabilizers/UPS for daily use.
— Spare fuses, diodes, controllers in the shielded box.

Not required:
— Shielding absolutely everything.
— Keeping all daily-use devices in a cage 24/7.
— Building elaborate metal rooms unless you have a specific requirement.
