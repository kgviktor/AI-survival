CHAPTER 5. MEDICINE AND FIRST AID
5.3 Fractures, dislocations, burns — immobilization and care

FRACTURES

Signs:
— Pain worse on loading.
— Swelling, bruising.
— Deformity.
— Abnormal motion at site (do not test for crepitus on purpose).
— Loss of function.

Open fracture (bone through wound or wound at fracture site):
— Very high osteomyelitis risk.
— Irrigate wound, cover sterile.
— Do not reduce bone in the field.
— Antibiotics essential.

Field principle:
“Splint them as you find them.” Do not straighten without imaging and proper analgesia.


IMMOBILIZATION (SPLINTING)

Goal: immobilize joints ABOVE and BELOW the injury.

Materials:
— Boards, branches, ski poles.
— Folded magazines/cardboard.
— Rolled clothing/blanket (soft splint).
— Inflatable splints if carried.

Rules:
1. Analgesia first (ibuprofen, ketorolac if available).
2. Check distal pulse BEFORE and AFTER splinting.
3. Padding under rigid splints — protect skin.
4. Secure above and below fracture.
5. Recheck pulse, sensation, color every ~30 min.
6. Slight elevation reduces swelling.

Humerus:
— Splint armpit to elbow + sling.
— Elbow ~90°, arm in sling around neck.

Forearm:
— Splint palm to elbow, elbow flexed, sling.

Femur:
— Severe — may lose ~1.5–2 L blood into tissues.
— Long splint groin to heel; second splint inner thigh to heel.
— Traction splint if trained and available for shaft fractures.

Lower leg:
— Splints both sides knee to foot.

Clavicle:
— Figure-8 wrapping both shoulders retracting scapulae, or sling supporting arm.

Ribs:
— Do NOT wrap tight — restricts breathing → pneumonia risk.
— Analgesia (ibuprofen 400–600 mg q6h if safe).
— Semi-sitting, lie on injured side for comfort.
— Multiple ribs with flail segment (paradoxical chest wall) — urgent care.


SPINE

— Minimize movement.
— C-collar: rolled clothes or cardboard.
— Log-roll carry with several rescuers on one axis.
— Paralysis or numbness — treat as unstable spine until ruled out.


PELVIS

— Massive blood loss possible (up to several liters in worst fractures).
— Compression belt at level of greater trochanters (belts, sheet).
— Supine, legs slightly flexed and adducted.


DISLOCATIONS

Signs:
— Severe pain, deformity, no normal motion.
— “Springy” block if tried.

Rule:
Reduce only if you are sure it is dislocation, not fracture. If doubt — splint as-is and evacuate.

Shoulder (most common):
— Many reduction techniques exist — slow traction methods; Stimson: prone, arm hangs with small weight at wrist to fatigue muscles.
— After reduction — sling ~2–3 weeks.

Finger:
— Traction along digit with pressure at base.
— Buddy-tape to neighbor finger.

Jaw (TMJ):
— Thumbs on lower molars (wrapped), press down and back — trained technique only.

Hip:
— Hard to reduce without help and strong analgesia — specialist methods.


BURNS

Degrees:

First (superficial):
— Red, painful, intact skin.
— Heals ~3–5 days.

Second (partial thickness):
— Blisters, severe pain.
— Superficial wet blisters — heal ~2–3 weeks.
— Deep pale mottled — less pain, slower heal.

Third (full thickness):
— White, charred, leathery — nerve damage may reduce pain.
— Needs grafting in hospital care.

“Rule of nines” (adult rough %):
Head 9%, each arm 9%, front torso 18%, back 18%, each leg 18%, groin 1% — verify pediatric charts separately.

Critical: >~20% body surface deep partial/full thickness — high mortality without ICU-level fluid resuscitation.


First aid — burns:

1. Cooling:
— Cool (not ice-cold) running water ~15–20 min.
— Start ASAP — useful up to ~30 min post burn.
— No ice on large burns (vasoconstriction injury).
— No butter, oil, sour cream — bacterial growth medium.

2. Clothing/jewelry:
— Cut away clothing around burn; do not peel if stuck.
— Remove rings and bracelets early — swelling follows.

3. Cover:
— Clean moist non-adherent dressing if possible.
— Hydrogel dressings ideal when available.
— No circumferential tight wraps.

4. Blisters:
— Do not pop — barrier to infection.
— If ruptured — treat as open wound.

5. Pain:
— Ibuprofen 400–600 mg or acetaminophen 500–1000 mg.

Field ongoing care:

Superficial partial-thickness I–II:
— Regular moist dressing changes (~1–2 days).
— Chlorhexidine at changes if available.
— Consider antibiotic prophylaxis for large II-degree.
— Heal ~2–3 weeks.

Large deep II / III:
— Fluid resuscitation (Parkland-style estimate): 4 ml × kg × %TBSA burned = ml for first 24 h; half in first 8 h from burn time — IV in hospital; by mouth only if no IV and conscious — aggressive small frequent sips of ORS/water with medical guidance.
— Antibiotics per severity and infection signs.
— Dress changes in warm setting — hypothermia kills burn patients.

Chemical burns:
— Flush water 20–30 min.
— Acid: weak baking soda solution after thorough rinse (controversial — priority is water).
— Alkali: weak vinegar after water (again, water first).
— Dry lime: brush off dry first, then water.

Airway burns:
— Facial burn + singed brows/lashes + hoarse voice + stridor.
— Swelling can close airway in hours.
— Hospital intubation if available.
— Field: semi-sitting, humid/cool air, urgent evacuation.
