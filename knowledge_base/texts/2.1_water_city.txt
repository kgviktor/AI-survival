CHAPTER 2. WATER
2.1 Water sources in the city

WATER PRIORITY

Rule of threes: without water a person dies in about 3 days. With exertion and heat — faster. Water is the first priority after shelter.

Daily needs:
— Bare survival minimum: 1 liter (drinking only, no cooking or hygiene)
— Comfortable minimum: 2–3 L (drinking + minimal hygiene)
— With cooking and sanitation: 4–6 L
— Physical labor in heat: up to 8–10 L

Dehydration signs: dark urine (normal is pale yellow), headache, dizziness, poor concentration. Dark urine means you are already dehydrated — drink immediately.


MUNICIPAL WATER AFTER SHUTOFF

Residual pressure in pipes:
After the supply is cut, water remains in the lines. In a multi-story building that can be hundreds of liters. Open a tap on the lowest floor — water will drain by gravity.

Water heaters and boilers:
A tank water heater (50–200 L) is a large reserve of relatively clean water. Open the drain cock at the bottom. Water inside is often suitable for drinking — it was hot and effectively pasteurized.

Toilet tanks:
The tank usually holds 6–12 L. Water in the tank — not the bowl — is clean potable water. Collect from every apartment in the building: many floors × many apartments = hundreds of liters.

Heating systems:
Water in radiators and heating pipes is not drinking water. It contains corrosion inhibitors, rust, and sometimes antifreeze. Do not drink without serious multi-stage treatment.

Fire systems:
Fire hydrants and tanks in large buildings. Water is non-potable — needs treatment.


NATURAL SOURCES IN THE CITY

Rainwater:
Often the cleanest urban source. Collect from clean surfaces: plastic pallets, film, clean metal sheets. Do not collect the first 5–10 minutes of rain: it washes contaminants off the roof. Rain from tile roofs is better than from bitumen. Rig: film as a funnel into a container. Store in closed containers; use within 24–48 hours or boil.

Snow:
Clean snow from uncontaminated surfaces is a good source. Do not collect yellow or gray snow or roadside snow (salt, heavy metals). Snow-to-water ratio is roughly 10:1 (10 L snow ≈ 1 L water). Melt gradually — do not eat snow directly (hypothermia, high energy cost).

Urban rivers and streams:
High risk of fuel, sewage, and chemical contamination. Requires full multi-stage treatment. Use only if no better option. Take water upstream of industry and dumps.

Groundwater:
Some cities have springs and shallow groundwater outcrops. Quality depends on depth and distance from pollution. Deep (artesian) water is usually much cleaner than surface water.


UNUSUAL URBAN SOURCES

Water coolers:
Offices, schools, hospitals — large bottles of clean water. A 19 L bottle = about 7–10 days minimum supply for one person.

Pools:
Chlorinated — that helps. Volume 50–80 thousand liters — huge reserve. If stagnant for long, algae grow — needs additional disinfection.

Aquariums:
Small volume but relatively clean water. Boil before drinking.

Air conditioning:
Condensate from AC units is distilled water. A drip tray can collect 1–3 L per day in hot weather. Relatively clean; minimal treatment needed.

Basements and mechanical floors:
Technical tanks — check every building. Often 1–5 thousand liters of non-potable or technical water.


ASSESSING WATER QUALITY WITHOUT TEST KITS

Visual:
Clear is better than cloudy. Cloudiness does not always mean danger; clarity does not guarantee safety. Oil film on the surface — petroleum hazard. Foam that does not dissipate — organic or chemical contamination.

Smell:
Chlorine smell — treated, relatively safer. Rotten-egg (hydrogen sulfide) — may be natural springs or bacterial growth. Chemical smell (gasoline, solvent) — do not drink.

Taste:
Metallic — rust or heavy metals. Bitter — possible alkali or chemicals. Soapy — alkaline. Salty — dissolved minerals (may be OK) or seawater/brackish water (do not drink in quantity).

Safety rule:
If in doubt — treat and boil. Illness from bad water can kill faster than thirst.


SOURCE PRIORITY (best to worst)

1. Bottled water (no treatment needed)
2. Water heaters and boilers (clean, was heated)
3. Toilet tanks (municipal supply water)
4. Cooler bottles (clean)
5. Rainwater (collected correctly)
6. Melted snow (from clean surfaces)
7. Pools (needs disinfection)
8. Urban rivers (high risk, full treatment)
9. Puddles and surface pools (last resort)


WATER RECONNAISSANCE WHEN ENTERING A NEW BUILDING

First 5 minutes in any building:
— Open taps on the lowest floor — is there water?
— Find water heater/boiler — how many liters?
— Count toilet tanks × ~10 L
— Check basement for technical tanks
— Look for office water coolers

Keep inventory: water stock for X people = X days at Y L/day.
