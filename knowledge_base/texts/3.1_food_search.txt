CHAPTER 3. FOOD
3.1 Finding food: shops, warehouses, apartments

FOOD PRIORITIES

Rule of threes: without food a person can survive up to about 3 weeks. But after only 3–5 days of fasting, decision-making and endurance drop sharply. Food is the third priority after shelter and water.

Survival calorie minimums:
— At rest: 1,200–1,500 kcal/day
— Moderate activity: 2,000–2,500 kcal/day
— Heavy labor: 3,000–4,000 kcal/day

In the first days of a crisis you may run on 1,000–1,500 kcal by reducing activity. Long term that leads to depletion — plan resupply.


SAFETY WHILE FORAGING

Always scout before entry:
— Observe outside for 5–10 minutes
— Signs of other people inside
— Alternate exits
— Do not go alone if you can avoid it

Gear: flashlight (dark stores are dangerous), pack for carry, knife/tool.

Timing: early morning often has the least human activity in a crisis.


SHOPS AND SUPERMARKETS

What lasts after looting:
Grocery stores are often stripped in the first 24–72 hours. Not everything goes — look for:
— Back stock and storerooms (not the sales floor)
— Non-food aisles — food may be overlooked nearby
— Hard-to-reach shelves: top shelves, corners, under racks
— Technical areas: cold rooms (food lasts longer there)

What people take last (often remains):
— Specialty/diet foods (gluten-free, diabetic)
— Spices and seasonings (high value, often ignored)
— Vegetable oils (calorie-dense, long shelf life)
— Salt, baking soda, vinegar (preservation)
— Pet food (edible, rich in protein and fat)
— Alcohol (not food, but barter and disinfectant)
— Flour and grains on distant shelves
— Baby food in glass jars

What goes first — do not waste time:
Bread, milk, fresh produce and meat — gone in hours.

Cold rooms:
After power loss, product in a sealed cold room lasts: ~4–6 hours with the door open, ~24–48 hours closed. Below 0 °C outside, the room can act like a freezer.


WAREHOUSES AND WHOLESALE

Often better than retail:
— Large volumes
— Less known to casual looters
— Original packaging — easier to check dates

Where to look:
— Industrial zones on city edges
— Behind large malls (stock often in back or basement)
— Former factories — sometimes material stockpiles

Valuable non-grocery warehouses:
— Construction: film, foam, fabrics (insulation)
— Medical supply: drugs, dressings
— Restaurant supply: bulk oil, spices, grains
— Civil-defense / emergency stocks (if found): long-shelf-life cans


APARTMENTS AND HOUSES

Often underrated:
In evacuations people grab documents, cash, valuables — food is often left.

Search first:
— Pantries and overhead storage: cans, grains, sugar, salt
— Freezers: frozen food (if power is out, judge freshness)
— Under-sink cabinets: common stash spots
— Balconies: potatoes, onions, preserves

Found in most homes:
— Cooking oil
— Salt, sugar, spices
— Grains (buckwheat, rice, millet)
— Canned meat/fish/pâté
— Pasta
— Tea, coffee
— Alcohol

Ethics and practice:
Clearly long-empty homes — take what you need. Leave a note (common crisis etiquette). Do not take more than you can carry and use.


INSTITUTIONS AND SPECIAL SITES

Schools and daycares:
Often weeks of food stock. Kitchens and storerooms first.

Hospitals:
Patient food stocks; also meds and dressings.

Military / emergency bases:
Long-storage cans in bulk — risk: guards and other seekers.

Restaurants and cafés:
Bulk oil, spices, grains; large institutional cans.

Farms on outskirts:
Grain, vegetables in storage. Livestock operations — animals, poultry, eggs.


CARRY AND PRIORITIZATION

Take first by calories per weight:
1. Vegetable oil: ~900 kcal/100 g, stores well
2. Hardtack/crackers: ~400 kcal/100 g
3. Nuts: ~600–700 kcal/100 g
4. Grains and legumes: ~300–370 kcal/100 g
5. Sugar: ~400 kcal/100 g
6. Canned meat/fish: ~150–300 kcal/100 g
7. Chocolate: ~500–550 kcal/100 g

Weight matters:
On foot, ~15–25 kg realistic. For one person ~2,000 kcal/day for 3 days you need ~1.5–2 kg dry food or ~4–5 kg cans. Prioritize calories and shelf life over taste.

Do not forget:
— Knife / can opener
— Water container
— Pot or any metal vessel for cooking
— Salt (makes almost anything palatable)
— Lighters / matches
