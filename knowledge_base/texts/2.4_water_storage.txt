CHAPTER 2. WATER
2.4 Storing and transporting water

HOW MUCH TO STORE

Minimum planning stock:
— One person: 14 L = 7 days × 2 L/day
— Two people: 28 L
— Family of four: 56 L minimum
— With cooking and hygiene: multiply by 2–3

30-day stock (one person):
— Drinking: 60 L
— With cooking: 90–120 L
— With full hygiene: 150–180 L

Practical advice: start at 14 L and grow the stock. Storing >200–300 L in living space is logistically hard.


CONTAINERS

PET plastic bottles:
Best common option. Resin code “1” (PET) — OK for water. “2” (HDPE), “4” (LDPE), “5” (PP) — OK. “3” (PVC), “6” (PS), “7” — avoid for drinking water. Convenient sizes: 1.5–5 L for carry and use. Large bottles (19 L) — harder to move, better volume efficiency.

Glass:
Chemically better than plastic — minimal leaching. Downsides: heavy, breaks, transmits light. Dark glass beats clear for long storage. Keep out of direct sun.

Metal:
Stainless steel is ideal — durable, inert. Galvanized and aluminum are poor for long storage (leaching). Stainless water cans are a good choice.

Food-grade plastic drums:
50–200 L with lids for bulk storage. Must be food-grade plastic. Dark color slows algae growth.

Avoid:
— Chemical, oil, or fuel cans (even if washed)
— Clear containers in bright light (algae)
— Rusty metal interiors
— Cracked or odd-smelling plastics


CONTAINER PREP

New containers:
Wash with soap, then rinse with ~1 tsp bleach per liter of water. Wait 2 minutes, rinse with clean water, dry.

Used containers:
Hot soapy wash to remove residues and odors. Bleach rinse as above. If smell persists — do not use for drinking.

Juice/milk jugs:
Milk jugs are hard to decontaminate from organics — avoid. PET juice bottles — OK after wash and disinfection.


STORAGE CONDITIONS

Temperature:
Cool is best — ideally ~10–15 °C. Prevent freezing (containers burst; thaw may contaminate). Avoid heat (>30 °C) — faster plastic leaching and bacterial growth.

Light:
Dark storage is ideal. Light encourages algae and plastic breakdown. Wrap clear bottles in film or cloth.

Ventilation:
Space should breathe; musty smell means problems.

Isolation:
Do not store next to chemicals, fuel, or paint — plastics absorb vapors.
Do not store on a basement floor that may flood.


SHELF LIFE

Chlorinated tap water in a sealed commercial container:
— Commercial packaging: 1–2 years
— Home-filled: ~6 months under good conditions

Home-treated water:
— Up to ~6 months in a clean, disinfected, sealed container

Natural/rainwater:
— Even after treatment — aim for ≤3 months
— Periodically check smell and clarity

Refreshing stock:
Every ~6 months: use old water for non-drinking tasks, refill fresh. Or add chlorine (~2 drops/L) and update the date.

Spoilage signs:
— New cloudiness
— Odor (except faint chlorine)
— Green or brown film on walls
— Slime on the bottom

What to do with spoiled water:
Do not waste it — use for non-potable needs. For drinking: boil; if heavily contaminated, discard.


TRANSPORTING WATER

Carry containers:
Hydration bladder in pack: 2–3 L on the move. 5 L cans — good volume/weight balance (5 kg). Large 20+ L: base camp, not hiking. 1 L water = 1 kg — plan loads.

Moving large amounts:
Cart, stroller, sled — huge help. Yoke: two buckets on a pole across shoulders. Drag sheet: plastic with load, pull along ground. Bike with panniers or trailer.

Minimize losses:
Lids must seal — check before leaving. Soft bladders pack smaller when empty.

On the move:
Sip regularly; do not wait for thirst. Thirst means dehydration has started. Hard work in heat: ~0.5 L every 30 minutes.


SIMPLE RAIN CATCHMENT

Basic system:
Sloped surface (metal sheet, film) → gutter → tank. 1 m² catch × 10 mm rain (≈1 L/m²) ≈ 10 L gross (minus evaporation ~70–80%). ~50 mm/month rain on 4 m² ≈ ~160 L/month net-ish.

Optimization:
Metal/plastic roofs beat bitumen. Divert first flush (first 5–10 min). Closed tank with lid reduces contamination and evaporation.

Winter:
Collect snow in containers. Dark vessels in sun melt faster; or melt near heat.
