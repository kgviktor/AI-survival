CHAPTER 3. FOOD
3.7 Fishing without tackle: improvised gear, spears, traps

FISH AS A SURVIVAL RESOURCE

Fish is high-quality protein and fat, available in most waters. Without store-bought tackle, fishing is still possible — dozens of methods from primitive to complex.

Where to find fish:
— Under overhanging banks (shade, cover)
— By fallen trees and snags in water
— Shallows early morning and evening
— Edge of current and slack water
— Pools — deeper holes


ROD FROM SCRAP MATERIALS

Rod:
Straight springy branch 1.5–2.5 m. Willow, hazel — flexible and strong. Bamboo if available.

Line:
Nylon thread from pack, clothing. Synthetic rope (unlay to thin strands). Several strands twisted. Thin wire 0.3–0.5 mm. Animal sinew (soak, dry, twist).

Hook:
Safety pin bent to hook shape. Wire piece bent and sharpened. Bird or fish bone sharpened both ends, tied at middle (gorge — swallowed and jams crosswise). Large needle bent. Several hawthorn/blackthorn thorns lashed together.

Sinker:
Stone tied above hook. Nut, bolt.

Float:
Tree bark. Bird feather. Foam scrap. Cork.

Bait:
Earthworms (best general bait). Larvae and insects. Meat scraps, fish guts. Grasshoppers and other insects. Bread rolled into a ball.


FISH SPEAR (GIG)

For shallow water (depth to ~60–80 cm) in clear water.

Build:
1. Straight pole 2–2.5 m from hard wood.
2. Split end lengthwise into 4 tines ~20–25 cm.
3. Wedge a small cross-stick in the split to spread tines.
4. Fire-harden and sharpen tines.

Use:
— Stand still, do not cast shadow on water
— Look through the water, not at the surface
— Refraction: fish looks closer to surface than it is — aim slightly below the apparent position
— Strike fast, without a big wind-up


FISH TRAPS

Woven funnel trap:
Cone basket from flexible withes. Principle: fish enters through narrow throat inside, cannot exit. Size: diameter 30–50 cm, length 60–90 cm.

Weave:
1. Tie several rods (8–12) at one end — base.
2. Spread rods like wheel spokes.
3. Weave with flexible cross withes (willow, vine) like a basket.
4. Entrance: extra withes forming inward-tapering cone.
5. Weave the bottom (tail) closed.

Set:
On bottom with bait inside (fish guts, bread). Pin with stake or stones. Throat faces upstream. Check every 12–24 hours.

Stone weir (V-dam):
On shallow rocky stream. Build V of stones across current with narrow gap. In gap — funnel trap or net. Fish moving with current enter trap. Labor-heavy but works continuously.

Tide pool (coast):
At low tide water recedes — fish trapped in pools. Hand or spear.


NET FROM SCRAP

For a crude net you need:
— Thin cord or threads (unlay rope, tear cloth to strips)
— Time: 4–8 hours

Fisherman’s mesh:
Two horizontal lines tensioned. Tie verticals at crossings with sheet bend / square knot. Mesh 2–3 cm for most fish. Weight bottom edge (stones), float top (corks, bark).

Set:
Across small creek or channel. Do not leave unattended more than 2–3 hours — tangled fish die.


ELECTRIC SHOCK (EXTREME ONLY)

If you have a battery and wires:
Two conductors in water + current source. Stuns fish in ~1–3 m radius. Dangerous: risk to the person. Illegal almost everywhere — in catastrophe only, and still hazardous.


DRESSING FISH

Scales: scrape tail toward head. Knife, spoon, coin.

Gutting:
1. Cut from vent toward head along belly.
2. Remove entrails.
3. Remove gall bladder (green) carefully — if burst, meat turns bitter.
4. Rinse in clean water.

Head:
Fat and cheek meat. Fish eyes are nutritious. Boiled head — good broth.

Bones:
Small fish — thin bones soften in long boil (40+ min). Large fish — eat carefully.

Cooking:
— Freshwater fish may carry parasites (varies by region — e.g. liver flukes, broad tapeworm in some areas)
— Always boil or fry until done (opaque white flesh)
— Minimum ~70°C in thickest part
— Raw dried/salted fish (“stroganina” style) — parasite risk
