CHAPTER 4. HEAT, WARMTH, AND FIRE
4.7 Winter survival: tactical notes

WINTER IS A DIFFERENT GAME

Every mistake costs more. Wet = hypothermia. Lose fire = crisis. Get lost = lethal. With sound tactics winter also offers advantages: snow as building material, cold preserves food, fewer insects and some hazards.


DAILY ROUTINE IN WINTER

Plan energy. Winter needs more calories; work generates heat but also exhaustion. Balance activity and rest.

Wake-up:
Have fire going before leaving sleep system. Coldest often at dawn. Warm shelter first, then tasks.

Activity:
Heavy work (wood, building) in mid-day warmth. If you start sweating hard — slow down; sweat freezes at rest.

Evening:
Load stove/fire for night. Dry clothes and boots before sleep — damp gear is ice by morning.

Night:
Sleep in clean dry clothes (not work clothes — they are damp). Keep feet dry and warm. Hat on. Fire watch if no sealed stove with chimney.


WATER IN WINTER

Snow is not water:
Melting snow in your mouth costs more body heat than you gain in water. Eat snow only if no alternative and you are not hypothermic. Melt snow and ice at the heat source.

Getting water:
— Streams often have open leads under ice
— Lake ice ~20–40 cm — break with bar or pointed rock
— Collect snow in container; melt at fire/stove
— Dark object in sun melts surface snow faster

Storing water:
Freezes — keep near body or heat. Store bottle inverted outdoors: ice forms at “top,” mouth stays liquid longer. Metal bottle inside clothing — less likely to freeze solid.


WINTER FIREWOOD

Standing dead:
Best winter fuel — died standing; frost removes residual moisture. Knock trunk — dull thud may mean rot; ringing sound — sounder wood.

Splitting frozen wood:
Often splits easier than summer. Use metal wedge or hard wood wedge; strike edge, not center.

Daily volume:
Rough guide: one night in heated shelter ~15–25 kg wood. Full day with cooking and heat ~30–50 kg. Labor-heavy — plan wood as a daily priority.


TRAVEL IN WINTER

Improvised snowshoes:
Flexible frames (willow, hazel) + cord or strap lacing. ~60–70 cm long, ~25–30 cm wide — reduces sink ~3–4×.

Skis:
Boards, plywood sheets, oversized snowshoes. Strap bindings. Slower than real skis — still better than post-holing.

Navigation in snowstorm:
Storms hide tracks and landmarks. Do not wander far without return plan. Mark trail every ~50–100 m (branches, cloth ties).

Speed and distance:
Deep snow ~1–2 km/h vs ~4–5 km/h summer walking. Plan legs realistically. Frozen rivers/lakes can be fast travel — if ice is safe.

Ice safety on foot:
Solo ~7+ cm clear hard ice often cited as minimum for walking; groups need more. Probe with stick every ~5–10 steps on unknown ice. Danger signs: dark gray ice, layered ice, weak ice near inlets/outlets, spring thaw.


NUTRITION IN WINTER

Calories:
At ~-20°C with moderate work ~3000–4000 kcal/day vs ~2000–2500 summer.

Fats first:
Fats yield more metabolic heat per gram than carbs. Traditional cold diets: fatty meat. Add fats (oil, tallow) to meals.

Hot food:
Hot meals and drinks warm core. Tea, broth, hot porridge — prioritize morning and evening. Cold snacks possible but hot anchor meals matter.

Do not rely on eating snow for hydration:
Melting snow internally costs heat — net loss to thermoregulation.


WINTER HAZARDS

Avalanches:
Mountains and steep slopes. Risk signs: fresh slab on slope >~30°, “whumph” sounds, cracks. Travel ridges, not under loaded slopes. If caught: protect face/airway, fight for space at surface, stay calm.

Hidden voids under snow:
Pits, deadfall, streams under deep snow — probe ahead with pole in suspect terrain.

Snow blindness:
UV off snow burns corneas. Dark glasses or slit cardboard/birch visor. Symptoms: burning, tears, vision loss hours after exposure. Treatment: dark rest, cool compresses.

CO₂ in snow shelters:
Sealed snow cave — CO₂ from breathing builds up. Always maintain ventilation hole.


PSYCHOLOGY OF WINTER

Short days:
Align hard work with daylight. Long dark — rest, sleep. Candle or lamp helps morale.

Monotony:
White landscape and routine sap motivation. Set concrete daily goals. Group talk matters.

Heat as morale:
Warm shelter and hot food disproportionately boost winter morale. Do not “save” heat at the cost of hope and judgment.
