CHAPTER 1. SHELTER AND A SAFE LOCATION
1.7 Fortified structures and perimeter protection

WHEN FORTIFICATION IS NEEDED

Camouflage and concealment are the first layer of defense. If you are discovered or contact risk is high, you need a second layer: physical barriers. Fortification does two jobs: it slows entry (buying reaction time) and creates positions to observe and respond.

Threat assessment before fortifying:
Low threat (stray looters, random passersby): door barricade and a noisy perimeter are often enough. High threat (organized armed groups): serious hardening and multiple defensive layers. Do not waste scarce resources on fortification when threat is low — invest in supplies and scouting instead.


HARDENING DOORS

Doors:
A wooden door can be kicked in at the hinges and lock area. Hinge reinforcement: steel strap across the hinge on both faces, long bolts through the door. Lock-area reinforcement: ~15×30 cm steel plate over the lock, bolted through. Diagonal bar: vertical wooden beam, bottom wedged to the floor, top under the door handle — blocks opening and needs no tools.

Interior barricade:
Furniture pushed tight against the door adds mass to shift. Wardrobes beat sofas for weight. Tilted furniture braces better than perfectly vertical. Tie multiple pieces together with cord — one combined mass.

Metal doors:
Better than wood, but hinges and the cylinder hole remain weak. Hinges can be pinned with welded rods from inside.

Exterior approach obstacles:
Prevent a running ram. Large objects (containers, vehicles) across a driveway. Junk piles that cannot be stepped over quietly.


HARDENING WINDOWS

Wooden grilles:
~5×10 cm timbers vertically on ~15 cm centers, mortised/nailed into the opening. Outside mounting is harder (no backstop); inside mount to frame or adjacent wall. Diagonal (diamond) pattern is stronger than vertical-only.

Steel bars:
Rebar ≥12 mm welded into the frame. Field expedient: bundle rebars with wire into a grid, bolt to the window frame.

Sandbags at windows:
~30 cm of sand stops many small-arms projectiles. In survival conditions, use any bags filled with soil, gravel, or tightly packed damp leaves. Stack low and to the sides — cover for someone observing from inside.

Furniture barriers:
Tipped wardrobes and fridges at windows slow entry and provide cover.


BUILDING A PERIMETER

Why an outer ring matters:
An outer perimeter warns before a threat reaches the building. That time lets you man positions, wake people, or pack for evacuation.

Alarm perimeter:
Tier 1 (~30–50 m from entrance): tripwire with cans ~25 cm off the ground; multiple rows. Bells, metal pendants. Tier 2 (~10–20 m): gravel or glass in pinch points.

Physical fence:
Materials: sheet metal, corrugated panels, boards, rebar stakes, barbed wire. Height: ~2 m — hard to climb without obvious effort. ~1.5 m slows but may not stop. Inward-leaning stakes on top strongly discourage climbing.

Trenches and pits:
A ~1 m wide, ~0.8 m deep ditch is hard to jump in the dark. Spike pits: build only if facing lethal armed threat — they are deadly traps. Mark them clearly for your own people.

Clear field of view:
Strip cover outside the fence: bushes, junk, cars. Open ground makes attack less attractive.


DEFENSIVE POSITIONS INSIDE

Defense in depth:
Multiple lines — perimeter, building entry, specific floor, hardened room. Each line buys time to fall back.

Hardened room:
Last-ditch hold point. One door, reinforced as much as possible. Water, food, and med kit inside. Comms (if any) and evacuation plan.

Observation / limited fields of fire:
Loophole: small opening in wall, door, or metal sheet — see and respond without full exposure. ~15×10 cm is enough to observe. Cover: sandbags or heavy furniture in front of observation points.

Dead zones:
Walk the building for blind spots where approaches cannot be seen. Ensure every approach is visible from at least one post. Mirrors can see around corners.


MATERIALS FOR FORTIFICATION

Best materials by availability:

Earth/sand bags:
Most effective and accessible. Soil from any ground. Any bag: trash bags, pillowcases, backpacks. ~30 cm thickness — meaningful ballistic cover. ~50–60 cm — better fragmentation protection.

Brick and blocks:
One brick thickness (~12 cm) is weak against bullets. Two bricks (~25 cm) with mortar is serious. Without mortar, walls are quick to build but easy to dismantle.

Metal:
Sheet metal, doors, car hoods — slow but do not fully stop bullets. Good as an extra layer over earth fill. Filled drums or containers are mobile barriers.

Wood:
Weaker than brick or metal. Logs ≥20 cm diameter are substantial cover. ~5 cm boards slow but do not stop.

Concrete:
If you can mix cement and sand, concrete blocks outperform plain brick. Unreinforced concrete is brittle against impact.


MANAGING THE FORTIFICATION

Watch rotation:
Fortifications are useless without observers. Rotate watches every 2–4 hours — fatigue kills awareness. Minimum one observer always.

Passwords and recognition:
Keyword or gesture for scouts returning — reduces friendly fire mistakes.

Map the perimeter:
Sketch where alarms, pits, and key positions are. Everyone in the group must know the layout.

Maintenance:
Check alarms daily. After rain, inspect pits — they may fill with water or collapse.
