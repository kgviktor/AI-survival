CHAPTER 3. FOOD
3.10 Nutrition norms and fasting

PHYSIOLOGY OF FASTING

What happens without food:

0–24 h: glycogen used (glucose stores in liver and muscle). Hunger, reduced focus, mild dizziness. Functions still normal.

24–72 h: body shifts to fat (ketosis). Hunger paradoxically eases. Mental clarity often increases. Sharp early weight loss is mostly water.

3–7 days: adaptation to fasting. Body spares protein, slows metabolism. Weakness grows. Physical work becomes hard.

1–3 weeks: heavy fat use. Muscle mass begins to drop. Immune system weakens.

3+ weeks: exhaustion. Very low body fat — danger zone. An average adult (~70 kg, moderate fat) may survive ~40–60 days without food if water intake is adequate.

Death from starvation: often not “hunger” alone — infections with weak immunity, heart failure from wasted cardiac muscle, or multi-organ failure.


CALORIE NEEDS

Basal metabolic rate (BMR) — at rest:
— Man ~70 kg: ~1700–1800 kcal/day
— Woman ~60 kg: ~1400–1500 kcal/day
— Child ~10 years: ~1200–1400 kcal/day

Multiply by activity:
— Rest (illness, sheltering in cold): ×1.1 ≈ 1900–2000 kcal
— Light (walking, shelter work): ×1.4 ≈ 2400–2500 kcal
— Moderate (carrying load, labor): ×1.7 ≈ 2900–3100 kcal
— Heavy (running, hard work in cold): ×2.0 ≈ 3500–4000 kcal

Cold increases burn:
At about -10°C, expenditure ~10–15% higher than at +20°C. Shivering costs a lot — insulation matters for survival, not only comfort.


MANAGING FOOD IN SHORTAGE

How low can you go:
— 1200–1500 kcal/day: minimal survival at rest. Long-term — depletion.
— 800–1000 kcal/day: acceptable ~1–2 weeks at very low activity.
— Below ~500 kcal/day: serious risk after 1–2 weeks.

Stretching stores:
1. Reduce physical activity — largest controllable cost.
2. Improve cold insulation — saves thermoregulation calories.
3. Smaller, more frequent meals — same daily total but less acute hunger spikes.
4. Prioritize fats: 9 kcal/g vs 4 kcal/g for protein and carbs.


MACRONUTRIENT PRIORITIES

Carbohydrates (4 kcal/g):
— Primary fuel for brain and muscles
— Grains, bread, roots, sugar
— If absent, body uses fat (and eventually protein)

Fats (9 kcal/g):
— Most calorie-dense. 1 tbsp oil ≈ 120 kcal.
— Needed to absorb vitamins A, D, E, K
— Animal fat, plant oil, nuts, seeds
— In survival: add fat where you can

Proteins (4 kcal/g):
— Tissue building blocks
— If carbs and fat are low, body catabolizes muscle protein
— Meat, fish, legumes, insects
— Minimum guideline: ~0.8 g/kg body weight (~56 g/day for 70 kg)

“Protein poisoning” / rabbit starvation:
Very lean meat only (high protein, almost no fat) causes nausea, diarrhea, exhaustion. You need fat. Traditional wisdom: eat brains and organ fat with lean game.


VITAMINS AND MINERALS

Scurvy (vitamin C lack):
Onset ~4–6 weeks without C. Bleeding gums, tooth loss, weakness, poor wound healing. Sources: rose hips, pine/spruce needle tea, fermented cabbage, fresh wild greens.

Beriberi (thiamine B1 lack):
Classically from polished rice only diet. Nerve and heart problems. Sources: legumes, nuts, whole grains.

Anemia (iron, B12):
Weakness, pallor, shortness of breath. Iron: meat, liver, dark leafy greens. B12: animal foods only.

Vitamin A deficiency:
Night blindness. Sources: liver, carrots, dark green and orange vegetables.

Practical minimum:
— Some fresh greens daily when possible
— Needle tea in winter when no greens
— Variety is rule #1


FASTING — PRACTICAL COPING

Occupation:
Idleness worsens hunger feelings. Staying busy helps.

Water:
Adequate water blunts hunger. Warm water or weak herb/bark “tea” fills the stomach.

Fiber:
Leaves, bark cambium — low calories but bulk. Caution: too much fiber causes diarrhea.

Consumption order:
Days 1–3: eat most perishable first.
Days 3–7: shift to main stores.
Day 7+: rationing mode; plan resupply.

Psychology of hunger:
— Hunger comes in waves every ~2–4 h — the peak passes.
— First 2–3 days often hardest — then often easier.
— Fixed meal times reduce mental stress.
— Small portions eaten slowly feel more filling.


SPECIAL GROUPS

Children:
— Deplete faster on deficit.
— Higher protein need per kg.
— Serious undernutrition signs: apathy, edema, hair loss.
— Prioritize children in rationing.

Pregnant and nursing:
— Add ~300–500 kcal/day to baseline.
— Protein need much higher.
— Maternal deficit directly risks infant.

Elderly:
— Less reserve to compensate.
— Dehydration develops faster.
— Protein need does not drop with age — often underestimated.


QUICK STOCK CALCULATIONS

If you know what you have:

Grain 1 kg ≈ ~3400 kcal ≈ ~2 days at 1700 kcal for one person.
Vegetable oil 1 L ≈ ~8100 kcal ≈ ~5 days at minimum for one person.
Canned meat ~325 g ≈ ~450 kcal ≈ ~¼ daily minimum.
Sugar 1 kg ≈ ~3900 kcal.
Hard crackers 1 kg ≈ ~3400 kcal.

Simple formula:
Total kcal in stores ÷ 1700 (rough minimum) ≈ person-days for one adult.
Divide by number of people — group coverage.

Example: 5 kg buckwheat (~17,000 kcal) + 2 L oil (~16,200 kcal) + 10 cans meat (~4500 kcal) = 37,700 kcal ÷ 1700 ≈ 22 person-days for one, or ~11 days for two.
