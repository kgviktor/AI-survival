CHAPTER 7. NAVIGATION AND MOVEMENT
7.3 Movement in damaged urban / rubble environments

RISK ASSESSMENT BEFORE EACH MOVE

Rubble and damaged buildings shift. What looks stable can fail from vibration or hidden decay.

“Look before you step”:
— Assess footing every step.
— Do not trust unsupported slabs.
— Slow beats rushed.

Unstable structure signs:
— Diagonal cracks in load-bearing walls/columns.
— Sagging floors (walls spread, slab dropped).
— Fresh dust/debris piles underneath (recent collapse still settling).
— Creaking, popping, groaning.
— Leaning load-bearing walls/columns.
— Voids visible under decking.

Relatively safer:
— Monolithic RC frames with intact columns.
— Bearing walls without major cracks.
— Solid flat floors without visible sag.


COMMON HAZARDS

Piles of debris:
— Brick/concrete/rebar heaps can slide under weight.
— Test with foot before committing full weight.
— Prefer largest stable blocks.
— Avoid deep loose gravel on slopes — slips.

Floor holes:
— Surface may look intact over void.
— Probe ahead with pole; listen for hollow sound.
— Walk perimeter near walls when unsure.
— Avoid centers of sagging spans.

Exposed rebar:
— Impalement risk — never dive into rubble.
— Bend or cut protruding bars only if safe and you have tools.

Open shafts:
— Elevator shafts without doors.
— Holes between floors.
— Basement openings — light every gap.

Glass:
— Floor shards cut boots and skin.
— Hanging window glass can fall when jarred.
— Hug wall under questionable windows when passing.

Fire risk:
— Smoldering zones can flash when air rushes in (your movement).
— Heat smell or warm surfaces — hidden combustion.
— Avoid passing through active smolder if you can.

Gas:
— Rotten-egg odor (mercaptan) — leave immediately; no sparks or open flame.

Electricity:
— Live conductors on wet floors — lethal.
— Metal framing may be energized — detour if unsure.

Dust and asbestos:
— Older building debris may contain asbestos — respirator or multi-layer damp cloth; minimize dust.


MOVEMENT TECHNIQUE

Horizontal rubble:
— Feet on large stable chunks; hands free for balance.
— Slight knee bend, low center of mass.
— If falling — forward onto hands, not backward onto rebar.

Damaged stairs:
— Use wall for support, not loose rails.
— Test each tread with weight at wall edge first.

Climbing rubble:
— Three points of contact always.
— Do not pull on loose blocks.
— Face-in descent.

Through wall breaches:
— Assess lintel/load before passing.
— Do not hang weight on broken edges.
— Move through quickly; do not loiter in the gap.

Basements:
— Always with light.
— Leave entry open for air and exit.
— Listen for structure sounds.
— Toxic gas may accumulate (CO from smolder, methane, H₂S in some sewers).
— Open flame test at entrance is unreliable and risky — prefer proper gas detector; low oxygen can extinguish flame but so can drafts.

Vertical routing:
— Fewer floor crossings = less cumulative risk.
— Ground floor when possible.
— Avoid top floors of heavily damaged buildings — collapse and escape issues.


GEAR FOR RUBBLE

Minimum:
— Sturdy boots, thick sole.
— Gloves.
— Headlamp + spare batteries.
— Long sturdy pole for probing/balance.
— Dust mask or damp cloth layers.

Useful:
— Hard hat.
— Knee pads.
— Knife for cord/wire.
— ~10–15 m rope for assist rappels or hauls.


GROUP MOVEMENT

— Lead moves slowly, tests route.
— Others step exactly in lead’s path.
— ~3–5 m spacing — one fall does not yard others in.
— Voice signals: “clear,” “stop,” “go around.”
— If someone stuck: assess before yanking — do not shake whole pile.


NAVIGATION IN RUINS

Problems:
— Signs and corners gone; streets unreadable.

Methods:
— High point for panorama when structurally safe.
— Seek large fixed objects: water towers, factory stacks, tall survivors.
— Drainage and streams.
— Sun and compass as always.
